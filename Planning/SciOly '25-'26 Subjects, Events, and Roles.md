Build Subjects(2)

Construction Build  
These are events that have Wooden builds, which you optimize for Power & Strength 

1. Helicopter  
   1. Prior to the tournament, teams design, construct, and test free flight rubber-powered helicopters to achieve maximum time aloft.  
   - Skills you will gain: airfoil and propeller tuning, torque/energy management with rubber motors, trim logging, iterative test design, lightweight structure building
   - Core topics: lift/drag, pitch and CG stability, propeller pitch/diameter tradeoffs, rubber winds vs torque curves, mass optimization, trimming in varying air conditions
   - Build tips: use alignment jigs for rotor shafts and blades, balance props, minimize adhesive and excess material, weigh everything to the tenth of a gram
   - Practice plan: maintain a flight log (winds/torque/turns/time/observations), run controlled experiments on rubber sizing and prop pitch, calibrate for room size and air currents
   - Recommended tools: accurate digital scale, torque meter, winder with counter, calipers, simple alignment jigs
2. Boomilever  
   1. Teams will design and build a Boomilever meeting requirements specified in these rules to achieve the highest structural efficiency(load/build weight)
   - Skills you will gain: structural analysis, member sizing, joint design, adhesive selection/technique, experimental logging, failure analysis
   - Core topics: tension vs compression, buckling and Euler load, truss geometry, load paths, density/stiffness tradeoffs, gusseting and bracing patterns
   - Build tips: straight, defect-free wood; consistent cross-sections; precise jigs for angles; reinforce joints intelligently; sand to final dimensions; weigh and record each component
   - Practice plan: test-to-failure cycles with post-mortem notes, design-of-experiments on cross-sections and geometries, iterate to maximize efficiency under rule constraints
   - Recommended tools: calipers, razor saw, sanding blocks, mass scale (0.01 g), assembly jig, slow and fast-setting adhesives

Precision Build  
These events build your knowledge of applied mathematics and electronics. The goal is to have your build meet certain distance/time requirements. This requires a knowledge of physics, statistics, and electronics.

1. Bungee Drop  
   1. Each team will design one elastic cord to conduct two separate drops at a given height(s) and attempt to get a drop mass, placed in a bottle, as close as possible to, but without touching, a landing surface.  
   2. very mathematical, involves calculating spring constants for different parts of a bungee cord under different stresses  
   - Skills you will gain: physical modeling, data regression, uncertainty analysis, iterative calibration, careful measurement under time constraints
   - Core topics: Hooke's law and non-linear elasticity, piecewise spring constants, energy conservation with damping, safety factors and overshoot control
   - Build tips: document cord segment lengths and knots; understand how knots and attachments change effective k; consider series/parallel segment designs
   - Practice plan: build height-vs-extension calibration tables, run multiple trials at practice heights, create a quick-reference chart for competition day
   - Recommended tools: meter stick/tape, high-speed or phone video for extension measurement, spreadsheet for regression
2. Hovercraft  
   1. Participants will be tested on their knowledge of classical mechanics and related topics as well as their ability to construct a self-propelled air-levitated vehicle that moves down a track.  
   - Skills you will gain: classical mechanics application, low-friction vehicle design, airflow management, measurement and timing, documentation of build parameters
   - Core topics: Newton's laws, power and energy, drag forces, lift generation via air cushion, skirt design and leakage, friction coefficient measurement
   - Build tips: prototype multiple skirt materials/geometries, ensure consistent lift vs thrust balance, control mass distribution for stability, standardize battery state
   - Practice plan: timed runs with consistent start procedures, track alignment checks, sensitivity testing to payload and surface changes
   - Recommended tools: accurate timer, scale, tachometer (if applicable), ruler/track, spare skirts and tapes
3. Electric Vehicle   
   1. Teams design, build, and test one vehicle that uses electrical energy as its sole means of propulsion to travel as quickly as possible and stop close to a Target Point.  
   - Skills you will gain: DC electronics, motor control, gearing and wheel sizing, closed-loop control, precision measurement and repeatability
   - Core topics: Ohm's/Kirchhoff's laws, motor curves and gearing, battery discharge behavior, kinematics, braking/stop mechanisms (mechanical vs electronic)
   - Build tips: ensure true wheel diameter and alignment, reduce slippage, consider encoders or calibrated timing for stop control, isolate variables during testing
   - Practice plan: generate distance vs time calibration curves under different battery states, test across surfaces, maintain a run log with conditions
   - Recommended tools: multimeter, soldering tools, tachometer/encoder, calipers, tape measure, test track
4. Robot Tour   
   1. Teams design, build, program and test one Robotic Vehicle to navigate a track to reach a target at a set amount of time as accurately and efficiently as possible.
   - Skills you will gain: embedded programming, sensor integration, control systems, path planning, robust build practices for repeatability
   - Core topics: dead reckoning vs sensor-based navigation, PID/feedback control, odometry, sensor fusion (e.g., IR/ultrasonic/IMU), timing and latency
   - Build tips: rigid drivetrain, consistent wheel traction, clean wiring, debounced sensors, configurable software parameters for quick tuning
   - Practice plan: break the course into segments with checkpoints, vary target times to test timing model, record tuning parameters per surface
   - Recommended tools: microcontroller platform (e.g., Arduino), encoder wheels, IMU, stopwatch, calibration fixtures

Academic Subjects(5)

Physics and Design  
These are all theory-based test+lab events. They require knowledge of Mechanical and Electrical Engineering.

1. Engineering CAD   
   1. Teams will read a set of engineering drawings and collaboratively create CAD parts and assemblies that match the drawings while incorporating provided components and be able to answer questions about the drawing and generated model.  
   - Skills you will gain: parametric modeling, constraints and mates, design intent, drawing interpretation, collaboration in CAD
   - Core topics: dimensioning standards, tolerances and GD&T basics, exploded views and BOMs, assemblies vs subassemblies, section views
   - Practice plan: speed modeling drills from drawings, recreate common features (holes, fillets, patterns), assemble and mate with proper constraints
   - Deliverables to master: fully constrained sketches, accurate parts, clean assemblies, readable drawings with correct annotations
2. Machines  
   1. Teams will complete a written test on compound machine concepts and construct a lever-based measuring device prior to the tournament to determine the ratio between the two masses.  
   - Skills you will gain: mechanical advantage analysis, device calibration, error analysis, test-taking under time
   - Core topics: levers, pulleys, gears, wheel-and-axle, IMA vs AMA, efficiency and losses, torque and moments
   - Build tips: precise pivot placement, low-friction bearings, rigid beams, repeatable measurement procedure with clear scale markings
   - Practice plan: lab-style experiments verifying MA and efficiency, quick calculations practice, calibration curves for your device
3. Circuit Lab  
   1. The written test consists of questions spanning a list of topics and can include a variety of question types. The hands-on component can test a variety of circuit elements in order to complete a task (such as constructing a magnet or determining the value of a resistor).
   - Skills you will gain: circuit analysis, instrument use (multimeter, power supply, breadboard), troubleshooting, lab documentation
   - Core topics: Ohm's and Kirchhoff's laws, series/parallel networks, Thevenin/Norton, RC/RL time constants, diodes and basic transistors, op-amp fundamentals
   - Lab focus: safe measurement techniques, reading color codes, building stable circuits, interpreting oscilloscope traces if provided
   - Practice plan: timed problem sets and quick-build labs, formula sheet organization, error-checking strategies

Earth Science  
These events focus on environmental systems. You get to have 2 reference sheets during tests.

1. Water Quality  
   1. Participants will be assessed on their understanding and evaluation of marine and estuary aquatic environments. Includes Build: Salinometer(5% of grade)  
   - Skills you will gain: field and lab data interpretation, instrument construction (salinometer), identification of aquatic organisms, environmental analysis
   - Core topics: water chemistry (salinity, dissolved oxygen, pH, nutrients), marine ecology and indicator species, sampling methods, pollution and remediation
   - Build tips: calibrate the salinometer with standard solutions, record calibration curves, control temperature effects
   - Practice plan: analyze past data sets/graphs, practice organism ID from images, prepare concise two-page references
2. Dynamic Planet  
   - Skills you will gain: geospatial reasoning, process-based Earth systems thinking, data reading from maps and graphs
   - Core topics: topic rotates annually; expect mapping/remote data use, physical processes, and quantitative problem solving tied to that year's theme
   - Practice plan: compile reference tables, work past tests, and annotate maps/figures for rapid lookup
3. Remote Sensing  
   1. Participants will use remote sensing imagery, data and computational process skills to complete tasks related to climate change processes in the Earth system.
   - Skills you will gain: interpreting satellite imagery, reading band combinations, index calculations, climate data analysis
   - Core topics: electromagnetic spectrum and sensors, common indices (e.g., NDVI), image classification basics, climate drivers and feedbacks
   - Practice plan: practice interpreting false-color composites, compute indices from sample data, build a reference of band meanings by sensor

Classification and Compilation  
These events test your ability to efficiently and effectively Research, compile and identify large quantities of information. They are unlimited binder events.

1. Rocks and Minerals  
   1. Identify rocks and minerals  
   - Skills you will gain: rapid identification, property testing, binder organization, pattern recognition
   - Core topics: mineral properties (hardness, luster, streak, cleavage), rock types and formation, economic uses, environments of formation
   - Practice plan: timed ID drills, property-first decision trees, maintain annotated specimen photos and property tables
   - Helpful tools: streak plate, hand lens, magnet, hardness picks (when allowed)
2. Entomology  
   1. In this event, competitors will be assessed on their knowledge of North American insect families(mostly identification)  
   - Skills you will gain: taxonomic key usage, anatomical vocabulary, high-speed visual ID, reference management
   - Core topics: orders and families, life cycles and metamorphosis, ecology and behavior, diagnostic traits
   - Practice plan: flashcarding diagnostic characters, labeled anatomy drills, curated binder tabs with side-by-side comparisons
3. Astronomy  
   1. Teams will demonstrate an understanding of Stellar Evolution: Star Formation & Exoplanets.
   - Skills you will gain: quantitative astronomy problem solving, spectral interpretation, image analysis
   - Core topics: stellar evolution pathways, HR diagram, blackbody radiation, exoplanet detection (transit/RV), simple orbital mechanics
   - Practice plan: past tests with formula sheet optimization, unit consistency and quick calculations (e.g., distance modulus, luminosity)

Biology  
These events test your knowledge of human biology, from DNA to populations of people.

1. Anatomy and Physiology  
   1. Participants will be assessed on their understanding of the anatomy and physiology for the human Nervous, Sensory, and Endocrine systems  
   - Skills you will gain: systems-level understanding, diagram labeling speed, mechanism tracing from organ to molecule
   - Core topics: neuroanatomy and signaling, sensory transduction and pathways, endocrine glands/hormones and feedback regulation
   - Practice plan: draw-and-label routines, pathway flowcharts, clinical vignette practice
2. Disease Detectives  
   1. Participants will use their investigative skills in the scientific study of disease, injury, health, and disability in populations or groups of people.  
   - Skills you will gain: epidemiological math, study design critique, outbreak investigation, data communication
   - Core topics: incidence/prevalence, attack rates, RR/OR calculations, bias and confounding, case definitions, line lists, surveillance
   - Practice plan: 2x2 table drills, interpreting epi curves and maps, concise write-ups of findings
3. Designer Genes  
   1. Participants will solve problems and analyze data or diagrams using their knowledge of the basic principles of genetics, molecular genetics and biotechnology.
   - Skills you will gain: genetic problem solving, pedigree and probability, molecular technique familiarity
   - Core topics: Mendelian and non-Mendelian inheritance, gene regulation, molecular biology central dogma, biotech methods (PCR, restriction mapping, CRISPR basics)
   - Practice plan: timed Punnett/pedigree sets, sequence interpretation, quick-reference tables for codons and enzymes

Experimentation and Analysis  
These events require critical analysis skills and (for the first 2\) a knowledge of AP-level chemistry

1. Chem Lab  
   1. Kinetics and Stoichiometry  
   - Skills you will gain: solution prep, titration technique, rate measurement, calculation speed with units
   - Core topics: rate laws and mechanisms, integrated rate equations, stoichiometric limiting/excess, error analysis
   - Practice plan: quick lab setups, calibration curves, past quantitative problems with dimensional analysis
2. Materials Science  
   - Skills you will gain: structure–property reasoning, data/graph interpretation, experimental observation
   - Core topics: polymers, metals, ceramics, composites; bonding and microstructure; phase diagrams (as applicable)
   - Practice plan: summarize property trends, analyze lab phenomena, build a concise reference of definitions and diagrams
3. Forensics  
   - Skills you will gain: qualitative analysis, systematic testing, meticulous documentation, chain-of-custody mindset
   - Core topics: powders and polymers, fibers and hair, chromatography, fingerprints, glass/soil, metal analysis (as applicable)
   - Practice plan: standardized testing flowcharts, timed unknown runs, photo documentation and clean lab notes
4. Experimental Design  
   - Skills you will gain: controlled experimentation, variable isolation, data table/graph design, statistical reasoning
   - Core framework: hypothesis and variables, materials and procedures, data collection, analysis with trends/errors, conclusion and improvements
   - Practice plan: design-build-execute reports under time limits, rubric cross-checking
5. Codebusters
   - Skills you will gain: pattern recognition, frequency analysis, teamwork under time
   - Core topics: monoalphabetic and polyalphabetic ciphers (e.g., Aristocrat/Patristocrat, Vigenère), transposition, Baconian; math snippets and cribbing strategies
   - Practice plan: timed cipher sets with role specialization, build a compact reference of common patterns and crib words

Academic SD Roles  
There will be 8 academic SDs across the 5 subjects:

1. Engineering: 1 SD  
2. Earth Science 2 SDs  
3. Research: 1 SD  
4. Biology: 2 SDs  
5. Math, Chem, Experimentation: 2 SDs

SDs will mainly be the ones teaching all of the events in their subject. This requires a mastery of the event content, as well as excellent communication and leadership skills.

The build directors have already been chosen and will act as SDs across the build subjects.

Event Manager Roles

There will be exactly 1 event manager for each event. This holds for both academic and build events. The role of event managers is mainly organizational and communicative. They are responsible for getting to know their members on a personal level, creating meeting activities, competitions, and facilitating learning and practice.
   - SD responsibilities: set curriculum and goals, run weekly lessons/practices, build shared resource libraries, design assessments, mentor event managers, coordinate with leadership on progress
   - Event Manager responsibilities: logistics and communication, meeting agendas and practice activities, check-ins with partners, organizing scrimmages/past-test practice, tracking attendance and needs
   - Expectations: maintain organized references (binders/sheets), consistent practice logs, proactive communication; typical weekly time 2–4 hours in-season depending on role

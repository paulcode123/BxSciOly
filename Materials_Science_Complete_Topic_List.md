# MATERIALS SCIENCE DIVISION C - COMPREHENSIVE TOPIC LIST

## I. FOUNDATIONAL CHEMISTRY AND STRUCTURE

### A. Carbon Chemistry Fundamentals
1. **Covalent Bonding**
   - Neutral carbon atom bonding capacity (4 covalent bonds)
   - Bond formation mechanisms
   - Stable organic structures
   
2. **Orbital Hybridization**
   - Electron promotion (2s → 2p)
   - Maximizing half-filled orbitals
   - Bonding capacity enhancement
   
3. **sp³ Hybridization (Tetrahedral)**
   - Four equivalent hybrid orbitals
   - 109.5° bond angles
   - Electron pair repulsion minimization
   - Diamond structure and properties
   - Poor electrical conductivity (no delocalized electrons)
   - Extreme durability and hardness
   - High thermal conductivity
   
4. **sp² Hybridization (Trigonal Planar)**
   - Three equivalent hybrid orbitals in plane
   - 120° bond angles
   - One unhybridized p orbital perpendicular to plane
   - π-bonding capability (side-by-side overlap)
   - Delocalized π-bond systems
   - Graphene and graphite structures
   - Electrical conductivity mechanism
   - Resonance in conjugated systems
   
5. **sp Hybridization (Linear)**
   - Two equivalent hybrid orbitals
   - 180° bond angles
   - Two unhybridized p orbitals
   - Two perpendicular π-systems
   - Carbyne chains
   - Acetylenic carbon structures

### B. Molecular Orbital Theory
1. **Basic MO Concepts**
   - HOMO (Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital)
   - LUMO (Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital)
   - Bonding and antibonding orbitals
   
2. **MO Theory vs. Hybridization**
   - Diamond: Band gap explanation (insulator behavior)
   - Graphene: Delocalized π-system → semimetallic conductivity
   - CNTs: Chirality-dependent electronic properties
   - Carbyne: Unusual electronic behavior from dual π-systems
   - Benzene: π-orbital system
   
3. **Electronic Band Structure**
   - Valence band
   - Conduction band
   - Band gap energy
   - Fermi level
   - Density of states (DOS)

- **Simple Cubic**

## III. DIFFUSION

### A. Fick's Laws
1. **Fick's First Law**
   - J = -D(dC/dx)
   - Steady-state diffusion
   - Concentration gradient
   - Diffusive conductance
   - Application to nanofibers
   
2. **Fick's Second Law**
   - ∂C/∂t = D(∂²C/∂x²)
   - Non-steady-state diffusion
   - Time-dependent concentration profiles

### B. Diffusion Mechanisms
1. Vacancy diffusion
2. Interstitial diffusion
3. Grain boundary diffusion
4. Surface diffusion
5. Temperature dependence: D = D₀ exp(-Q/RT)

### C. Diffusion Applications

- Doping semiconductors
- Poisonous gas diffusion in composite materials
- Carbon-based nanomaterial composites for diffusion control

## IV. TYPES AND APPLICATIONS OF NANOMATERIALS

### A. Dimensionality Classification

#### 1. Zero-Dimensional (0D) Nanomaterials
- **Quantum Dots**
  - Size: 2-10 nm typically
  - Quantum confinement in all three dimensions
  - Discrete energy levels
  - Size-dependent optical properties
  - Common materials: CdSe, PbS, InP, ZnS
  - Fluorescence and photoluminescence
  - Blue light emission: 2-3 nm dots
  - Stokes shift (emission wavelength > absorption wavelength)
  - Quantum yield
  - Nonradiative recombination
  - Applications: displays, solar cells, bioimaging
  
- **Fullerenes**
  - Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀)
    - 60 carbon atoms
    - Named after R. Buckminster Fuller
    - 12 pentagons and 20 hexagons
    - Soccer ball structure
  - Other fullerenes: C₇₀, C₈₄, etc.
  - Cage-like structures
  - sp² hybridization
  - Applications: drug delivery, lubricants, superconductors
  
- **Nanoparticles (General)**
  - Metal nanoparticles (Au, Ag, Cu)
  - Metal oxide nanoparticles (TiO₂, ZnO, Fe₂O₃)
  - Semiconductor nanoparticles
  - Ceramic nanoparticles

#### 2. One-Dimensional (1D) Nanomaterials
- **Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs)**
  - Single-walled nanotubes (SWNT)
  - Multi-walled nanotubes (MWNT)
  - **CNT Structures:**
    - Armchair configuration
    - Zig-zag configuration
    - Chiral configuration
  - Chirality vector (n,m)
  - Diameter-dependent properties
  - **Electronic Properties:**
    - Metallic vs. semiconducting based on chirality
    - Molecular orbital theory application
    - Density of states with Van Hove singularities
  - Quantum confinement (1D - confined in 2 directions)
  - **Mechanical Properties:**
    - Extremely high tensile strength
    - Young's modulus ~1 TPa
    - sp² σ-bond strength
  - Applications: composites, electronics, sensors
  
- **Carbyne Chains**
  - sp hybridization
  - Linear geometry
  - Two perpendicular π-systems
  - Alternating single-triple bonds (polyyne) vs. cumulene (equal double bonds)
  - Bond-length alternation stability

  - Ultra-high stiffness
  
- **Nanowires**
  - Metallic nanowires
  - Semiconductor nanowires
  - Oxide nanowires

- **Nanorods**
  - Aspect ratio considerations
  - Metal and semiconductor variants
  
- **Nanofibers**
  - Carbon nanofibers

  - Oxide nanofibers
  - Cylindrical geometry modeling

  - Hollow nanofiber SA/V: complex geometry

#### 3. Two-Dimensional (2D) Nanomaterials

    - Tunable optical absorption
  - **Applications:**
    - High-speed electronics
    - Field Effect Transistors (FET)
    - Transparent conductors
    - Sensors
    - Energy storage (supercapacitors, batteries)
    - Composites
  - 2010 Nobel Prize in Physics (Andre Geim and Konstantin Novoselov)
  
- **Graphene Oxide (GO)**
  - Functionalized graphene
  - Oxygen-containing groups
  - **Functional Groups:**
    - Epoxide (most common)
    - Hydroxyl (alcohol)
    - Carboxyl
  - Hydrophilic properties

- **Transition Metal Dichalcogenides**

## V. SIZE EFFECTS OF NANOMATERIALS

### A. Quantum Confinement Effects

#### 1. General Principles
- Confinement in 1, 2, or 3 dimensions
- Discrete energy levels
- Size-dependent band gap
- Wave function quantization

#### 2. Quantum Wells (2D confinement)
- Thin films and layers
- Allowed energy levels: En = (n²π²ℏ²)/(2meL²)
- Subband formation
- Energy spacing between subbands
- Thickness dependence
- Comparison to bulk Fermi energy
- Thermal energy considerations (kT)
- Reverse transition to 3D behavior

#### 3. Quantum Wires (1D confinement)
- Nanowires and nanotubes
- Van Hove singularities
- Sharp peaks in density of states
- Influence on optical absorption spectra

#### 4. Quantum Dots (0D confinement)
- Size-dependent emission color
- Smaller dots → higher energy → blue shift
- Larger dots → lower energy → red shift
- Quantum yield considerations

#### 5. Effects on Electrical Properties
- Band gap widening with decreasing size
- Quantum tunneling
- Modified carrier mobility
- Ballistic transport in very small structures
- Discrete subbands vs. continuous bands

#### 6. Effects on Optical Properties
- Size-tunable photoluminescence
- Quantum dot color variation
- Exciton confinement
- Enhanced absorption
- Plasmon resonance shifts

### B. Surface Area to Volume Ratio

#### 1. Geometric Relationships
- Sphere: SA/V = 3/r
- Cube: SA/V = 6/side length
- Cylinder (infinite): SA/V = 2/r
- Cylinder (finite): SA/V = [2πr(L + r)]/[πr²L]
- Hollow cylinder: complex geometry
- Scaling laws: SA/V increases as size decreases

#### 2. Consequences for Properties
- **Chemical Reactivity:**
  - More surface atoms available
  - Enhanced catalytic activity
  - Increased adsorption sites
  - Gas absorption and retention
- **Thermal Properties:**
  - Modified melting temperature
  - Enhanced heat transfer
- **Mechanical Properties:**
  - Surface stress effects
- **Optical Properties:**
  - Enhanced scattering
  - Plasmonic effects
- **Energy Storage:**
  - Increased capacity (supercapacitors, batteries)
  - Faster charging rates
  - Better charge distribution

#### 3. Applications Enabled by High SA/V
- Catalysis
- Energy storage devices
- Gas sensors
- UV reflection (small nanoparticles)
- Thermal management
- Drug delivery

### C. Melting Temperature Depression
- Size-dependent melting point
- Surface energy contributions
- Smaller particles melt at lower temperatures

## VI. CHEMISTRY OF SURFACES

### A. Surface Energy and Surface Tension

#### 1. Surface Energy
- Energy required to create new surface area
- Units: J/m²
- Related to bond breaking at surface
- Higher for materials with stronger bonds

#### 2. Surface Tension
- Force per unit length at liquid surface
- Units: N/m
- Minimization drives shape formation
- Temperature dependence
- Low surface tension liquids (e.g., Mortivex analog in tests)

### B. Wetting and Contact Angles

#### 1. Wetting Angle (Contact Angle)
- Angle formed between liquid droplet and solid surface
- Measured through the liquid phase

#### 2. Hydrophobicity
- High wetting angle (>90°)
- Water repellent
- Low surface energy materials
- Applications: self-cleaning, waterproofing

#### 3. Hydrophilicity
- Low wetting angle (<90°)
- Water attracting
- High surface energy materials
- Enhanced spreading
- Applications: absorption, coatings

#### 4. Wetting Applications
- Absorption: low contact angle desired
- Water repulsion: high contact angle desired
- Poison absorption: low angle for poison
- Water repulsion combined: high angle for water

### C. Surfactants

#### 1. Structure
- Amphiphilic molecules
- Hydrophobic tail (nonpolar)
- Hydrophilic head (polar)

#### 2. Function
- Reduce surface tension of liquids
- Facilitate emulsion formation
- Stabilize colloidal suspensions
- Modify wetting angles

#### 3. Applications
- Detergents and cleaners
- Emulsifiers
- Coating enhancement
- Nanoparticle stabilization
- Tuning wetting behavior

### D. Surface Chemistry in Nanomaterials

#### 1. Surface Functionalization
- Chemical modification of surfaces
- Attachment of functional groups
- Ligand exchange
- **Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) Coating:**
  - Improved biocompatibility
  - Reduced protein adsorption
  - Enhanced circulation time (in vivo)
  - Steric stabilization

#### 2. Surface Charge
- Zeta potential
- Colloidal stability predictor
- Influenced by:
  - Particle size
  - Surface charge density
  - Ionic strength of medium
  - pH

#### 3. Colloidal Stability
- **DLVO Theory** (Derjaguin-Landau-Verwey-Overbeek)
  - Van der Waals attraction forces
  - Electrostatic repulsion forces
  - Total interaction energy
  - Barrier to aggregation
  
- **Electric Double Layer (EDL)**
  - Stern layer (tightly bound ions)
  - Diffuse layer (loosely associated ions)
  - Debye length
  - Effect of electrolyte concentration
  
- **Flocculation and Aggregation**
  - Electrolyte concentration effects
  - Debye length decrease with electrolyte increase
  - Barrier reduction
  - Stability maintenance strategies

## VII. NANOMATERIALS SYNTHESIS AND FABRICATION

### A. Top-Down Synthesis Techniques

#### 1. Mechanical Methods
- **Ball Milling**
  - High-energy ball milling
  - Cost-effective nanoparticle production
  - Reducing bulk materials to nanoparticles
  - Blade edge conversion to nanoparticles
  - Scalable process
  
- **Milling and Grinding**
  - Jet milling
  - Attrition milling

#### 2. Sputtering
- Physical vapor deposition (PVD)
- Plasma-based material removal
- Thin film deposition
- Target material ejection
- Controlled film growth

#### 3. Chemical Etching

  - Isotropic etching

  - **Benefits:**
    - Scalable
    - Cost-effective
    - Batch processing capability
    - Simple equipment
    - Multi-wafer processing
    - High selectivity
    - Fast etch rates
  - **Disadvantages:**
    - Isotropic (undercutting)
    - Less precise

  - Anisotropic capability

    - Higher precision

#### 4. Lithography

  - Photomask
  - Photoresist (positive/negative)
  - UV exposure

  - Pattern transfer
  - Resolution limitations (~200 nm)

  - UV energy breaking chemical bonds

  - Distinguished from photoelectric effect

  - Sub-10 nm resolution
  - Direct writing
  - Slow process

- **Focused Ion Beam (FIB)**
  - Nanoscale machining
  - Material removal and deposition
  - Imaging capability

#### 5. Extrusion
- Mechanical forcing through die
- Nanofiber production

### B. Bottom-Up Synthesis Techniques

#### 1. Sol-Gel Process
- Solution-based synthesis
- **Steps:**
  - Hydrolysis
  - Condensation (polymerization)
  - Gelation
  - Drying (evaporation)
  - Sintering (optional)
- **Hydrolysis and Condensation Role:**
  - Govern polymerization kinetics
  - Affect final homogeneity
  - Control porosity and microstructure
  - Network formation
- Metal oxide nanoparticle synthesis
- Ceramic material production
- Thin film coatings

#### 2. Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)
- Gas-phase precursors
- **Precursor Compounds:**
  - Metal hydrides
  - Metal halides
  - Metal carbonyls
  - Metal alkyls (organometallics)
- Substrate heating
- Decomposition and deposition
- **Catalytic CVD:**
  - Template-directed synthesis
  - Self-assembly mechanism
  - Carbon nanotube growth
  - Graphene synthesis
- **High Pressure High Temperature CVD (for diamond-like materials):**
  - Bulk material synthesis
  - Less pure products
  - Larger crystals
  - Not suitable for layered materials
- **Low Pressure CVD:**
  - Layered material production
  - Higher purity
  - Better control
  - Thinner films

#### 3. Self-Assembly
- **Molecular Self-Assembly**
  - Spontaneous organization
  - Thermodynamic equilibrium
  - Weak interactions (H-bonds, van der Waals)
  - Supramolecular structures
  
- **Catalytic Self-Assembly**
  - Vapor-Liquid-Solid (VLS) mechanism
  - Catalyst-mediated growth
  - Nanofiber and nanowire synthesis
  - **Advantages:**
    - Greater scalability than atom-by-atom
    - Faster production
  - **Trade-offs:**
    - Distribution in chirality
    - Variable defect densities
    - Less uniformity than manual assembly
    - Grain boundary formation

#### 4. Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE)
- Ultra-high vacuum environment
- Atomic layer-by-layer growth
- Slow deposition rate: 0.1-1 µm/h (or 0.01-1 nm/s)
- High precision
- High purity
- Expensive
- Semiconductor device fabrication

#### 5. Electrospinning
- Polymer solution or melt
- High voltage application
- Nanofiber production
- Oxide nanofibers
- Large-scale production

#### 6. Hydrothermal/Solvothermal Synthesis
- Solution-based
- Elevated temperature and pressure
- Autoclave or sealed vessel
- Crystal growth
- Metal oxide nanoparticles

#### 7. Spin Coating
- Solution deposition on rotating substrate
- **Factors Determining Film Thickness:**
  - Spin speed (RPM)
  - Solution viscosity
  - Solvent evaporation rate
- Thin film formation
- Uniform coating
- Applications: photoresist, polymers, sol-gel films

### C. Synthesis Method Comparison
- **Top-Down:**
  - Start with bulk material
  - Higher defect density possible
  - Generally less precise control
  - Faster for some methods
  - Lower purity potential
  
- **Bottom-Up:**
  - Build from atoms/molecules
  - Better control of structure
  - Higher purity potential
  - Can be slower
  - **Advantages for specific applications:**
    - Less waste production (cleaner)
    - Energy efficiency
    - Precision building
    - Atomic-level control

## VIII. CHARACTERIZATION TECHNIQUES

### A. X-Ray Diffraction (XRD)

#### 1. Bragg's Law
- nλ = 2d sin(θ)
- n = order of diffraction (usually 1)
- λ = wavelength (Cu Kα = 1.54 Å typical)
- d = interplanar spacing
- θ = diffraction angle (Bragg angle)

#### 2. d-Spacing Calculations
- For cubic systems: d = a/√(h² + k² + l²)
- For tetragonal systems: different relationships
- For hexagonal systems: more complex
- Miller indices assignment from peaks

#### 3. Peak Analysis
- **Peak Position:**
  - Determines d-spacing
  - Identifies crystal structure
  - Lattice parameter calculation
  
- **Peak Width (Broadening):**
  - Broad peaks → small crystal size
  - Narrow peaks → large crystal size
  - FWHM (Full Width at Half Maximum)
  - Scherrer equation
  
- **Peak Intensity:**
  - Atomic scattering factors
  - Crystal orientation
  - Texture effects

#### 4. Debye-Scherrer Equation
- D = Kλ/(β cos θ)
- D = crystallite size
- K = shape factor (typically 0.90)
- λ = X-ray wavelength
- β = FWHM in radians
- θ = Bragg angle
- Calculates average crystallite/grain size

#### 5. Crystal Structure Determination
- Lattice type identification
- Systematic absence analysis
- Lattice constant calculation
- Phase identification
- Comparison to reference databases

### B. Microscopy Techniques

#### 1. Optical Microscopy
- Visible light illumination
- Resolution limit: ~200 nm (diffraction limited)
- Resolution range: 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁴ m
- Limited for nanomaterials

#### 2. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
- Electron beam scanning
- Secondary electron detection
- Resolution: 1-10 nm
- Resolution range: 10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁷ m (approximately)
- Surface morphology imaging
- Topographical information
- Sample must be conductive (coating may be required)
- **Applications:**
  - Surface structure analysis
  - Particle size determination
  - Morphology characterization

#### 3. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
- Electron beam transmission
- Very high resolution: 0.1-0.2 nm
- Resolution range: 10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁷ m
- Internal structure imaging
- Lattice imaging
- Selected area electron diffraction (SAED)
- Thin samples required
- **Applications:**
  - Crystal structure determination
  - Defect analysis
  - Particle size and shape
  - Compositional analysis (with EDS)

#### 4. Scanning Probe Microscopy (SPM)
- **Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)**
  - Physical tip-sample interaction
  - Surface topography mapping
  - Sub-nanometer resolution
  - Can work in ambient conditions
  - Step height measurements
  - Layer counting (e.g., graphene layers)
  - Roughness analysis
  
- **Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM)**
  - Quantum tunneling current

## IX. PHYSICAL AND MECHANICAL PROPERTIES

### A. Mechanical Properties Fundamentals

#### 1. Stress and Strain
- **Stress (σ):**
  - Force per unit area
  - σ = F/A
  - Units: Pa (N/m²)
  - Types: tensile, compressive, shear
  
- **Strain (ε):**
  - Deformation per unit length
  - ε = ΔL/L₀
  - Dimensionless (or percent)
  - Engineering strain vs. true strain

#### 2. Elastic Modulus
- **Young's Modulus (E):**
  - Tensile modulus
  - Elastic modulus
  - E = σ/ε (in elastic region)
  - Slope of stress-strain curve (linear region)
  - Material stiffness measure
  - Units: Pa (GPa or TPa for nanomaterials)
  - **Examples:**
    - Graphene: ~1 TPa
    - Diamond: ~1 TPa
    - Carbon nanotubes: ~1 TPa
    - Carbyne: even higher
  
- **Bulk Modulus:**
  - Resistance to compression
  - Volume change under pressure
  
- **Shear Modulus (G):**
  - Resistance to shear deformation
  - Torsional rigidity
  
- **Flexural Modulus:**
  - Resistance to bending
  - Stiffness in bending

#### 3. Hardness
- Resistance to indentation
- **Testing Methods:**
  - Brinell hardness
  - Vickers hardness
  - Rockwell hardness
  - Knoop hardness
  - Nanoindentation
- **Hardness Proxy:**
  - H = 1/D (D = indent diameter)
  - Useful for relative comparisons
  - Qualitative assessment
  - Heat treatment effects

#### 4. Strength
- Maximum stress before failure
- Tensile strength
- Compressive strength
- Yield strength (σy)
- Ultimate tensile strength (UTS)

#### 5. Toughness
- Energy absorption before fracture
- Area under stress-strain curve
- Resistance to crack propagation

#### 6. Adhesion
- Bonding between surfaces
- Interface strength
- Coating adhesion

#### 7. Wear
- Surface damage from friction
- Material loss mechanisms
- Tribological properties

### B. Hall-Petch Relationship

#### 1. Hall-Petch Equation
- σy = σ₀ + ky/√d
- σy = yield strength
- σ₀ = lattice friction stress
- ky = Hall-Petch constant (strengthening coefficient)
- d = average grain size

#### 2. Physical Interpretation
- Grain boundaries impede dislocation motion
- Smaller grains → more grain boundaries → higher strength
- Grain size strengthening mechanism

#### 3. Grain Size Effects
- Smaller grain size → higher yield strength
- Heat treatments affect grain size:
  - **Recrystallization (350°C):**
    - New strain-free grains form
    - Smaller grain size
    - Higher yield strength
  - **Grain Growth (500°C, long time):**
    - Existing grains grow
    - Larger grain size
    - Lower yield strength

#### 4. Reverse Hall-Petch Effect
- At very small grain sizes (< ~10-20 nm)
- Strength decreases with decreasing grain size
- Grain boundary sliding dominates
- Different deformation mechanisms

#### 5. Applicability Limits
- Hall-Petch applies to conventional grain sizes (> ~20 nm)
- Does not apply to nanoscale grains
- Transition region exists

### C. Nanomechanics

#### 1. Size-Dependent Mechanical Properties
- Surface effects dominate at nanoscale
- Surface stress contributions
- Enhanced strength in small structures

#### 2. Mechanical Testing of Nanomaterials
- **Nanoindentation:**
  - Hardness and elastic modulus
  - Load-displacement curves
  
- **Tensile Testing:**
  - Nanowire/nanofiber testing
  - In-situ TEM testing
  - AFM-based testing
  - Young's modulus calculation: E = (F/A)/(ΔL/L)
  - **Example:** TiO₂ nanowire testing

#### 3. Buckling of Nanofibers
- **Euler Buckling Theory:**
  - Critical buckling load: Pcrit = π²EI/L²
  - E = Young's modulus
  - I = second moment of area
  - L = length
  - **For hollow cylinder:** I = π(R⁴ - r⁴)/4
  - **For solid cylinder:** I = πR⁴/4
- Pinned/clamped end conditions
- Maximum length to avoid buckling
- Comparison: hollow vs. solid

### D. Statistical Strength Analysis (Weibull Statistics)

#### 1. Weibull Distribution
- Describes variability in material strength
- Particularly important for brittle materials
- Size effects on strength

#### 2. Weibull Survival Probability
- Psurvival = exp[-(σ/σ₀)^m × (L/L₀)]
- σ = applied stress
- σ₀ = characteristic strength (scale parameter)
- m = Weibull modulus (shape parameter)
- L = specimen length
- L₀ = reference length

#### 3. Weibull Modulus (m)
- Shape parameter
- **Higher m:**
  - Less scatter in strength values
  - Narrower distribution
  - More reliable material
  - Weaker size effect
  - Decreased outliers in tail
- **Lower m:**
  - Greater scatter
  - Broader distribution
  - Less predictable strength
- Does NOT depend only on cross-sectional area

#### 4. Characteristic Strength (σ₀)
- Scale parameter
- Stress at which ~63.2% fail
- Length-dependent
- Material and processing dependent

#### 5. Size Effect
- Larger specimens have lower strength (statistically)
- "Weakest link" theory
- More volume → higher probability of critical flaw

#### 6. Linearization for Parameter Extraction
- Plot: ln[-ln(Psurvival)] vs. ln(σ)
- Slope = Weibull modulus (m)
- Intercept relates to σ₀
- Linear regression analysis

#### 7. System Reliability
- **Parallel Fibers:**
  - Psystem = 1 - (1 - Psingle)^n
  - n = number of fibers
  - Redundancy improves reliability
- **Series Configuration:**
  - Psystem = (Psingle)^n
  - Chain is only as strong as weakest link

#### 8. Applications to Nanofibers
- Carbon nanofiber strength variability
- Manual assembly vs. catalytic self-assembly
- Length-dependent strength
- Design for reliability

### E. Density
- Mass per unit volume
- ρ = m/V
- **Theoretical density (crystals):** ρ = (Z × M)/(a³ × NA)
- Size effects on density (surface contributions)

### B. Electrical Properties

#### 1. Electrical Conductivity (σ)

#### 2. Resistivity (ρ)

- Material property
- Temperature dependence

#### 3. Electronic Band Structure
- Valence band
- Conduction band
- **Band Gap:**
  - Energy difference between valence and conduction bands
  - Determines electrical behavior
  - **Insulators:** Large band gap (> 3 eV)
  - **Semiconductors:** Moderate band gap (0.1-3.0 eV typical)
  - **Metals/Conductors:** No band gap or overlapping bands
  - **Semimetals:** Zero or very small band gap (graphene)

#### 4. Semiconductor Behavior
- **Intrinsic Semiconductors:**
  - Pure material (Si, Ge)
  - Electron-hole pair generation
  - Temperature-dependent conductivity
  
- **Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doping):**
  - **n-Type:**
    - Pentavalent dopants (P, As, Sb)

    - Majority carriers: electrons
  
  - **p-Type:**
    - Trivalent dopants (B, Al, Ga)
    - Electron deficiency (holes)
    - Majority carriers: holes
    - Example: Boron in silicon
  
- **Band Gap Values:**
  - Semiconductors: typically 0.1-3.0 eV
  - Examples:
    - Si: 1.1 eV
    - GaAs: 1.4 eV

### C. Electron Transport Models

#### 1. Classical Drude Model
- **Assumptions:**
  - Free electrons in uniform positive background
  - Instantaneous, random collisions
  - Relaxation time (τ) characterizes collisions
  - All conduction electrons participate equally
  - Classical Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics (NOT quantum)
  
- **Equation of Motion:**
  - F = ma = -eE - mv/τ
  - -eE: force from electric field
  - -mv/τ: friction/collision term
  
- **Drift Velocity:**
  - vd = -eEτ/m
  - Proportional to electric field
  - Very small compared to thermal velocity
  
- **Conductivity:**
  - σ = ne²τ/m
  
- **Limitations:**
  - Thermal velocity vs. drift velocity discrepancy
  - Heat capacity prediction incorrect

#### 2. Drude-Sommerfeld Model (Quantum Extension)
- **Improvements:**
  - Fermi-Dirac statistics (quantum)
  - Only electrons near Fermi surface conduct
  - Fermi energy (EF) concept
  - Better heat capacity prediction
  
- **Fermi Energy:**
  - EF = (ℏ²/2me)(3π²n)^(2/3)
  - Highest occupied energy at T = 0 K
  - Example: Copper EF ≈ 7 eV
  
- **Fermi Velocity:**
  - vF = √(2EF/me)
  - Thermal velocity of electrons at Fermi level
  - Much larger than drift velocity (vF >> vd)
  - Example: Copper vF ≈ 10⁶ m/s

#### 3. Scattering Mechanisms
- **Phonon Scattering:**
  - Lattice vibrations
  - Dominant at high temperature
  - Temperature-dependent
  
- **Impurity Scattering:**
  - Defects and dopants
  - Dominant at low (cryogenic) temperatures
  - Temperature-independent
  
- **Grain Boundary Scattering:**
  - Important in polycrystalline materials
  - Increases resistivity
  
- **Surface Scattering:**
  - Critical in thin films and nanowires
  - Mean free path comparable to size
  
- **Matthiessen's Rule:**
  - 1/τtotal = 1/τphonon + 1/τimpurity + 1/τsurface + ...

#### 4. Nanoscale Transport Effects
- **Ballistic Transport:**
  - Mean free path > material dimensions
  - No scattering
  - Quantum conductance
  - Landauer formula
  
- **Quantum Confinement:**
  - Wave function quantization in confined dimensions
  - Discrete subbands instead of continuous bands
  - Size-dependent conductivity
  
- **Surface Dominance:**
  - Surface/interface scattering exceeds bulk scattering
  - Increased resistivity in nanostructures

### D. Magnetic Properties

#### 1. Types of Magnetism
- **Diamagnetism:**
  - Weak repulsion
  - All materials (but often masked)
  
- **Paramagnetism:**
  - Weak attraction
  - Unpaired electrons
  - No spontaneous magnetization
  
- **Ferromagnetism:**
  - Strong attraction
  - Spontaneous magnetization
  - Magnetic domains
  - Examples: Fe, Co, Ni
  
- **Antiferromagnetism:**
  - Adjacent spins antiparallel
  - No net magnetization
  
- **Ferrimagnetism:**
  - Unequal antiparallel spins
  - Net magnetization
  - Example: magnetite (Fe₃O₄)

#### 2. Magnetic Fields
- External magnetic field (H)
- Magnetization (M)
- Magnetic flux density (B)
- Permeability

#### 3. Saturation Magnetization
- Maximum magnetization achievable
- All domains aligned
- **Size Effects:**
  - Ultrasmall nanoparticles: reduced saturation
  - Surface spin disorder
  - Broken surface symmetry
  - Uncompensated surface spins

#### 4. Curie Temperature (Tc)
- **Ferromagnetic to Paramagnetic Transition:**
  - Above Tc: paramagnetic (random spins)
  - Below Tc: ferromagnetic (ordered spins)
  - Thermal energy overcomes magnetic coupling
- **Quantum Confinement Effects (State/National):**
  - Modified density of states in nanoparticles
  - Reduced Curie temperature
  - Size-dependent magnetic properties

#### 5. Magnetic Anisotropy
- Direction-dependent magnetic properties
- Anisotropy axis
- **Synthesis Effects:**
  - Applying magnetic field during synthesis
  - Preferred orientation/alignment
  - Anisotropy axis distribution control

#### 6. Superparamagnetism
- Single-domain nanoparticles
- Thermal fluctuations
- Magnetic moment fluctuates (not fixed)
- No hysteresis at room temperature
- Blocking temperature

#### 7. Magnetic Nanoparticles
- Iron oxide (magnetite, maghemite)
- Cobalt ferrite
- **Applications:**
  - MRI contrast agents
  - Magnetic hyperthermia
  - Drug delivery
  - Data storage

## I. INTRODUCTION AND DIMENSIONALITY

Nanomaterials are classified by the number of dimensions that are **not** in the nanoscale (< 100 nm).

| Type | Dimensionality | Examples |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **0D** | Confined in all 3 dimensions | Fullerenes (C₆₀), Carbon Dots, Nanodiamonds |
| **1D** | Confined in 2 dimensions (one long axis) | Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs), Carbyne chains, Carbon Nanofibers |
| **2D** | Confined in 1 dimension (thin sheet) | Graphene, Graphene Oxide (GO) |
| **3D** | Not confined in any dimension | Graphite, Diamond, Nanocrystalline Bulk Carbon |

## II. CARBON CHEMISTRY FUNDAMENTALS

### 2.1 Covalent Bonding
Carbon has **4 valence electrons** ($2s^2 2p^2$), allowing it to form 4 covalent bonds. The geometry of these bonds depends on **orbital hybridization**.

### 2.2 Orbital Hybridization
| Hybridization | Geometry | Bond Angle | Structure | Examples |
|---------------|----------|------------|-----------|----------|
| **sp³** | Tetrahedral | 109.5° | 3D Lattice | Diamond, Nanodiamonds |
| **sp²** | Trigonal Planar | 120° | Planar sheets/tubes | Graphene, CNTs, Fullerenes, Graphite |
| **sp** | Linear | 180° | 1D Chain | Carbyne |

### 2.3 Electronic Implications
- **sp³ (Diamond):** All electrons are localized in $\sigma$-bonds. No free electrons → **Insulator** (Large band gap).
- **sp² (Graphene/CNTs):** Three electrons form $\sigma$-bonds; one electron resides in an unhybridized **p-orbital**. These p-orbitals overlap to form a **delocalized $\pi$-system** → **Excellent electrical conductivity**.

## III. 0D NANOMATERIALS: FULLERENES

### 3.1 Buckminsterfullerene ($C_{60}$)
- **Structure:** 60 carbon atoms in a "soccer ball" shape.
- **Geometry:** 12 pentagons and 20 hexagons. (Euler's formula for polyhedra applies).
- **Hybridization:** sp² (with some curvature-induced strain).
- **Properties:** Cage-like structure, can trap atoms inside (endohedral fullerenes).
- **Applications:** Drug delivery, lubricants, organic photovoltaics.

## IV. 1D NANOMATERIALS: CNTs AND CARBYNE

### 4.1 Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs)
CNTs are rolled-up sheets of graphene.
- **SWNT:** Single-walled nanotube (one layer).
- **MWNT:** Multi-walled nanotube (multiple nested layers).
- **Chirality $(n, m)$:** The angle at which the graphene is rolled determines its properties.
    - **Armchair $(n, n)$:** Always metallic (conductive).
    - **Zig-zag $(n, 0)$:** Can be metallic or semiconducting.
    - **Chiral $(n, m)$:** Usually semiconducting.
- **Properties:** 
    - **Mechanical:** Tensile strength ~100x steel; Young's modulus ~1 TPa.
    - **Thermal:** Very high thermal conductivity along the axis.

### 4.2 Carbyne
- **Structure:** A single chain of carbon atoms.
- **Bonding:** Either alternating single-triple bonds (polyyne) or continuous double bonds (cumulene).
- **Properties:** Predicted to be the **strongest material ever** (stiffest and highest tensile strength), surpassing even graphene.

## V. 2D NANOMATERIALS: GRAPHENE

### 5.1 Graphene Structure
- A single atomic layer of sp²-hybridized carbon in a **hexagonal (honeycomb) lattice**.
- Isolated in 2004 by Geim and Novoselov (Nobel Prize 2010) using the "Scotch Tape method."

### 5.2 Electronic Properties
- **Semimetal:** Zero band gap.
- **Dirac Cones:** Electrons behave as "massless Dirac fermions," moving at relativistic speeds ($1/300$th the speed of light).
- **Mobility:** Extremely high electron mobility ($> 200,000 \, \text{cm}^2/\text{V}\cdot\text{s}$).

### 5.3 Graphene Oxide (GO)
- Functionalized graphene with oxygen groups (epoxide, hydroxyl, carboxyl).
- **Properties:** Hydrophilic (water-dispersible), electrical insulator (until reduced to rGO).

## VI. SYNTHESIS AND CHARACTERIZATION

### 6.1 Synthesis Methods
- **Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD):** The standard for high-quality graphene and CNTs. Uses a carbon gas (methane) and a metal catalyst (nickel, copper).
- **Bottom-Up:** Building from molecules (e.g., CVD, Sol-gel).
- **Top-Down:** Breaking bulk materials (e.g., Exfoliation of graphite, Ball milling).

### 6.2 Characterization Techniques
| Technique | Application for Carbon Nanomaterials |
|-----------|-------------------------------------|
| **Raman Spectroscopy** | **Gold Standard.** Identifies D, G, and 2D peaks. Used for layer counting and defect analysis. |
| **XRD (X-Ray Diffraction)** | Measures d-spacing between graphene layers (Graphite: $d \approx 0.335 \, \text{nm}$). |
| **SEM (Scanning Electron)** | Views 3D morphology of CNT forests or nanofiber mats. |
| **TEM (Transmission Electron)** | **Atomic Resolution.** Visualizes individual graphene sheets or the number of walls in MWNTs. |
| **AFM (Atomic Force)** | Measures the **height/thickness** of graphene flakes to confirm single-layer status. |

### 6.3 Raman Spectroscopy Details
The most important tool for identifying carbon nanomaterials.
- **D-peak (~1350 cm⁻¹):** Indicates **defects** (sp³ hybridization in an sp² lattice).
- **G-peak (~1580 cm⁻¹):** Indicates the **sp² graphitic lattice**.
- **2D-peak (~2700 cm⁻¹):** Used to determine the **number of layers** (very sharp/high for single-layer graphene).

## VII. PHYSICAL AND MECHANICAL PROPERTIES

### 7.1 Young's Modulus ($E$)
- Measure of stiffness. $E = \sigma / \epsilon$.
- **Graphene/CNTs:** ~1.0 TPa ($10^{12}$ Pa).

### 7.2 Thermal Conductivity
- Graphene holds the record: $> 3,000 \, \text{W/m}\cdot\text{K}$.
- Comparison: Copper is only $\sim 400 \, \text{W/m}\cdot\text{K}$.

## IX. PRACTICE PROBLEMS

1. **Calculate the SA/V ratio of a Carbon Nanofiber (solid cylinder) with radius $r = 5 \, \text{nm}$ and infinite length.**
   *Answer: $SA/V = 2/r = 2 / (5 \times 10^{-9}) = 4 \times 10^8 \, \text{m}^{-1}$.*

2. **Why is diamond an insulator while graphene is a conductor?**
   *Answer: Diamond is sp³ hybridized with all electrons locked in $\sigma$-bonds. Graphene is sp² hybridized with delocalized $\pi$-electrons free to move.*

3. **In Raman spectroscopy, what does a high D-peak relative to the G-peak indicate?**
   *Answer: High defect density or significant disorder in the carbon lattice.*

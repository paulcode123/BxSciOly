=== SECTION I: CHEMICAL REACTIONS AND EQUATIONS ===

I.A - Reaction Types and Classification
  • Synthesis (Combination):
    - A + B → AB
    - Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O (when balanced)
  • Decomposition:
    - AB → A + B
    - Example: 2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂
    - Example: NH₄NO₃ → N₂O + 2H₂O
  • Single Displacement (Single Replacement):
    - A + BC → AC + B
    - Example: Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag
    - Redox reaction
  • Double Displacement (Metathesis):
    - AB + CD → AD + CB
    - Example: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl + NaNO₃
    - Includes precipitation, acid-base, gas-forming reactions
  • Combustion:
    - Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (for hydrocarbons)
    - Example: C₁₀H₂₂ + O₂ → H₂O + CO₂ (when balanced)
    - Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
  • Redox (Oxidation-Reduction):
    - Transfer of electrons
    - Includes single displacement, combustion
    - Example: Cu + 2AgNO₃ → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag
  • Acid-Base (Neutralization):
    - Acid + Base → Salt + Water
    - Example: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O
  • Comproportionation:
    - Two species with different oxidation states form one with intermediate state
    - Example: H₂S + SO₂ → S + H₂O
  • Disproportionation:
    - One species undergoes both oxidation and reduction
    - Example: Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl

I.B - Balancing Chemical Equations
  • Basic Balancing:
    - Use lowest whole-number coefficients
    - Balance atoms one element at a time
    - Start with most complex compounds
    - Examples:
      - C₃H₈ + 5O₂ → 3CO₂ + 4H₂O
      - Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3Ca(OH)₂ → 2Al(OH)₃ + 3CaSO₄
      - 2KClO₃ → 2KCl + 3O₂
      - Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂
  • Redox Balancing - Acidic Conditions:
    - Identify oxidation and reduction half-reactions
    - Balance atoms (except H and O)
    - Balance O with H₂O
    - Balance H with H⁺
    - Balance charge with e⁻
    - Multiply to equalize electrons
    - Combine and simplify
    - Example: MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O
  • Redox Balancing - Basic Conditions:
    - Balance as if acidic first
    - Add OH⁻ to both sides to neutralize H⁺
    - Combine H⁺ + OH⁻ → H₂O
    - Cancel excess H₂O
    - Example: MnO₂ + ClO⁻ + 2OH⁻ → MnO₄⁻ + Cl⁻ + H₂O

I.C - Net Ionic Equations
  • Complete Ionic Equation:
    - Shows all ions separately
    - Example: Ag⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) + Na⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)
  • Net Ionic Equation:
    - Removes spectator ions
    - Shows only reacting species
    - Example: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
  • Spectator Ions:
    - Ions that don't participate in reaction
    - Same on both sides of equation
    - Example: Na⁺ and NO₃⁻ in AgNO₃ + NaCl reaction
  • Precipitation Reactions:
    - Formation of insoluble solid
    - Use solubility rules to predict products
    - Example: Ba²⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → BaSO₄(s)
  • Solubility Rules:
    - Most nitrates, acetates, chlorides soluble (except AgCl, PbCl₂, Hg₂Cl₂)
    - Most sulfates soluble (except BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄)
    - Most carbonates, phosphates insoluble (except Group 1, NH₄⁺)
    - Most hydroxides insoluble (except Group 1, Ba²⁺, Sr²⁺)

I.D - Formula Writing
  • Common Ions:
    - Cations: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺, Cu²⁺, Ag⁺, NH₄⁺
    - Anions: Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻, F⁻, OH⁻, NO₃⁻, SO₄²⁻, CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻, HCO₃⁻, CH₃COO⁻ (acetate)
  • Polyatomic Ions:
    - Bicarbonate: HCO₃⁻
    - Phosphate: PO₄³⁻
    - Acetate: CH₃COO⁻ or C₂H₃O₂⁻
    - Sulfate: SO₄²⁻
    - Nitrate: NO₃⁻
    - Carbonate: CO₃²⁻
  • Naming:
    - Perovskite: CaTiO₃ (Calcium titanate)
    - Charge of Ti in perovskite: +4

=== SECTION II: STOICHIOMETRY ===

II.A - Mole Concept and Conversions
  • Avogadro's Number:
    - 6.022 × 10²³ particles per mole
  • Molar Mass:
    - Mass of one mole of substance (g/mol)
    - Sum of atomic masses
    - Example: H₂O = 18.02 g/mol
  • Mole Calculations:
    - Moles = mass / molar mass
    - Mass = moles × molar mass
    - Number of particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³
  • Atom Counting:
    - Example: CH₂Cl₂ (molar mass = 84.93 g/mol)
    - 400 g CH₂Cl₂ = 400/84.93 = 4.71 mol
    - Cl atoms = 4.71 mol × 2 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 5.67 × 10²⁴ atoms
  • Comparing Number of Molecules:
    - Convert all to moles, then compare
    - Example: 16 g H₂O = 0.889 mol; 21 g C₃H₆ = 0.5 mol; 84 g CH₄ = 5.25 mol; 47 g N₂O = 1.07 mol

II.B - Stoichiometric Calculations
  • Mole Ratios:
    - From balanced equation coefficients
    - Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
      - 2 mol H₂ : 1 mol O₂ : 2 mol H₂O
  • Limiting Reactant:
    - Reactant that is completely consumed
    - Determines maximum product yield
    - Calculate moles of each reactant
    - Use mole ratios to find which limits
  • Excess Reactant:
    - Reactant present in excess
    - Some remains after reaction
  • Percent Yield:
    - % Yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%
    - Example: If 4.0 g H₂O obtained from reaction that should produce 7.4 g:
      - % Yield = (4.0/7.4) × 100% = 54%
  • Multi-step Reactions:
    - Account for yields at each step
    - Example: R → 2B (86.3% yield), 3B → 5G + C (53.0% yield)
    - Calculate moles through each step with yields

II.C - Solution Stoichiometry
  • Molarity (M):
    - M = moles of solute / liters of solution
    - Units: mol/L or M
  • Dilution:
    - M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
    - Moles remain constant
  • Mixing Solutions:
    - Calculate total moles of each ion
    - Divide by total volume
    - Example: 0.25 L of 0.15 M NaCl + 0.5 L of 0.55 M MgCl₂
      - Total Cl⁻ = (0.25 × 0.15) + (0.5 × 0.55 × 2) = 0.0375 + 0.55 = 0.5875 mol
      - Total volume = 0.75 L
      - [Cl⁻] = 0.5875/0.75 = 0.78 M
  • Precipitation Calculations:
    - Identify limiting reactant
    - Use stoichiometry to find precipitate mass
    - Example: 15.0 mL of 0.56 M Na₃PO₄ + 50.0 mL of 0.32 M Ba(NO₃)₂
      - Find limiting reactant, calculate Ba₃(PO₄)₂ mass

II.D - Gas Stoichiometry
  • STP Conditions:
    - Standard Temperature and Pressure
    - T = 273 K (0°C), P = 1 atm
    - 1 mol gas = 22.4 L at STP
  • Ideal Gas Law:
    - PV = nRT
    - R = 0.0821 L·atm/(mol·K)
    - Can solve for n, V, P, or T
  • Gas Volume Calculations:
    - From moles: V = n × 22.4 L/mol (at STP)
    - From ideal gas law: V = nRT/P
    - Example: Decompose 14.5 mL H₂O (liquid, density = 1.00 g/mL)
      - Moles H₂O = 14.5/18.02 = 0.805 mol
      - Moles O₂ = 0.805/2 = 0.402 mol
      - Volume O₂ = 0.402 × 22.4 = 9.02 L at STP
  • Non-STP Conditions:
    - Use ideal gas law with given P and T
    - Example: 100.0 mL of 90% H₂O₂ (density = 1.29 g/mL) at 741.5 Torr and 23.1°C
      - Calculate moles O₂, then use PV = nRT

II.E - Empirical and Molecular Formulas
  • Empirical Formula:
    - Simplest whole-number ratio of atoms
    - From percent composition or mass data
    - Steps:
      1. Convert % to grams (assume 100 g)
      2. Convert to moles
      3. Divide by smallest number of moles
      4. Multiply to get whole numbers
    - Example: 25.07% Fe, 32.83% Cl, 43.10% O
      - Fe: 25.07/55.85 = 0.449 mol
      - Cl: 32.83/35.45 = 0.926 mol
      - O: 43.10/16.00 = 2.694 mol
      - Ratio: Fe:Cl:O = 1:2:6 → FeCl₂O₆ (FeCl₂(ClO₄)₄, but typically written as FeCl₂O₆)
  • Molecular Formula:
    - Actual number of atoms in molecule
    - Multiple of empirical formula
    - Need molar mass to determine
    - Example: Empirical CH₂O, molar mass = 180 g/mol
      - Empirical mass = 30 g/mol
      - Multiplier = 180/30 = 6
      - Molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆
  • Freezing Point Depression:
    - ΔT_f = K_f × m × i
    - K_f = freezing point depression constant
    - m = molality (mol/kg solvent)
    - i = van't Hoff factor (number of particles)
    - Can determine molecular mass from freezing point data

=== SECTION III: KINETICS ===

III.A - Rate Laws and Reaction Orders
  • Rate Law:
    - rate = k[A]^m[B]^n
    - k = rate constant
    - m, n = orders with respect to A and B
    - Overall order = m + n
  • Determining Rate Law from Data:
    - Compare experiments where one concentration changes
    - Example: Rate doubles when [A] doubles → first order in A
    - Rate quadruples when [B] doubles → second order in B
  • Zero Order:
    - rate = k
    - Rate independent of concentration
    - Units: M/s or mol/(L·s)
  • First Order:
    - rate = k[A]
    - Rate proportional to concentration
    - Units: s⁻¹
    - Half-life: t₁/₂ = ln(2)/k (constant, independent of [A])
  • Second Order:
    - rate = k[A]² or rate = k[A][B]
    - Units: M⁻¹s⁻¹ or L/(mol·s)
    - Half-life: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀) (depends on initial concentration)
  • Higher Order:
    - Example: rate = k[A]⁴[B]²[C]
    - Overall order = 7
    - Units: L⁶/(mol⁶·s)

III.B - Integrated Rate Laws
  • Zero Order:
    - [A] = [A]₀ - kt
    - Linear plot: [A] vs. t
  • First Order:
    - ln[A] = ln[A]₀ - kt
    - Linear plot: ln[A] vs. t
    - Half-life: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k
  • Second Order:
    - 1/[A] = 1/[A]₀ + kt
    - Linear plot: 1/[A] vs. t
    - Half-life: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀)
  • Determining Order from Data:
    - Plot [A] vs. t: linear → zero order
    - Plot ln[A] vs. t: linear → first order
    - Plot 1/[A] vs. t: linear → second order

III.C - Reaction Mechanisms
  • Elementary Steps:
    - Individual steps in mechanism
    - Molecularity: number of molecules involved
      - Unimolecular: A → products
      - Bimolecular: A + B → products
      - Termolecular: A + B + C → products (rare)
  • Rate-Determining Step (RDS):
    - Slowest step in mechanism
    - Determines overall rate
    - Step with largest activation energy
  • Intermediates:
    - Species formed and consumed in mechanism
    - Not in overall reaction
    - Example: X₂Y in mechanism X + Y → X₂Y (fast), X₂Y + Z → X₂Z + Y (slow)
  • Catalysts:
    - Appear in mechanism but not consumed
    - Lower activation energy
    - Example: Fe³⁺ in H₂O₂ decomposition
  • Steady-State Approximation:
    - For intermediates: d[I]/dt = 0
    - Rate of formation = rate of consumption
    - Used to derive rate laws from mechanisms
  • Pre-equilibrium:
    - Fast equilibrium before slow step
    - Example: Cl₂ ⇌ 2Cl (fast), Cl + CH₄ → products (slow)
    - Rate law involves equilibrium constant

III.D - Temperature and Catalysis
  • Arrhenius Equation:
    - k = A·e^(-E_a/RT)
    - E_a = activation energy
    - A = pre-exponential factor
    - R = gas constant (8.314 J/(mol·K))
    - T = temperature (K)
  • Temperature Effects:
    - Higher temperature → more molecules with sufficient energy
    - More collisions with E > E_a
    - Rate increases exponentially with temperature
    - Does NOT change activation energy
  • Activation Energy:
    - Minimum energy needed for reaction
    - Determines temperature dependence
    - Higher E_a → stronger temperature dependence
  • Catalysts:
    - Homogeneous: Same phase as reactants
      - Example: Fe³⁺(aq) in H₂O₂ decomposition
    - Heterogeneous: Different phase
      - Example: Platinum surface for NO₂ decomposition
    - Effects:
      - Lower activation energy
      - Provide alternate pathway
      - Not consumed
      - Do NOT change equilibrium constant
      - Speed up both forward and reverse reactions equally

III.E - Complex Mechanisms
  • Chain Reactions:
    - Initiation: Creates radicals
    - Propagation: Radicals react, create new radicals
    - Termination: Radicals combine
    - Example: Cl₂ + CH₄ → CH₃Cl + HCl
      - Initiation: Cl₂ → 2Cl·
      - Propagation: Cl· + CH₄ → CH₃· + HCl, CH₃· + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl·
      - Termination: Cl· + Cl· → Cl₂
  • Enzyme Kinetics (Michaelis-Menten):
    - v = (V_max[S])/(K_M + [S])
    - V_max = maximum velocity
    - K_M = Michaelis constant
    - K_M = (k₋₁ + k_cat)/k₁
    - When [S] << K_M: v ≈ (V_max/K_M)[S] (first order)
    - When [S] >> K_M: v ≈ V_max (zero order)
  • Pseudo-First Order:
    - One reactant in large excess
    - Its concentration effectively constant
    - Example: [B] >> [A], rate = k'[A] where k' = k[B]
  • Lindemann Mechanism:
    - For unimolecular reactions in gas phase
    - A + M → A* + M (activation)
    - A* → products (decomposition)
    - A* + M → A + M (deactivation)
    - At high pressure: first order
    - At low pressure: second order

=== SECTION IV: EQUILIBRIUM ===

IV.A - Equilibrium Concepts
  • Dynamic Equilibrium:
    - Forward and reverse rates equal
    - Concentrations constant (not equal)
    - Macroscopically static, microscopically dynamic
  • Equilibrium Constant (K):
    - K = [products]^coefficients / [reactants]^coefficients
    - For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: K = [C]^c[D]^d / [A]^a[B]^b
    - Pure solids and liquids not included
    - Depends only on temperature
  • K_c vs. K_p:
    - K_c: Concentrations (M)
    - K_p: Partial pressures (atm)
    - K_p = K_c(RT)^(Δn)
    - Δn = moles gas products - moles gas reactants
  • Magnitude of K:
    - K >> 1: Products favored
    - K << 1: Reactants favored
    - K ≈ 1: Significant amounts of both
  • Reaction Quotient (Q):
    - Same form as K but with current concentrations
    - Q < K: Reaction proceeds forward
    - Q > K: Reaction proceeds reverse
    - Q = K: At equilibrium

IV.B - Le Châtelier's Principle
  • Statement:
    - System at equilibrium responds to stress to minimize change
  • Concentration Changes:
    - Add reactant: Shifts right (toward products)
    - Remove product: Shifts right
    - Add product: Shifts left (toward reactants)
    - Remove reactant: Shifts left
  • Pressure Changes (gases):
    - Increase pressure: Shifts toward fewer moles of gas
    - Decrease pressure: Shifts toward more moles of gas
    - No change if Δn = 0
  • Temperature Changes:
    - Endothermic: Heat is reactant
      - Increase T: Shifts right
      - Decrease T: Shifts left
    - Exothermic: Heat is product
      - Increase T: Shifts left
      - Decrease T: Shifts right
  • Catalysts:
    - Do NOT shift equilibrium
    - Speed up both directions equally
    - Only affect rate, not position
  • Inert Gases:
    - Adding at constant volume: No effect
    - Adding at constant pressure: Shifts toward more moles

IV.C - Equilibrium Calculations
  • ICE Tables:
    - Initial, Change, Equilibrium concentrations
    - Use stoichiometry for changes
    - Solve for x using K expression
  • Example: PCl₅ ⇌ PCl₃ + Cl₂
    - Initial: [PCl₅] = 0.0250 M, [PCl₃] = 0, [Cl₂] = 0
    - Change: -x, +x, +x
    - Equilibrium: 0.0250-x, x, x
    - Use total pressure to find x
    - Calculate K_p = (P_PCl₃)(P_Cl₂)/(P_PCl₅)
  • Approximations:
    - If K << 1 and initial [reactant] >> K: x is small
    - Can approximate [reactant] - x ≈ [reactant]
    - Check validity after solving
  • Multiple Equilibria:
    - Combine equilibrium constants
    - If A ⇌ B (K₁) and B ⇌ C (K₂), then A ⇌ C (K = K₁K₂)
    - If reverse: K_reverse = 1/K_forward

IV.D - Solubility Equilibria
  • Solubility Product Constant (K_sp):
    - For sparingly soluble salts
    - Example: AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq), K_sp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻]
    - Example: Fe(OH)₃(s) ⇌ Fe³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq), K_sp = [Fe³⁺][OH⁻]³
  • Molar Solubility:
    - Moles of salt that dissolve per liter
    - Related to K_sp through stoichiometry
    - Example: For AgCl, if K_sp = 1.8 × 10⁻¹⁰, solubility = √K_sp = 1.3 × 10⁻⁵ M
  • Common Ion Effect:
    - Adding common ion decreases solubility
    - Example: AgCl in NaCl solution less soluble than in pure water
  • pH Effects:
    - For salts with basic anions (CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻, OH⁻)
    - Lower pH increases solubility
    - Example: CuS solubility increases as [H₃O⁺] increases
      - Uses K_sp, K_a1, K_a2 for H₂S
      - Mass balance: [S²⁻] + [HS⁻] + [H₂S] = total S
  • Complex Ion Formation:
    - Can increase solubility
    - Example: AgCl + 2NH₃ ⇌ Ag(NH₃)₂⁺ + Cl⁻
    - Formation constant K_f = [Ag(NH₃)₂⁺]/([Ag⁺][NH₃]²)
    - Overall: AgCl(s) + 2NH₃ ⇌ Ag(NH₃)₂⁺ + Cl⁻, K = K_sp × K_f
    - Solubility decreases initially (common ion), then increases (complex formation)

IV.E - Acid-Base Equilibria
  • Acid Dissociation Constant (K_a):
    - HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻, K_a = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]
    - Stronger acid → larger K_a
    - pK_a = -log K_a
  • Base Dissociation Constant (K_b):
    - B + H₂O ⇌ BH⁺ + OH⁻, K_b = [BH⁺][OH⁻]/[B]
    - K_a × K_b = K_w = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ (for conjugate pair)
  • pH Calculations:
    - pH = -log[H⁺]
    - pOH = -log[OH⁻]
    - pH + pOH = 14
  • Buffer Solutions:
    - Resist pH changes
    - Contains weak acid and its conjugate base (or weak base and conjugate acid)
    - Henderson-Hasselbalch: pH = pK_a + log([A⁻]/[HA])
    - Example: Buffer with pH = 4.76 using acetic acid (K_a = 1.80 × 10⁻⁵)
      - pK_a = 4.74
      - [A⁻]/[HA] = 10^(pH - pK_a) = 10^(4.76 - 4.74) = 1.05
    - To prepare: Add appropriate ratio of acid and conjugate base

=== SECTION V: THERMODYNAMICS ===

V.A - Spontaneity
  • Spontaneous Process:
    - Occurs without external intervention
    - Not necessarily fast
  • Factors Affecting Spontaneity:
    - Enthalpy (ΔH): Exothermic favors spontaneity
    - Entropy (ΔS): Increase in disorder favors spontaneity
    - Temperature: Affects relative importance
  • Gibbs Free Energy:
    - ΔG = ΔH - TΔS
    - ΔG < 0: Spontaneous
    - ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous
    - ΔG = 0: At equilibrium
  • Examples:
    - Endothermic reaction producing gas: Can be spontaneous (entropy increase)
    - Exothermic reaction: Often spontaneous
    - Solid dissolving (endothermic): Can be spontaneous if entropy increases
    - Gas condensing (exothermic): Spontaneous at low T, non-spontaneous at high T
    - Exothermic with fewer gas moles: Depends on temperature

V.B - Phase Changes
  • Enthalpy Changes:
    - Vaporization: Endothermic (ΔH > 0)
    - Condensation: Exothermic (ΔH < 0)
    - Melting: Endothermic
    - Freezing: Exothermic
  • Entropy Changes:
    - Gas > Liquid > Solid
    - More disorder → higher entropy
  • Temperature Dependence:
    - Phase changes occur at specific temperatures
    - At transition temperature: ΔG = 0

=== SECTION VI: LABORATORY TECHNIQUES ===

VI.A - Titration
  • Purpose:
    - Determine concentration of unknown solution
    - Identify equivalence point
  • Acid-Base Titration:
    - Add base to acid (or vice versa)
    - Equivalence point: Moles acid = moles base
    - For diprotic acid: Need 2× moles of base
    - Example: H₂A (MW = 100.8 g/mol), 0.748 g titrated with 28.2 mL NaOH
      - Moles H₂A = 0.748/100.8 = 0.00742 mol
      - Moles NaOH = 2 × 0.00742 = 0.0148 mol
      - [NaOH] = 0.0148/0.0282 = 0.526 M
  • Indicators:
    - Change color at specific pH
    - Choose based on equivalence point pH

VI.B - Spectrophotometry
  • Beer's Law:
    - A = εbc
    - A = absorbance
    - ε = molar absorptivity
    - b = path length
    - c = concentration
  • Applications:
    - Determine concentration from absorbance
    - Monitor reaction progress
    - Example: Food coloring + bleach reaction
      - Measure absorbance at λ_max = 635 nm
      - Plot ln(A) vs. t for first-order kinetics
  • Wavelength Selection:
    - Use λ_max for maximum sensitivity
    - Using wrong wavelength affects accuracy

VI.C - Gas Collection
  • Methods:
    - Water displacement
    - Gas syringe
    - Eudiometer
  • Ideal Gas Law:
    - PV = nRT
    - Use to find moles from volume, pressure, temperature
    - Example: 0.361 L at 293 K, 1 atm
      - n = PV/RT = (1)(0.361)/(0.0821)(293) = 0.0150 mol

VI.D - Precipitation and Filtration
  • Purpose:
    - Separate solid from solution
    - Determine mass of precipitate
  • Process:
    - Add precipitating agent
    - Filter through filter paper
    - Dry and weigh
    - Mass of precipitate = (filter paper + precipitate) - (filter paper)
  • Example: Determine K₂CO₃ in antacid tablet
    - React with SrCl₂: SrCl₂ + K₂CO₃ → SrCO₃ + 2KCl
    - Mass SrCO₃ = 2.81 - 1.22 = 1.59 g
    - Moles SrCO₃ = 1.59/147.63 = 0.0108 mol
    - Moles K₂CO₃ = 0.0108 mol
    - Mass K₂CO₃ = 0.0108 × 138.21 = 1.49 g
    - Mass % = (1.49/3.90) × 100% = 38.2%

=== SECTION VII: ADVANCED TOPICS ===

VII.A - Advanced Kinetics
  • Steady-State Approximation:
    - For intermediates: d[I]/dt = 0
    - Rate of formation = rate of consumption
    - Used to derive complex rate laws
  • Pre-equilibrium:
    - Fast equilibrium before slow step
    - Rate law involves equilibrium constant
    - Example: A ⇌ I (fast, K_eq), I + B → P (slow, k)
      - Rate = k'[A][B] where k' = K_eq × k
  • Temperature Dependence:
    - Arrhenius equation: k = Ae^(-E_a/RT)
    - Two temperatures: ln(k₂/k₁) = (E_a/R)(1/T₁ - 1/T₂)
    - Example: k₁ = 2.50 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹ at 300K, k₂ = 7.50 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹ at 310K
      - E_a = R × ln(k₂/k₁) / (1/T₁ - 1/T₂) = 61.2 kJ/mol
  • Complex Mechanisms:
    - Chain reactions: Initiation, propagation, termination
    - Enzyme kinetics: Michaelis-Menten
    - Photochemical reactions: Light absorption creates excited states

VII.B - Complex Equilibria
  • Multiple Equilibria:
    - Combine K values
    - Example: Fe(OH)₃(s) ⇌ Fe³⁺ + 3OH⁻ (K_sp)
    - Fe³⁺ + SCN⁻ ⇌ FeSCN²⁺ (K_f)
    - Overall: Fe(OH)₃ + SCN⁻ ⇌ FeSCN²⁺ + 3OH⁻ (K = K_sp × K_f)
  • Solubility with pH:
    - For salts with basic anions
    - Use K_sp, K_a values, mass balance
    - Example: CuS in acidic solution
      - K_sp = [Cu²⁺][S²⁻]
      - K_a1, K_a2 for H₂S
      - Mass balance: [S²⁻] + [HS⁻] + [H₂S] = [Cu²⁺]
      - Solve for solubility

VII.C - Isotope Effects
  • Kinetic Isotope Effects (KIE):
    - Different rates for different isotopes
    - Primary KIE: Bond to isotope broken
    - Secondary KIE: Isotope not in bond broken
  • Heavy Atom KIE:
    - Generally small (< 1.05)
    - Example: ¹²C vs. ¹³C
    - Due to zero-point energy differences
    - Smaller than H/D effects

=== SECTION I: NERVOUS SYSTEM ===

I. NEUROANATOMY & STRUCTURAL ORGANIZATION

I.A - Central Nervous System (CNS) Gross Anatomy
  • Brain divisions: Cerebrum, Cerebellum, Brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla)
  • Lobes of the cerebral cortex: Frontal, Parietal, Temporal, Occipital
    - Frontal lobe functions: Motor control, executive function, personality
    - Parietal lobe: Somatosensory processing, spatial awareness
    - Temporal lobe: Auditory processing, memory, language comprehension
    - Occipital lobe: Visual processing
  • Cerebellum: Coordination, balance, motor learning
  • Brainstem structures: Midbrain (colliculi), Pons, Medulla oblongata
  • Reticular Formation: Arousal, sleep-wake cycle regulation
  • Thalamus: Sensory relay (except olfaction), consciousness
  • Hypothalamus: Autonomic regulation, neuroendocrine control, thermoregulation
  • Hippocampus: Long-term memory consolidation, spatial memory
  • Amygdala: Emotional processing, fear responses, memory consolidation
  • Basal Ganglia: Movement selection, habit formation, reward processing
    - Components: Striatum, Globus pallidus, Substantia nigra
  • Corpus Callosum: Interhemispheric communication
  • Limbic System: Emotion, motivation, memory (amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate)
  • Brainstem Pathways: Corticospinal tract, spinothalamic tract, dorsal columns
  • Cranial Nerves: CN I (Olfactory) through CN XII (Hypoglossal)
    - Functions, nuclei locations, peripheral distributions
  • Spinal Cord: Gray matter (dorsal/ventral horns), white matter tracts
  • Ventricular System: Lateral ventricles, 3rd ventricle, 4th ventricle
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF): Production sites, circulation, functions
  • Meninges: Dura mater, Arachnoid mater, Pia mater
  • Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB): Endothelial tight junctions, astrocytes, selective permeability

I.B - Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) Structure
  • Somatic nervous system: Voluntary skeletal muscle control
  • Autonomic nervous system divisions: Sympathetic, Parasympathetic, Enteric
  • Sympathetic nervous system:
    - Thoracolumbar outflow
    - Norepinephrine neurotransmitter
    - Fight-or-flight responses
    - Adrenergic receptors (α1, α2, β1, β2)
  • Parasympathetic nervous system:
    - Craniosacral outflow (CN III, VII, IX, X; S2-S4 spinal nerves)
    - Acetylcholine neurotransmitter
    - Rest-and-digest responses
    - Muscarinic receptors
  • Enteric nervous system: Gut innervation, intrinsic control
  • Reflex arcs: Sensory neuron → spinal cord synapse → motor neuron
  • Dermatomes: Sensory distribution by spinal nerve
  • Myotomes: Motor distribution by spinal nerve

I.C - Cellular Components of the Nervous System
  • Neurons: Structure and classification
    - Soma (cell body), Dendrites, Axon, Axon terminals
    - Unipolar, Bipolar, Multipolar neuron morphology
    - Sensory (afferent), Motor (efferent), Interneurons
  • Glial cells:
    - Astrocytes: Support, BBB formation, ion regulation
    - Oligodendrocytes: Myelination in CNS
    - Schwann cells: Myelination in PNS (one Schwann cell per axon segment)
    - Microglia: Immune function, phagocytosis
    - Ependymal cells: CSF production
  • Nodes of Ranvier: Unmyelinated axon segments between myelin sheaths
  • Synapses: Chemical synapses (presynaptic, synaptic cleft, postsynaptic)
  • Neuromuscular junction: Motor end plate, acetylcholine release

I.D - Spinal Cord Anatomy
  • Cross-sectional structure: Dorsal/ventral horns, lateral horns
  • White matter: Dorsal columns, spinothalamic tract, corticospinal tract
  • Gray matter: Rexed laminae organization (laminae I-X)
  • Cervical, Thoracic, Lumbar, Sacral segments
  • Spinal cord injuries and lesions:
    - Brown-Séquard syndrome: Ipsilateral motor loss + contralateral pain/temp loss
    - Anterior spinal artery occlusion: Loss of motor + pain/temp below lesion
    - Dorsal column lesions: Loss of proprioception/vibration (ipsilateral)
    - Central cord syndrome: Greater upper limb than lower limb loss

I.E - Visual System Anatomy
  • Eye structure:
    - Cornea: Refraction, immune protection
    - Anterior chamber: Aqueous humor
    - Iris: Pupil size regulation
    - Lens: Accommodation, refraction
    - Vitreous humor: Light transmission
    - Retina: Photoreceptors, bipolar cells, ganglion cells
    - Optic disc: Blind spot (no photoreceptors)
    - Macula lutea: Central, high-acuity vision
    - Fovea centralis: Highest rod and cone density
  • Accessory structures: Eyelids, lacrimal apparatus, extraocular muscles
  • Optic pathways:
    - Optic nerve (CN II), Optic chiasm, Optic tract
    - Meyers loop: Visual field defects with temporal lobe lesions
    - Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) relay in thalamus
    - Primary visual cortex (V1) in occipital lobe
    - Visual field organization: Contralateral representation
  • Photoreceptors:
    - Rods: Dim light vision, achromatic, high sensitivity
    - Cones: Color vision, high acuity, three types (L, M, S wavelengths)
  • Retinal cell types:
    - Photoreceptors, Bipolar cells, Ganglion cells
    - Horizontal cells, Amacrine cells (lateral processing)
  • Pupil control:
    - Parasympathetic (CN III): Pupil constriction (miosis)
    - Sympathetic: Pupil dilation (mydriasis)
    - Light reflex pathway
    - Accommodation reflex: Lens thickening, pupil constriction, convergence

I.F - Auditory System Anatomy
  • External ear: Pinna, auditory canal, cerumen production
  • Middle ear (ossicles):
    - Malleus (hammer), Incus (anvil), Stapes (stirrup)
    - Eustachian tube: Pressure equalization, nasopharyngeal connection
    - Tympanic membrane: Vibration transmission
  • Inner ear:
    - Cochlea: Spiral organ, frequency tonotopy
    - Basilar membrane: Frequency separation by location
    - Hair cells: Inner (120,000) and outer (20,000)
    - Stereocilia: Mechanoreceptor structures
    - Organ of Corti: Hair cell and supporting cell arrangement
    - Endolymph: High K+, low Na+ fluid (K+ from stria vascularis)
    - Perilymph: High Na+, low K+ fluid (similar to blood)
  • Auditory processing:
    - Cochlear tonotopy: Base (high freq) to apex (low freq)
    - Frequency discrimination: Place theory and temporal coding
    - Auditory nerve (CN VIII): Bipolar neurons from spiral ganglion
  • Central auditory pathway:
    - Cochlear nucleus (medulla), Superior olivary complex
    - Inferior colliculus, Medial geniculate nucleus (thalamus)
    - Primary auditory cortex (superior temporal lobe)

I.G - Vestibular System Anatomy
  • Semicircular canals: Angular acceleration detection (3 orthogonal planes)
  • Utricle and Saccule: Linear acceleration and gravity detection
  • Ampulla: Cupula and sensory epithelium
  • Hair cells and stereocilia: Mechanoreception mechanism
  • Vestibular nuclei (medulla/pons): CN VIII connections
  • Vestibuloocular reflex (VOR): Eye stabilization during head movement
  • Vestibulospinal reflex: Postural balance

I.H - Olfactory System Anatomy
  • Olfactory epithelium: Location (superior nasal cavity, 10-20% of area)
  • Olfactory neurons: Bipolar, neuroepithelial cells with cilia
  • Olfactory receptors: ~400 functional types in humans
  • Olfactory transduction: G-protein coupled receptors, cAMP signaling
  • Olfactory bulb: Glomeruli, mitral cells, granule cells
  • Cribriform plate: CN I passage from nasal cavity to olfactory bulb
  • Primary olfactory cortex: Piriform cortex, amygdala, orbitofrontal cortex
  • Olfactory regeneration: Basal stem cells, neurogenesis capability
  • Olfactory disorders:
    - Anosmia: Loss of smell
    - Parosmia: Distorted smell perception

I.I - Gustatory System Anatomy
  • Taste bud distribution:
    - Fungiform papillae (anterior 2/3 tongue, anterior-lateral): Bitter, salty, sweet, sour
    - Circumvallate papillae (posterior 1/3 tongue, arranged in V): Bitter
    - Foliate papillae (lateral tongue): Sour
  • Taste receptor cells: Elongated epithelial cells with microvilli
  • Taste modalities: Sweet, Salty, Sour, Bitter, Umami
  • Taste transduction mechanisms:
    - Sweet: GPCR → cAMP signaling
    - Salty: Direct ion channel (NaCl) and nucleotide taste receptors
    - Sour: Acid (H+) blocking K+ channels, TRPV channels
    - Bitter: GPCR → IP3 signaling
    - Umami: GPCR for glutamate
  • Taste bud innervation:
    - Anterior 2/3 tongue: CN VII (Facial)
    - Posterior 1/3 tongue: CN IX (Glossopharyngeal)
    - Pharynx: CN X (Vagus)
  • Taste disorders:
    - Ageusia: Loss of taste
    - Dysgeusia: Distorted taste perception
    - Hypogeusia: Reduced taste sensitivity

I.J - Somatosensory System Anatomy
  • Mechanoreceptors in skin:
    - Meissner's corpuscles: Light touch, 2-point discrimination
    - Pacinian corpuscles: Pressure and vibration, rapid adaptation
    - Merkel cells: Sustained pressure, fine detail
    - Ruffini endings: Skin stretch
  • Proprioceptors:
    - Muscle spindles: Intrafusal muscle fiber innervation, stretch detection
    - Golgi tendon organs: Tension detection
    - Joint receptors: Position sensing
  • Primary sensory neurons:
    - Dorsal root ganglia location and organization
    - Sensory modality segregation
  • Spinal pathways:
    - Dorsal columns (proprioception/vibration): Fasciculus gracilis, cuneatus
    - Spinothalamic tract (pain/temperature): Lateral vs anterior
    - Spinocerebellar tracts: Unconscious proprioception
  • Somatosensory cortex (S1): Somatotopic organization, homunculus
  • Pain disorders:
    - Referred pain: Projection to distant body area
    - Phantom limb pain: Pain in absent limb
    - Allodynia: Pain from non-noxious stimulus
    - Hyperalgesia: Increased pain response

II. NEUROPHYSIOLOGY & CELLULAR FUNCTION

II.A - Ion Channels and Membrane Potential
  • Resting membrane potential: -70 mV in neurons
  • Ion concentrations:
    - Intracellular: High K+, Low Na+
    - Extracellular: Low K+, High Na+
  • Nernst equation: Equilibrium potential for single ion
    Eion = (RT/zF) ln([ion]out/[ion]in) = (61/z) log([ion]out/[ion]in) at 37°C
  • Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (GHK) equation: Resting membrane potential
    Vm = (RT/F) ln((Pk[K+]out + PNa[Na+]out + PCl[Cl-]in) / (Pk[K+]in + PNa[Na+]in + PCl[Cl-]out))
  • Na+/K+ ATPase: Active transport (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in per ATP)
  • Leak channels: Differential permeability to K+, Na+, Cl-
  • Voltage-gated ion channels:
    - Sodium channels: Activation, inactivation gates, tetrodotoxin blockade
    - Potassium channels: Multiple types, delayed opening
    - Calcium channels: L-type, P/Q-type, N-type, T-type
  • Ligand-gated ion channels: Nicotinic ACh, GABAA, AMPA, NMDA
  • K+ channel blockers: Tetraethylammonium (TEA)
  • Na+ channel blockers: Tetrodotoxin (TTX), Local anesthetics

II.B - Action Potentials
  • Resting state: Voltage-gated Na+ inactivated, K+ channels closed
  • Depolarization phase:
    - Threshold reached (-55 mV)
    - Voltage-gated Na+ channels open (activation gate)
    - Rapid Na+ influx → positive feedback → depolarization
    - Peak: Na+ equilibrium potential (~+60 mV)
  • Repolarization phase:
    - Voltage-gated Na+ channels inactivate
    - Voltage-gated K+ channels open (delayed)
    - K+ efflux repolarizes membrane
  • Hyperpolarization (afterhyperpolarization):
    - K+ channels remain open slightly too long
    - Membrane potential becomes more negative than resting
  • Refractory periods:
    - Absolute refractory period: Na+ channels inactivated, no new AP possible
    - Relative refractory period: K+ channels still open, higher threshold needed
  • Hodgkin-Huxley model: Mathematical description of conductances
    - Gating variables: m (Na+ activation), h (Na+ inactivation), n (K+ activation)
    - Voltage- and time-dependent changes
  • All-or-none principle: AP fires fully or not at all
  • Conduction velocity factors:
    - Axon diameter: Larger diameter → faster conduction
    - Myelination: Saltatory conduction in myelinated axons
    - Temperature: Increased temperature → faster conduction

II.C - Synaptic Transmission
  • Neurotransmitter types:
    - Amino acids: Glutamate (excitatory), GABA (inhibitory), Glycine (inhibitory)
    - Monoamines: Dopamine, Norepinephrine, Serotonin, Histamine
    - Neuropeptides: Substance P, Endorphins, Enkephalins
    - Acetylcholine: Nicotinic (ionotropic), Muscarinic (metabotropic)
  • Synaptic vesicles: SNARE proteins, Synaptotagmin, Syntaxin
  • Neurotransmitter release:
    - Ca2+ influx triggering mechanism
    - Exocytosis and vesicle fusion
    - Quantum hypothesis: Single vesicle = minimal unit (quant)
  • Postsynaptic receptors:
    - Ionotropic (ligand-gated): Direct ion channel opening
    - Metabotropic (G-protein coupled): Second messenger cascades
  • Synaptic plasticity:
    - Short-term: Facilitation, Depression, Post-tetanic potentiation
    - Long-term: LTP (Long-Term Potentiation), LTD (Long-Term Depression)
  • NMDA receptor: Voltage-dependent Mg2+ block, Ca2+ influx role in LTP
  • AMPA receptor: Primary fast glutamate receptor
  • Synaptic strength modulation: Receptor insertion/removal, neurotransmitter synthesis
  • Hebbian learning: "Cells that fire together, wire together"
  • Homeostatic synaptic scaling: Compensation for activity levels

II.D - Neurotransmitter Synthesis and Metabolism
  • Acetylcholine (ACh):
    - Synthesis: Acetyl-CoA + Choline → ACh (Choline Acetyltransferase, ChAT)
    - Degradation: Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in synaptic cleft
    - Reuptake: Choline recycled back to presynaptic terminal
  • Catecholamines (Dopamine, Norepinephrine, Epinephrine):
    - Synthesis: Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine
    - Degradation: MAO (Monoamine Oxidase) and COMT (Catechol-O-Methyltransferase)
    - Reuptake: DAT, NET, SERT transporters
  • Serotonin (5-HT):
    - Synthesis: Tryptophan → 5-HTP → Serotonin
    - Degradation: MAO
    - Reuptake: SERT transporter
  • GABA:
    - Synthesis: Glutamate → GABA (Glutamic Acid Decarboxylase, GAD)
    - Degradation: GABA transaminase (GABA-T)
  • Glutamate:
    - Primary excitatory neurotransmitter
    - Reuptake: Excitatory amino acid transporters (EAAT)
  • Neuropeptides: Slow-acting, neuromodulatory roles

II.E - Neurotoxins and Pharmacological Agents
  • Tetrodotoxin (TTX): Blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels
  • Tetraethylammonium (TEA): Blocks voltage-gated K+ channels
  • α-Bungarotoxin: Irreversible acetylcholine receptor antagonist
  • Strychnine: Glycine receptor antagonist (causes muscle rigidity)
  • Curare: Acetylcholine receptor antagonist (muscle paralysis)
  • Botulinum toxin: SNARE protein cleavage, preventing ACh release
  • Ricin: Protein synthesis inhibitor
  • MAO inhibitors: Increased monoamine levels (therapeutic use)
  • Anticholinergics: ACh receptor antagonists (e.g., atropine)
  • Local anesthetics: Na+ channel blockade (e.g., lidocaine, procaine)

II.F - Neurotransmitter Receptors
  • Acetylcholine:
    - Nicotinic: Ionotropic, excitatory (muscle, neurons)
    - Muscarinic: Metabotropic, diverse effects (M1-M5)
  • Glutamate:
    - AMPA: Fast, primary excitatory receptor
    - NMDA: Voltage-dependent, Ca2+ permeable
    - Kainate: Fast synaptic transmission
    - Metabotropic: mGluR1-8
  • GABA:
    - GABAA: Ionotropic, inhibitory, Cl- channel
    - GABAB: Metabotropic, inhibitory
  • Dopamine (D1-D5 receptors): G-protein coupled
  • Serotonin (5-HT1A, 5-HT2, 5-HT3, etc.): Multiple subtypes, diverse effects
  • Norepinephrine:
    - α1: Excitatory, GPCR
    - α2: Inhibitory, GPCR
    - β1, β2, β3: Adrenergic receptors

III. NEUROLOGICAL SYSTEMS & INTEGRATION

III.A - Motor Control Systems
  • Primary motor cortex (M1): Contralateral voluntary movement control
  • Premotor cortex: Movement planning
  • Supplementary motor area: Bilateral and coordinated movements
  • Corticospinal tract: Descending motor pathway (90% decussate at medullary pyramids)
  • Brainstem motor pathways: Vestibulospinal, Reticulospinal, Rubrospinal
  • Cerebellum: Motor coordination, error correction, motor learning
    - Purkinje cells: Inhibitory output
    - Granule cells: Input layer
    - Deep cerebellar nuclei: Output
  • Basal Ganglia: Movement selection and initiation
    - Direct pathway: Facilitate movement
    - Indirect pathway: Inhibit unwanted movement
    - Substantia nigra: Dopamine release to striatum
  • Proprioceptive feedback: Muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs

III.B - Reflex Arcs and Spinal Circuits
  • Monosynaptic reflex: Stretch reflex (patellar, Achilles)
    - Ia sensory neuron synapse directly on motor neuron
    - 1-2 ms latency
  • Polysynaptic reflex: Withdrawal reflex
    - Multiple interneurons involved
    - Reciprocal inhibition: Flexors contract while extensors relax
  • Renshaw cell inhibition: Motor neuron axon collateral feedback
  • Central pattern generators: Rhythmic motor output (walking, swimming)

III.C - Sleep and Arousal
  • Sleep stages:
    - NREM (Non-REM) Stage 1-3: Progressive deepening
      - Stage 1: Transition, theta waves (4-8 Hz)
      - Stage 2: Sleep spindles, K-complexes
      - Stage 3: Slow-wave sleep (delta waves, 0.5-4 Hz)
    - REM (Rapid Eye Movement): Dreams, muscle atonia
  • Circadian rhythm: Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) control
  • Melatonin: Pineal gland hormone, sleep regulation
  • Orexin/Hypocretin: Promote wakefulness
  • GABA and sleep: Increased during sleep
  • Reticular activating system: Arousal control
  • Sleep disorders:
    - Sleep apnea: Obstructive and central types
    - Narcolepsy: Hypocretin/Orexin deficiency
    - Restless leg syndrome: Dopamine dysregulation
    - REM behavior disorder: Loss of muscle atonia during REM
    - Insomnia: Sleep initiation/maintenance difficulty

III.D - Memory and Learning
  • Memory types:
    - Sensory memory: Brief (visual iconic ~250 ms, auditory echoic ~2-4 s)
    - Short-term/Working memory: 7±2 items, ~30 seconds
    - Long-term memory: Potentially permanent
      - Declarative (explicit): Facts, events (hippocampus-dependent)
      - Procedural (implicit): Skills, habits (basal ganglia)
      - Emotional memory: Amygdala-dependent
  • Long-term potentiation (LTP): Strengthening of synapses
    - Postsynaptic Ca2+ influx through NMDA receptors
    - AMPA receptor insertion
    - Gene expression changes
  • Long-term depression (LTD): Weakening of synapses
    - Lower-frequency stimulation
    - AMPA receptor removal
  • Synaptic consolidation: Hours to days
  • Systems consolidation: Days to years (hippocampus → cortex)
  • Reconsolidation: Memory lability after retrieval
  • Extinction learning: New inhibitory associations formed

III.E - Language and Speech
  • Broca's area (inferior frontal, dominant hemisphere): Speech production
    - Damage → Broca's aphasia (non-fluent, agrammatic)
  • Wernicke's area (superior temporal, dominant hemisphere): Language comprehension
    - Damage → Wernicke's aphasia (fluent but nonsensical)
  • Arcuate fasciculus: White matter tract connecting Broca's and Wernicke's
  • Primary motor cortex: Articulation control
  • Primary auditory cortex: Sound perception
  • Language and speech disorders:
    - Dysarthria: Speech articulation difficulty
    - Dysphasia/Aphasia: Language production/comprehension deficit
    - Apraxia: Motor planning difficulty
    - Agnosia: Sensory recognition failure (visual, auditory, tactile)
    - Neglect syndrome: Contralateral space/body neglect

III.F - Higher Brain Functions
  • Attention: Prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate control
  • Executive function: Prefrontal cortex (planning, decision-making)
  • Spatial awareness: Parietal cortex
  • Emotional processing: Amygdala, prefrontal cortex interaction
  • Social cognition: Medial prefrontal cortex, amygdala, fusiform gyrus
  • Neuroplasticity: Cortical reorganization, Phantom limb remapping
  • Neurogenesis: Adult hippocampal neurogenesis
  • Synaptic pruning: Developmental refinement of connections
  • Myelination development: Childhood and adolescent brain maturation
  • Critical periods: Sensitive periods for development
  • Neuroimaging techniques:
    - Electroencephalography (EEG): Electrical brain activity
    - Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): Soft tissue imaging, No radiation
    - Functional MRI (fMRI): Blood oxygen level-dependent (BOLD) imaging
    - Computerized tomography (CT): Bone and fast imaging
    - Positron emission tomography (PET): Metabolic imaging

III.G - Neurological Pathologies
  • Multiple Sclerosis (MS): Demyelinating disease, oligodendrocyte loss
    - Plaques, optic neuritis, pyramidal signs, spasticity
  • Parkinson's Disease: Dopamine loss in substantia nigra
    - Bradykinesia, rigidity, resting tremor, postural instability
  • Alzheimer's Disease: β-amyloid plaques, tau tangles
    - Cholinergic neuron loss, cognitive decline
  • Huntington's Disease: CAG trinucleotide repeat
    - GABA neuron loss in basal ganglia, involuntary movements
  • Epilepsy: Seizure disorders, hyperexcitability
    - Tonic-clonic, absence, focal seizures
    - Temporal lobe epilepsy: Seizures originating in medial temporal structures
    - Absence seizures: Brief loss of consciousness, 3 Hz spike-and-wave EEG
    - Status epilepticus: Prolonged or repeated seizures without recovery
    - Electroencephalogram (EEG): Abnormal spike patterns
  • Stroke:
    - Ischemic: Blood flow blockade, infarction
    - Hemorrhagic: Bleeding, mass effect
    - Subdural hematoma: Chronic and acute presentations
    - Epidural hematoma: Traumatic brain injury
    - Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Severe headache, meningeal signs
  • Myasthenia Gravis: Antibodies against acetylcholine receptors
    - Muscle weakness, fatigability, anti-AChE drugs
  • Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome: P/Q-type Ca2+ channel antibodies
    - Muscle weakness, autonomic symptoms
  • Wernicke Encephalopathy: Thiamine (B1) deficiency
    - Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion
  • Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease: Peripheral neuropathy, axonal or demyelinating
  • Cerebral Palsy: Developmental motor disorder
  • Autism Spectrum Disorder: Social and communication deficits
  • Brain tumors: Primary and secondary
    - Glioblastoma multiforme: Most common primary brain tumor
    - Meningioma: Dural-based tumor
  • Cerebral aneurysms: Berry aneurysms
  • Hydrocephalus: Obstructive vs. communicating
  • Neurogenic shock: Spinal cord injury complication
  • Peripheral neuropathy: Causes (DM, chemotherapy, B12 deficiency)
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome: Autoimmune polyneuropathy
  • Bell's palsy: CN VII palsy
  • Trigeminal neuralgia: CN V pain
  • Horner's syndrome: Sympathetic interruption (miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis)
  • Meningitis: CNS infection, meningeal inflammation
  • Encephalitis: Brain inflammation
  • Polio: Poliovirus motor neuron destruction
  • Tetanus: Clostridium tetani neuromuscular blockade
  • Rabies: Fatal encephalitis
  • Prion diseases: Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD)

=== SECTION II: SPECIAL SENSES ===

IV. VISION

IV.A - Refractive Errors and Corrections
  • Myopia (Nearsightedness): Cornea too steep or eye too long
    - Objects beyond focal length are blurred
    - Corrected with concave lens
  • Hyperopia (Farsightedness): Cornea too flat or eye too short
    - Objects before focal length are blurred
    - Corrected with convex lens
  • Astigmatism: Unequal corneal or lens curvature
    - Blurred vision at all distances
    - Corrected with cylindrical lens
  • Presbyopia: Age-related lens stiffness
    - Loss of accommodation ability
    - Affects near vision
  • Color blindness: X-linked red/green cone deficiency
    - Red-green color vision loss
    - Protanomaly/Deuteranomaly: Mild red-green color blindness
    - Achromatopsia: Complete color blindness
  • Visual disorders:
    - Night blindness: Vitamin A deficiency, Retinitis pigmentosa
    - Macular degeneration: Age-related central vision loss
    - Glaucoma: Elevated intraocular pressure → Optic nerve damage
    - Cataracts: Lens opacity
    - Keratoconus: Corneal ectasia
    - Amblyopia: Lazy eye
    - Strabismus: Eye misalignment
    - Photophobia: Light sensitivity
    - Scotoma: Visual field defect
    - Hemianopsia: Loss of half visual field
    - Anopsia: Complete visual field loss

IV.B - Phototransduction
  • Photoreceptor structure: Outer segment (disc stack), Inner segment, Synaptic terminal
  • Photopigment: Opsin + Retinal (11-cis)
    - Rhodopsin in rods (λmax 507 nm)
    - Cone pigments (L, M, S cone pigments)
  • Light activation:
    - Photon absorption → retinal isomerization (11-cis → all-trans)
    - Opsin conformational change → G-protein coupling
  • G-protein cascade (transducin):
    - Transducin-GTP activation
    - PDE (Phosphodiesterase) activation
    - cGMP hydrolysis → cGMP-gated channel closure
    - Hyperpolarization (−2 to −5 mV)
  • cGMP-gated channels: Maintained open in darkness
    - Closure reduces Na+/Ca2+ influx
  • Calcium feedback: Ca2+ reduction → GC (Guanylyl cyclase) activation → cGMP synthesis
  • Rhodopsin regeneration: All-trans-retinal → 11-cis-retinal (via retinol)
  • Dark adaptation: Pupil dilation, cones adaptation (fast), rods adaptation (slow, 20-30 min)
  • Light adaptation: Pupil constriction, calcium feedback

IV.C - Retinal Processing
  • Rod-cone interaction: Rods for night vision (scotopic), cones for day vision (photopic)
  • Bipolar cells:
    - ON-bipolar cells: Depolarize to light (mGluR6 inhibition removal)
    - OFF-bipolar cells: Hyperpolarize to light
  • Horizontal cells: Lateral inhibition, contrast enhancement
  • Amacrine cells: Lateral interactions, motion detection
  • Ganglion cells: Center-surround receptive fields
    - ON-center: Respond to central light
    - OFF-center: Respond to central darkness
  • Optic disc: No photoreceptors (blind spot)
  • Fovea: Highest rod and cone density, minimal light scattering
  • Peripheral retina: High rod density, low acuity

IV.D - Color Vision
  • Trichromatic theory: L (564 nm), M (533 nm), S (420 nm) cone pigments
  • Opponent-process theory: Red-green, Yellow-blue, Light-dark channels
  • Color blindness:
    - Protanopia: L cone loss (red-green)
    - Deuteranopia: M cone loss (red-green)
    - Tritanopia: S cone loss (rare, blue-yellow)
  • Color constancy: Perception remains stable under varying illumination

IV.E - Visual Pathways and Processing
  • Optic nerve: CN II, ganglion cell axons
  • Optic chiasm: Nasal fibers cross (bitemporal)
  • Optic tract: Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) projection
  • Lateral geniculate nucleus: Layer organization, parvocellular (fine detail) and magnocellular (motion)
  • Primary visual cortex (V1): Occipital lobe, retinotopic mapping
    - Simple cells: Orientation selectivity
    - Complex cells: Orientation + direction selectivity
    - Ocular dominance columns
    - Cytochrome oxidase blobs: Color processing
  • Visual field organization:
    - Contralateral representation
    - Monocular crescent at edge of visual field
    - Meyers loop: Temporal lobe portion of optic radiation
  • Ventral stream ("what"): Temporal lobe, object recognition
    - V2, V4, Inferior temporal cortex
  • Dorsal stream ("where"): Parietal lobe, spatial processing
    - V2, MT (middle temporal), Posterior parietal cortex

IV.F - Intrinsically Photosensitive Retinal Ganglion Cells (ipRGCs)
  • Melanopsin photopigment: λmax 480 nm
  • Non-image-forming vision functions:
    - Circadian rhythm regulation
    - Pupillary light reflex
  • Direct innervation of suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)
  • Slower response than rod/cone signaling

IV.G - Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex and Eye Movement
  • VOR: Compensatory eye movement during head rotation
    - 3-synapse arc
    - Minimal latency (~6 ms)
  • Saccades: Rapid eye movements, frontal eye fields control
  • Smooth pursuit: Following moving objects, visual cortex control
  • Vergence: Convergence (near) and divergence (far)

V. HEARING AND BALANCE

V.A - Auditory Transduction
  • Basilar membrane vibration: Position determines frequency
  • Hair cell stereocilia arrangement: 3-D organization (tallest to shortest)
  • Stereocilia deflection:
    - Deflection toward tallest → Tip links open, cation influx (K+ from endolymph)
    - Depolarization → Ca2+ influx → Neurotransmitter (Glutamate) release
    - Deflection away: Hyperpolarization
  • Adaptation: Fast (20-40 ms, motor protein climbing), Slow (seconds, Ca-CaMKII modulation)
  • Cochlear amplification: Outer hair cell motility (prestin protein)
  • Auditory nerve: CN VIII, bipolar neurons from spiral ganglion
    - ~30,000 neurons, ~10-20 inner hair cells per neuron convergence

V.B - Cochlear Frequency Analysis
  • Cochlear tonotopy: Base (40 kHz equivalent) to apex (100 Hz)
  • Place theory: Frequency determined by basilar membrane location
  • Temporal coding: Phase-locking to low-frequency stimuli
  • Frequency discrimination: Combination of place and temporal cues

V.C - Central Auditory Pathway
  • Cochlear nucleus (medulla): Dorsal and ventral divisions
    - Temporal processing
  • Superior olivary complex (medulla/pons):
    - Interaural time differences (ITD) for sound localization
    - Interaural level differences (ILD)
  • Inferior colliculus (midbrain): Binaural integration
  • Medial geniculate nucleus (thalamus): Relay to cortex
  • Primary auditory cortex (superior temporal lobe): Tonotopic organization
  • Medial Olivocochlear (MOC) pathway: Efferent feedback to outer hair cells
    - Reduces cochlear amplifier gain
    - Improves detection in background noise
    - Protective role against acoustic overexposure

V.D - Sound Localization
  • Interaural time difference (ITD): Timing difference between ears
    - Low frequencies (<1500 Hz)
  • Interaural level difference (ILD): Volume difference between ears
    - High frequencies (>1500 Hz)
  • Head-related transfer function (HRTF): Spectral cues for vertical localization
  • Superior olivary complex: ITD processing
  • Inferior colliculus: ITD and ILD integration
  • Hearing disorders:
    - Tinnitus: Auditory perception of sound without external source
    - Conductive hearing loss: Middle ear ossicle dysfunction
    - Sensorineural hearing loss: Inner ear or CN VIII dysfunction
    - Presbycusis: Age-related hearing loss
    - Otosclerosis: Stapes fixation
    - Eustachian tube dysfunction: Otitis media, Serous otitis
    - Cerumen impaction: Earwax buildup

V.E - Vestibular Transduction
  • Semicircular canals: Detect angular acceleration
    - Ampulla: Sensory epithelium with cupula
    - Stereocilia deflection with head movement
  • Utricle and Saccule: Detect linear acceleration and gravity
    - Otoliths (CaCO3 crystals) provide inertial mass
    - Stereocilia deflection proportional to acceleration
  • Vestibular nuclei: Medial, lateral, superior, inferior
  • Vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR): Eye stabilization
  • Vestibulospinal reflexes: Postural control

V.F - Balance and Equilibrium
  • Three sensory systems: Vestibular, proprioceptive, visual
  • Vestibular adaptation: Central compensation after unilateral loss
  • Nystagmus: Rhythmic eye movement during rotation or tilting
    - Types: Optokinetic, Post-caloric, Saccadic, Gaze-holding
  • Vertigo: Sensation of spinning (vestibular disorders)
    - Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV): Otolith displacement
    - Meniere's disease: Endolymphatic hydrops
    - Vestibular neuritis: CN VIII inflammation
    - Central vertigo: CNS pathology (stroke, tumor)

V.G - Taste and Olfaction Integration
  • Flavor perception: Combination of taste + olfaction + texture
  • Retronasal olfaction: Food aroma during swallowing

=== SECTION III: ENDOCRINE SYSTEM ===

VI. ENDOCRINE GLANDS AND HORMONE PHYSIOLOGY

VI.A - Hormone Classification and Signaling
  • Hormone types:
    - Steroid hormones: Lipophilic, intracellular receptors, gene transcription
      (Cortisol, Aldosterone, Testosterone, Estrogen, Progesterone)
    - Peptide/Protein hormones: Hydrophilic, cell surface receptors, rapid effects
      (Insulin, Glucagon, Growth hormone, ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH)
    - Monoamine hormones: Catecholamines (Epinephrine, Norepinephrine), Thyroid (T3, T4)
  • Signal transduction pathways:
    - Nuclear receptors: Ligand binding → DNA binding → Transcription
    - Tyrosine kinase receptors: Ligand → Autophosphorylation → MAPK/PI3K signaling
    - G-protein coupled receptors: Ligand → G-protein → Second messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG)
    - Ion channel receptors: Direct electrical effects
  • Second messengers: cAMP, IP3, DAG, Ca2+
  • Endocrine vs. Paracrine vs. Autocrine: Distance and scope of effects
  • Hormone transport:
    - Hydrophilic: Free or with binding proteins
    - Lipophilic: Binding proteins (cortisol-binding globulin, etc.)
  • Half-life variations: Minutes (catecholamines) to hours/days (steroid hormones)
  • Hormone degradation:
    - Hepatic metabolism: Conjugation, oxidation
    - Renal excretion
    - Receptor-mediated endocytosis and lysosomal degradation

VI.B - Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis
  • Hypothalamus: CRH (Corticotropin-releasing hormone) synthesis
    - Parvocellular neurons
  • Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis):
    - ACTH (Adrenocorticotropic hormone) secretion
    - Basophil cells
  • Adrenal Cortex:
    - Zona fasciculata: Cortisol production (17-hydroxylase, 11β-hydroxylase)
    - Cortisol signaling: Glucocorticoid receptors
    - Cortisol functions:
      * Gluconeogenesis promotion
      * Anti-inflammatory effects
      * Permissive effect (allows catecholamine action)
      * Stress response
  • Negative feedback: Cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH
  • Circadian rhythm: Peak cortisol early morning, nadir at night
  • Acute stress response: Rapid ACTH and cortisol release

VI.C - Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) Axis
  • Hypothalamus: TRH (Thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
  • Anterior Pituitary: TSH (Thyroid-stimulating hormone, Thyrotropin)
  • Thyroid Gland:
    - Follicular cells: T3 and T4 synthesis
    - Parafollicular cells (C cells): Calcitonin production
    - Thyroid peroxidase (TPO): Iodination of tyrosine
    - Thyroglobulin: T3/T4 carrier protein
  • T4 (Thyroxine): Main secreted form, peripheral conversion to T3
  • T3 (Triiodothyronine): Most biologically active form
  • Thyroid hormone functions:
    - Metabolic rate increase
    - Thermogenesis
    - Growth and development
    - CNS development
    - Cardiovascular effects (↑ HR, contractility)
  • Negative feedback: T3 and T4 inhibit TRH and TSH
  • Iodine requirement: Essential for thyroid hormone synthesis
  • Thyroid disorders:
    - Hyperthyroidism: Graves' disease (TSH receptor antibodies), Toxic nodule, Thyroiditis
      * Tachycardia, Heat intolerance, Weight loss, Exophthalmos (Graves')
      * Thyroid storm: Life-threatening severe hyperthyroidism
    - Hypothyroidism: Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Iodine deficiency
      * Bradycardia, Cold intolerance, Weight gain, Myxedema (severe)
      * Myxedema coma: Life-threatening severe hypothyroidism
    - Goiter: Thyroid enlargement
    - Cretinism: Congenital hypothyroidism (iodine deficiency)
    - Thyroid cancer: Papillary, Follicular, Medullary, Anaplastic

VI.D - Growth Hormone and IGF-1 Axis
  • Hypothalamus:
    - GHRH (Growth Hormone-releasing hormone): Stimulates GH
    - Somatostatin (Somatotropin release-inhibiting factor): Inhibits GH
  • Anterior Pituitary: Somatotroph cells secrete GH
  • Growth hormone functions:
    - Protein synthesis promotion
    - Lipolysis (fat breakdown)
    - Glucose production (anti-insulin effect)
    - IGF-1 stimulation
  • IGF-1 (Insulin-like Growth Factor 1):
    - Liver and peripheral tissue production (GH-stimulated)
    - Growth promotion (longitudinal bone growth, muscle)
    - Negative feedback on GH secretion
  • Growth hormone regulation:
    - Pulsatile secretion (multiple pulses per day)
    - Stimuli: Sleep, Exercise, Amino acids, Hypoglycemia, Stress
    - Inhibition: Hyperglycemia, Elevated FFA, IGF-1, Somatostatin
  • Growth hormone disorders:
    - Acromegaly: GH excess in adults
      * Enlarged hands/feet, Jaw prognathism, Carpal tunnel, Diabetes risk
    - Gigantism: GH excess before epiphyseal closure
    - Growth hormone deficiency: Short stature, developmental delay

VI.E - Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) Axis
  • Hypothalamus: GnRH (Gonadotropin-releasing hormone) pulsatile secretion
  • Anterior Pituitary: Gonadotrophs secrete FSH and LH
  • Ovaries:
    - Follicular phase: FSH stimulates follicle growth
      * Estrogen production by granulosa and theca cells
    - Ovulation: LH surge triggers ovulation
    - Luteal phase: LH stimulates corpus luteum
      * Progesterone and estrogen production
  • Testes:
    - FSH: Sertoli cell function, spermatogenesis
    - LH: Leydig cell testosterone production
  • Sex hormone functions:
    - Testosterone: Male sexual development, muscle/bone growth, libido
    - Estrogen: Female sexual development, bone health, lipid metabolism
    - Progesterone: Menstrual cycle regulation, pregnancy maintenance
  • Negative feedback:
    - Estrogen: Biphasic (negative at low, positive at high near ovulation)
    - Testosterone: Negative feedback on GnRH and LH
    - Progesterone: Negative feedback
  • Menstrual cycle: Follicular (0-14d), Ovulation (d14), Luteal (14-28d)
  • Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS):
    - Insulin resistance, Androgen excess, Anovulation
    - Irregular menses, Hirsutism, Infertility, Metabolic dysfunction

VI.F - Posterior Pituitary Hormones
  • Neurohypophysis: Axonal projections from hypothalamus
  • Arginine Vasopressin (AVP, ADH - Antidiuretic hormone):
    - Hypothalamus: Supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei synthesis
    - Functions: Water reabsorption in kidney collecting duct, Vasoconstriction
    - Regulation: Plasma osmolality (osmoreceptors), Blood pressure (baroreceptors)
    - Disorders:
      * Diabetes Insipidus (central): AVP deficiency
        - Polyuria, Polydipsia, Hypernatremia
        - Treatment: Desmopressin (synthetic AVP)
      * SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH): AVP excess
        - Hyponatremia, Volume expansion
        - Causes: CNS disease, Malignancy, Drugs, Pulmonary disease
  • Oxytocin:
    - Functions: Uterine contraction (labor), Milk letdown reflex, Bonding
    - Regulation: Neuroendocrine reflex, Afferent input from breast/uterus

VI.G - Adrenal Medulla
  • Chromaffin cells: Catecholamine production
  • Epinephrine and Norepinephrine:
    - Synthesis: Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine
    - Phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT): Final step
    - Release: Sympathetic stimulation (acetylcholine nicotinic receptors)
  • Epinephrine functions: Fight-or-flight response
    - Increased HR, Contractility, BP
    - Bronchodilation
    - Glucose mobilization (glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis)
    - Inhibition of insulin, Stimulation of glucagon
  • Adrenergic receptors:
    - α1: Vasoconstriction, Pupil dilation, Bladder contraction
    - α2: Inhibition of norepinephrine release, Blood vessel constriction
    - β1: Cardiac stimulation
    - β2: Bronchodilation, Vasodilation, Glycogenolysis
    - β3: Lipolysis
  • Pheochromocytoma: Catecholamine-secreting tumor
    - Severe hypertension, Palpitations, Sweating, Tremor, Headache
    - Elevated plasma and urine metanephrines

VI.H - Adrenal Cortex
  • Three zones:
    - Zona glomerulosa: Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid)
    - Zona fasciculata: Cortisol (glucocorticoid)
    - Zona reticularis: Androgen precursors (DHEA, DHEA-S)
  • Aldosterone:
    - Regulation: Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)
      * Juxtaglomerular cells: Renin secretion (low Na+, low BP, β-agonists)
      * Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I (Renin)
      * Angiotensin II: ACE conversion, Aldosterone stimulation
    - Functions: Na+ reabsorption, K+ secretion (distal tubule/collecting duct)
      * Maintains blood volume and blood pressure
    - Negative feedback: High Na+, High K+ → Reduced renin/RAAS
  • Cortisol (covered in HPA axis section)
  • Adrenal insufficiency:
    - Primary (Addison's disease): Adrenal destruction
      * Loss of cortisol and aldosterone
      * Hypotension, Hyponatremia, Hyperkalemia
      * Skin hyperpigmentation (ACTH elevation)
    - Secondary: Pituitary ACTH deficiency
      * Loss of cortisol only (aldosterone preserved)
  • Cushing's Syndrome:
    - Cortisol excess
    - Central (Cushing's disease): Pituitary adenoma secreting ACTH
    - Adrenal adenoma: Primary adrenal cortisol secretion
    - Ectopic: Non-pituitary ACTH source
    - Iatrogenic: Pharmacological glucocorticoid therapy
    - Manifestations: Central obesity, Hirsutism, Purple striae, HTN, DM, Osteoporosis

VI.I - Pancreatic Islet Hormones
  • Beta cells: Insulin production and secretion
    - Glucose-stimulated secretion (main stimulus)
    - GSIS (Glucose-stimulated insulin secretion):
      * Glucose uptake (GLUT2)
      * Glycolysis ATP production
      * ATP-sensitive K+ channel closure
      * Depolarization, Ca2+ influx
      * Exocytosis
    - Additional stimuli: Amino acids, Fatty acids, GLP-1
  • Insulin functions:
    - Glucose uptake (GLUT4 translocation in muscle/adipose)
    - Glycogen synthesis (glycogenesis)
    - Lipid synthesis (lipogenesis)
    - Protein synthesis
    - Inhibition of gluconeogenesis, Lipolysis, Glycogenolysis
  • Alpha cells: Glucagon production and secretion
    - Low glucose, Amino acids, Sympathetic stimulation
  • Glucagon functions:
    - Glycogenolysis (liver glycogen breakdown)
    - Gluconeogenesis (new glucose production)
    - Lipolysis
    - Ketogenesis (in fasting)
  • Somatostatin (Delta cells): Paracrine inhibition of insulin and glucagon
  • Pancreatic Polypeptide (PP cells): Digestive enzyme secretion inhibition
  • Diabetes Mellitus:
    - Type 1: Autoimmune β-cell destruction
      * Absolute insulin deficiency
      * Hyperglycemia, Polyuria, Polydipsia, Weight loss
      * DKA risk (Diabetic Ketoacidosis): Ketone production, Acidosis
    - Type 2: Insulin resistance and relative deficiency
      * Combination of decreased insulin secretion and tissue resistance
      * Often associated with obesity, metabolic syndrome
    - Gestational: Pregnancy-induced glucose intolerance
    - Secondary: Pancreatitis, Cystic fibrosis, Hemochromatosis, Cushing's
  • Hypoglycemia:
    - Symptoms: Tremor, Tachycardia, Anxiety (adrenergic)
               Confusion, Altered consciousness (CNS glucose deprivation)
    - Counter-regulatory hormones: Glucagon, Epinephrine, Cortisol, GH
  • Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State (HHS):
    - Severe hyperglycemia, Dehydration, Altered consciousness
    - Type 2 diabetes complication

VI.J - Thyroid Hormones (extended)
  • T4 and T3: Biological roles
    - Metabolic rate control
    - Thermoregulation
    - Cardiovascular function
    - Bone metabolism
    - Lipid and glucose metabolism
  • Peripheral conversion: T4 (circulating) → T3 (active)
    - Deiodinase enzymes
    - Conversion affected by: Stress, Illness, Drugs, Nutrition
  • Thyroid hormone metabolism:
    - Conjugation in liver (sulfation, glucuronidation)
    - Enterohepatic circulation
    - Renal excretion

VI.K - Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) and Calcium Regulation
  • Parathyroid glands: Four small glands on thyroid posterior
  • PTH synthesis and secretion:
    - Stimulus: Low serum Ca2+ (via Ca2+-sensing receptor)
    - Inhibition: High serum Ca2+, High serum PO43-
  • PTH functions:
    - Bone: Osteoclast activation → Calcium release
    - Kidney:
      * ↑ Ca2+ reabsorption (distal tubule)
      * ↓ PO43- reabsorption (proximal tubule)
      * ↑ 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D3 synthesis (1α-hydroxylase)
    - Intestine: ↑ Ca2+ absorption (via vitamin D)
  • Calcitonin (thyroid C cells):
    - Stimulus: High serum Ca2+
    - Functions: ↓ Bone resorption, ↑ Renal Ca2+ excretion
    - Physiological role: Minor in humans
  • Vitamin D (Calcitriol):
    - Synthesis: Skin (7-dehydrocholesterol → Vitamin D2/D3)
               Liver (25-hydroxylation)
               Kidney (1α-hydroxylation, PTH-stimulated)
    - Functions: ↑ Intestinal Ca2+ absorption
               ↑ Intestinal PO43- absorption
               Bone mineralization
               Cell differentiation
               Immune function
  • Calcium homeostasis:
    - Normal serum Ca2+: 8.5-10.5 mg/dL
    - PTH, Vitamin D: Main regulators
    - Hypercalcemia: PTH suppression, Calcitonin stimulation
    - Hypocalcemia: PTH stimulation, Vitamin D activation
  • Hyperparathyroidism: PTH excess
    - Primary: Parathyroid adenoma/hyperplasia
    - Secondary: Kidney disease (phosphate retention, Ca2+ loss)
    - Tertiary: Autonomous PTH secretion after prolonged secondary hyperparathyroidism
    - Manifestations: Hypercalcemia, Kidney stones, Osteoporosis, "Stones, Bones, Groans"
  • Hypoparathyroidism: PTH deficiency
    - Hypocalcemia, Hyperphosphatemia
    - DiGeorge syndrome: Parathyroid hypoplasia

VI.L - Other Endocrine Hormones
  • Leptin:
    - Adipose tissue hormone
    - Satiety signal
    - Energy expenditure regulation
    - Immune function
  • Ghrelin:
    - Stomach hormone
    - Hunger signal
    - Growth hormone stimulation
  • GLP-1 (Glucagon-like Peptide-1):
    - Incretin hormone (stimulates insulin in response to glucose)
    - Delayed gastric emptying
    - Satiety
  • Melatonin:
    - Pineal gland production
    - Circadian rhythm regulation
    - Sleep induction
    - Antioxidant effects
    - Seasonal affective disorder
  • Prolactin:
    - Anterior pituitary hormone
    - Milk production
    - TRH stimulation
    - Dopamine inhibition
  • Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH):
    - 21-hydroxylase deficiency (most common, 90%)
    - Cortisol synthesis impairment → ↑ ACTH → ↑ Androgen precursors
    - Salt-wasting form: Aldosterone deficiency, Hyponatremia, Shock
    - Simple virilizing: Virilization without salt-wasting
    - Non-classic: Mild androgen excess
  • Hyperprolactinemia:
    - Lactation, Amenorrhea, Sexual dysfunction
    - Dopamine antagonists (risperidone, metoclopramide)
    - Pituitary adenoma
    - Hypothyroidism (TRH stimulation)

VI.M - Endocrine Disruptors
  • Bisphenol A (BPA): Plastics, Can linings
    - Estrogen receptor agonist
    - Developmental effects, Metabolic dysfunction
  • DDT and Pesticides: Persistent organic pollutants
    - Estrogenic and androgenic effects
    - Bioaccumulation
  • Perfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS): Nonstick coatings
    - Thyroid hormone disruption
    - Immune suppression
  • Phthalates: Plasticizers
    - Anti-androgenic effects
    - Developmental toxicity
  • Dioxins: Industrial byproducts
    - Aryl hydrocarbon receptor activation
    - Multiple endocrine disruptions

=== SECTION I: REMOTE SENSING FUNDAMENTALS ===

I.A - Active vs. Passive Sensors
  • Active sensors:
    - Emit their own energy and measure reflections
    - Examples: Radar, LIDAR, Sonar
    - Advantages: Can operate day/night, control signal properties
    - Disadvantages: Require power, more complex systems
  • Passive sensors:
    - Detect naturally emitted or reflected energy
    - Examples: Radiometers, Visible cameras, Infrared cameras, Spectrometers
    - Advantages: Simpler, no power needed for emission
    - Disadvantages: Dependent on external energy sources (e.g., sunlight)
  • Key distinction: Active sensors generate their own signals; passive sensors detect natural radiation

I.B - Electromagnetic Spectrum
  • Regions used in remote sensing:
    - Radio waves: Longest wavelength, lowest frequency
    - Microwaves: Used in radar, ATMS, SMAP
    - Infrared: Thermal imaging, temperature measurement
      - Near-infrared (NIR): 0.7-1.3 μm
      - Shortwave infrared (SWIR): 1.3-3 μm
      - Mid-wave infrared (MWIR): 3-5 μm
      - Long-wave infrared (LWIR): 8-14 μm
    - Visible: 0.4-0.7 μm (blue to red)
    - Ultraviolet: Shorter wavelengths, higher energy
  • Wavelength-frequency relationship: c = λν (c = speed of light)
  • Energy-wavelength relationship: E = hc/λ (h = Planck's constant)

I.C - Blackbody Radiation
  • Blackbody: Idealized object that absorbs all incident radiation
  • Planck's Law: Describes spectral radiance of blackbody
    - More accurate than Rayleigh-Jeans Law across all wavelengths
    - Rayleigh-Jeans Law accurate for long wavelengths only
  • Wien's Displacement Law:
    - λ_max = b/T (b = Wien's constant ≈ 2898 μm·K)
    - Peak wavelength inversely proportional to temperature
    - Hotter objects emit at shorter wavelengths
  • Stefan-Boltzmann Law:
    - Total energy radiated ∝ T⁴
    - L = 4πR²σT⁴ (L = luminosity, R = radius, σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant)
    - If temperature triples, brightness increases by 81× (3⁴)
  • Emissivity (ε): Ratio of actual emission to blackbody emission
    - Blackbody: ε = 1
    - Real objects: 0 < ε < 1
    - Ocean surface: ~0.98-0.99 (near-blackbody)
  • Reflectivity + Emissivity + Transmissivity = 1 (for opaque objects, transmissivity = 0)

I.D - Scattering Mechanisms
  • Rayleigh Scattering:
    - Occurs when wavelength >> particle size
    - Scattering ∝ 1/λ⁴
    - Responsible for blue sky color
    - Dominant for small particles (molecules)
  • Mie Scattering:
    - Occurs when wavelength ≈ particle size
    - Less wavelength-dependent than Rayleigh
    - Important for aerosols, cloud droplets
  • Non-selective Scattering:
    - Occurs when wavelength << particle size
    - All wavelengths scattered equally
    - Causes white appearance of clouds
  • Brillouin Scattering: Inelastic scattering from acoustic waves
  • Raman Scattering: Inelastic scattering with energy transfer

I.E - Reflection, Refraction, and Absorption
  • Reflection: Light bounces off smooth surface (specular) or rough surface (diffuse)
  • Refraction: Light bends when passing between media with different refractive indices
    - Snell's Law: n₁sin(θ₁) = n₂sin(θ₂)
    - Light bends toward normal when entering higher refractive index medium
  • Absorption: Energy converted to heat or other forms
  • Attenuation: Combined effect of scattering and absorption
  • Brewster's Angle: Angle at which unpolarized light becomes fully polarized upon reflection
    - tan(θ_B) = n₂/n₁
    - Reflected and refracted rays are perpendicular at Brewster's angle

=== SECTION II: SATELLITE SYSTEMS AND ORBITS ===

II.A - Orbital Types
  • Low Earth Orbit (LEO):
    - Altitude: 160-2000 km
    - Advantages: High spatial resolution, low latency
    - Disadvantages: Atmospheric drag, short lifetime, frequent revisits needed
    - Examples: MODIS, Landsat, ICESat-2
  • Geostationary Orbit:
    - Altitude: ~36,000 km above equator
    - Period: 24 hours (matches Earth's rotation)
    - Appears fixed over same location
    - Advantages: Continuous monitoring, weather forecasting
    - Examples: GOES satellites
  • Geosynchronous Orbit:
    - Same period as geostationary but not necessarily equatorial
    - Can have inclination, appears to oscillate north-south
  • Sun-Synchronous Orbit (SSO):
    - Always passes over equator at same local solar time
    - Ensures consistent lighting conditions
    - Typical altitude: 600-800 km
    - Examples: MODIS (Terra/Aqua), Landsat
  • Polar Orbit:
    - Passes over poles
    - Allows global coverage over time due to Earth's rotation
    - Often sun-synchronous

II.B - Orbital Mechanics
  • Kepler's Laws:
    - First Law: Orbits are elliptical with Sun/planet at one focus
    - Second Law: Equal areas swept in equal times (conservation of angular momentum)
    - Third Law: T² ∝ a³ (T = period, a = semi-major axis)
      - For circular orbits: T² = (4π²/GM) × a³
      - Derived from equating gravitational and centrifugal forces
  • Orbital Velocity:
    - v = √(GM/r) for circular orbits
    - Decreases with altitude
    - Lower orbits have higher velocities
  • Orbital Period:
    - T = 2π√(a³/GM)
    - Longer period for higher altitude
  • Atmospheric Drag:
    - Causes orbital decay
    - Lower orbits experience more drag
    - Velocity increases as orbit decays (counterintuitive but true)

II.C - Satellite Instruments and Missions
  • MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer):
    - Onboard Terra and Aqua satellites
    - Sun-synchronous orbit
    - Spatial resolutions: 250 m, 500 m, 1 km
    - Bands 1-19, 26: Reflected sunlight (daytime)
    - Bands 20-25, 27-36: Thermal emission (day/night)
    - Applications: Cloud detection, SST, vegetation, aerosols
  • GOES-R ABI (Advanced Baseline Imager):
    - Geostationary orbit
    - 16 spectral bands
    - Bands 1-8: Visible/NIR
    - Bands 9-16: Infrared
    - Band 2: Aerosol and dust detection
    - Flex-mode scanning for hemispheric and regional coverage
  • Landsat OLI (Operational Land Imager):
    - Bands: Visible, NIR, SWIR
    - Panchromatic band (Band 8): Improves spatial resolution via pan-sharpening
    - 30 m resolution (typical)
  • ATMS (Advanced Technology Microwave Sounder):
    - Passive microwave radiometer
    - 22 channels
    - Channels 1-16: Atmospheric temperature profiling
    - Channels 17-22: Tropospheric water vapor
    - Not impeded by cloud cover
  • OCO-2 (Orbiting Carbon Observatory-2):
    - Measures column-averaged CO₂ (XCO₂)
    - Uses spectroscopy to detect CO₂ absorption
  • GRACE/GRACE-FO (Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment):
    - Twin satellites measuring distance between them
    - Tracks gravitational variations (mass changes)
    - Applications: Ice mass loss, sea level rise, groundwater
    - SST-LL (Satellite-to-Satellite Low-Low) tracking mode
    - Laser interferometry for nanometer accuracy (GRACE-FO)
  • ICESat-2:
    - LIDAR altimeter
    - Measures ice sheet elevation changes
  • SMAP (Soil Moisture Active Passive):
    - Measures soil moisture via microwave radiometry
  • CALIPSO/CALIOP:
    - LIDAR system
    - Vertical profiles of aerosols and clouds
    - ~15 m resolution
  • CloudSat:
    - Radar system
    - Cloud and precipitation particles
    - 240 m range resolution

=== SECTION III: RADAR AND LIDAR SYSTEMS ===

III.A - Radar Fundamentals
  • Radar (Radio Detection and Ranging):
    - Active sensor using microwave frequencies
    - Measures return time of transmitted pulses
    - Range resolution: ρ = cτ/2 (c = speed of light, τ = pulse duration)
    - Signal bandwidth: B = 1/τ
  • Doppler Radar:
    - Measures velocity of precipitation particles
    - Uses frequency shift (Doppler effect)
    - Applications: Meteorology, storm tracking
  • Radar Altimetry:
    - Measures surface height (especially ocean)
    - Sea surface altimetry missions: TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason-3, Sentinel-6
    - Return waveform shape indicates surface roughness
    - Smooth ocean: Sharp intensity increase
    - Choppy ocean: Smoother, broader waveform
  • Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR):
    - Superior azimuth resolution compared to traditional radar
    - Uses motion of platform to synthesize large aperture
    - Can penetrate clouds (microwave)
    - Applications: Oceanography, terrain mapping

III.B - Polarimetric SAR
  • Polarization modes:
    - VV: Vertical transmit, vertical receive
    - HH: Horizontal transmit, horizontal receive
    - HV: Horizontal transmit, vertical receive
    - VH: Vertical transmit, horizontal receive
  • Quad-pol systems: Produce all four polarization combinations
  • Dual-pol systems: Typically HH/HV or VV/VH
  • Scattering mechanisms:
    - Surface/specular scattering: Smooth surfaces (water) → co-polarized (VV/HH)
    - Double-bounce: Urban areas, buildings → co-polarized
    - Volume scattering: Vegetation → cross-polarized (HV/VH)
    - Triple-bounce: Complex structures
    - Helix scattering: Urban areas with tall buildings
  • Backscatter coefficient (β⁰):
    - Influenced by: Terrain features, radar echo power, incidence angle

III.C - LIDAR Systems
  • LIDAR (Light Detection and Ranging):
    - Active sensor using laser pulses
    - Measures return time to determine distance
    - Applications: Topography, biomass measurement, atmospheric profiling
  • Coordinate Systems and Rotation Matrices:
    - Roll (ω), Pitch (φ), Yaw (κ): Rotation about x, y, z axes
    - Used to transform between ground and sensor coordinate systems
    - Right-hand rule determines positive rotation direction
  • Ground Control Points vs. Direct Georeferencing:
    - Indirect georeferencing (GCPs): Higher accuracy, more field time
    - Direct georeferencing (GNSS/INS): Less time, lower accuracy
  • Applications:
    - Standing biomass measurement in forests
    - Elevation mapping
    - 3D terrain reconstruction

=== SECTION IV: IMAGE INTERPRETATION AND PROCESSING ===

IV.A - Spatial Resolution Concepts
  • Ground Sample Distance (GSD):
    - Size of single pixel on ground (e.g., 10 m × 10 m)
    - Also called spatial resolution
  • Instantaneous Field of View (IFOV):
    - Angular size of single detector element
    - IFOV = pixel pitch / focal length
  • Field of View (FOV):
    - Total angular coverage of sensor
    - FOV = swath width / altitude
  • Swath Width:
    - Total geographic area visible in single scan
  • Resolution vs. Coverage Trade-off:
    - Higher resolution → smaller swath
    - Lower resolution → larger swath
  • Typical resolutions:
    - 30 m: Common for many instruments (Landsat)
    - 250-1000 m: MODIS
    - Sub-meter: High-resolution commercial satellites

IV.B - Image Characteristics
  • Radiometric Resolution:
    - Number of bits per pixel
    - Determines number of gray levels
    - Higher bits = finer intensity discrimination
  • Spectral Resolution:
    - Number and width of spectral bands
    - Multispectral: Few bands (4-10)
    - Hyperspectral: Many narrow bands (100+)
  • Temporal Resolution:
    - Revisit period: Time between observations of same location
    - Geostationary: Continuous
    - LEO: Days to weeks
  • Image Scale:
    - Ratio of map/photo distance to ground distance
    - Example: 1:15,000 means 1 unit on photo = 15,000 units on ground
    - Scale varies with elevation in aerial photography

IV.C - Image Distortions and Corrections
  • Relief Displacement:
    - Objects away from nadir appear to lean away
    - Objects at nadir show only tops
    - Height can be calculated using parallax measurements
  • Bowtie Artifact:
    - Occurs at edges of images
    - Caused by varying ground sampling distance with scan angle
  • Geometric Distortions:
    - Perspective projection vs. orthographic projection
    - Maps use orthographic (no perspective)
    - Photos use perspective (distortion present)
  • Atmospheric Corrections:
    - Account for scattering and absorption
    - Important for accurate reflectance measurements

IV.D - False Color Composites (FCC)
  • Purpose: Enhance visualization of features
  • Common combinations:
    - RGB = NIR, Red, Green: Vegetation appears red
    - RGB = SWIR, NIR, Red: Burned areas visible
  • True Color: RGB = Red, Green, Blue (natural colors)
  • False Color: Non-natural color assignments

IV.E - Vegetation Indices
  • NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index):
    - NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red)
    - Range: -1 to +1
    - High values: Healthy, dense vegetation
    - Low values: Sparse vegetation, water, urban
    - Applications: Vegetation health monitoring, biomass estimation
    - Seasonal variations: Higher NDVI at high latitudes due to longer growing seasons
  • NDWI (Normalized Difference Water Index):
    - NDWI = (Green - NIR) / (Green + NIR)
    - Better for water detection than NDVI
    - Accounts for water absorption in NIR
  • Vegetation Reflectance:
    - High reflectance in NIR (strong scattering by cell structure)
    - Low reflectance in visible red (chlorophyll absorption)
    - Low reflectance in blue (400-500 nm) indicates high organic matter content

=== SECTION V: CLIMATE PROCESSES AND ATMOSPHERIC REMOTE SENSING ===

V.A - Greenhouse Gases and Radiation
  • Major Greenhouse Gases:
    - Water vapor (H₂O): Most abundant, ~50% of greenhouse effect
      - Positive feedback: Warming → more evaporation → more H₂O → more warming
    - Carbon dioxide (CO₂): Long atmospheric lifetime
    - Methane (CH₄): High GWP but shorter lifetime
    - Nitrous oxide (N₂O): Long lifetime, from fertilizers, animal production
    - Sulfur hexafluoride (SF₆): Most potent (23,500× CO₂), from power appliances
    - Ozone (O₃): Tropospheric warming, stratospheric cooling
  • Greenhouse Effect Mechanism:
    - Gases absorb infrared radiation
    - Re-emit in all directions (including back to surface)
    - Traps heat in lower atmosphere
  • Atmospheric Layers:
    - Troposphere: Contains most greenhouse gases, weather occurs here
    - Stratosphere: Contains ozone layer
    - Modern climate change: Warming in lower atmosphere, cooling in upper atmosphere
  • Global Warming Potential (GWP):
    - Measured relative to CO₂
    - Accounts for both radiative efficiency and atmospheric lifetime

V.B - Climate Feedbacks
  • Positive Feedbacks (amplify warming):
    - Ice-albedo feedback: Ice melt → lower albedo → more absorption → warming
    - Water vapor feedback: Warming → more H₂O → more warming
    - Cloud feedback (high-altitude cirrus): Trap longwave radiation
  • Negative Feedbacks (dampen warming):
    - Increased clouds → more reflection → cooling
    - Increased aerosols → more scattering → cooling
  • Albedo:
    - Fraction of incident radiation reflected
    - High albedo: Ice, snow, clouds (~0.8-0.9)
    - Low albedo: Ocean, forests (~0.1-0.2)
    - Earth's average albedo: ~0.3
    - Surface albedo ~4%, but combined with atmosphere ~30% (due to clouds)

V.C - Energy Balance and Equilibrium Temperature
  • Solar Constant (S):
    - Solar energy flux at top of atmosphere
    - ~1366 W/m² (varies slightly)
    - S = L_sun / (4πD²) where D = Earth-Sun distance
  • Equilibrium Temperature:
    - For blackbody: T_eq = [S(1-A)/(4σ)]^(1/4)
    - A = albedo, σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant
    - Earth: T_eq ≈ 255 K (without atmosphere)
    - Actual surface temperature: ~288 K (33 K warmer due to greenhouse effect)
  • Radiative Forcing:
    - Change in energy balance due to external factors
    - Positive forcing: Warming
    - Negative forcing: Cooling
  • Simple Greenhouse Model:
    - Two-layer model: Surface and atmosphere
    - Atmosphere absorbs and re-emits infrared
    - Surface temperature > equilibrium temperature

V.D - Aerosols and Their Effects
  • Aerosol Sources:
    - Volcanic eruptions: Sulfur dioxide (SO₂) → sulfate aerosols
    - Wildfires: Black carbon, organic carbon
    - Industrial: Sulfate, nitrate aerosols
    - Agricultural burning: Slash-and-burn agriculture
  • Aerosol Effects:
    - Direct: Scatter/absorb sunlight (cooling or warming depending on type)
    - Indirect: Act as cloud condensation nuclei
      - More aerosols → more, smaller cloud droplets → brighter clouds → cooling
    - Black carbon: Absorbs sunlight, reduces albedo of ice
  • Aerosol Properties:
    - Extinction coefficient: Measures light attenuation
    - Angstrom exponent: Indicates particle size
      - High values: Small particles
      - Low values: Large particles
    - Seasonal variations: High Angstrom exponent in summer (small particles from biogenic sources)
  • Volcanic Aerosols:
    - Typically cooling effect (sulfate aerosols reflect sunlight)
    - Can persist in stratosphere for years
    - Tonga 2022: Released water vapor → enhanced aerosol growth
  • Monitoring:
    - OMPS-LP (Ozone Mapping and Profiling Suite Limb Profiler)
    - Measures extinction at multiple wavelengths (510, 600, 675, 745, 869, 997 nm)
    - Wavelengths in visible/NIR range

V.E - Carbon Cycle and CO₂ Monitoring
  • Carbon Sources:
    - Fossil fuel combustion
    - Deforestation
    - Soil respiration
  • Carbon Sinks:
    - Ocean absorption
    - Vegetation (photosynthesis)
    - Soil storage
  • Seasonal CO₂ Variations:
    - Northern Hemisphere summer: CO₂ decreases (vegetation uptake)
    - Northern Hemisphere winter: CO₂ increases (reduced photosynthesis, heating)
    - Mauna Loa measurements: Famous Keeling Curve
    - Mauna Loa ideal: High altitude, remote from local sources, representative of global atmosphere
  • XCO₂ (Column-Averaged CO₂):
    - Measured by OCO-2
    - Represents tropospheric average dry-air mole fraction
    - Seasonal patterns visible in satellite data
  • Carbon Cycle Disruption:
    - Anthropogenic nitrogen deposition: Can increase N₂O emissions, affect carbon storage
    - Deforestation: Reduces carbon sinks, increases CO₂

V.F - Nitrogen Cycle
  • Key Processes:
    - Nitrogen fixation: N₂ → NH₃ (by diazotrophs)
    - Nitrification: NH₃ → NO₃⁻
    - Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (by heterotrophs)
    - Haber-Bosch process: Industrial N fixation (major human impact)
  • Human Impacts:
    - Fertilizer use increases N₂O emissions
    - Nitrogen deposition affects ecosystems
    - Can alter carbon cycling

=== SECTION VI: OCEANIC AND CLIMATE PHENOMENA ===

VI.A - Sea Surface Temperature (SST)
  • Measurement:
    - MODIS, GOES, other thermal sensors
    - Uses thermal infrared wavelengths
    - Stefan-Boltzmann law: Can estimate temperature from radiance
  • Applications:
    - Hurricane formation: Requires SST > 26.5°C
    - Ocean warming trends: Indicator of climate change
    - ENSO monitoring
  • Data Issues:
    - Cloud cover can obscure measurements
    - Polar regions: Less data due to ice cover
    - Seasonal variations

VI.B - ENSO (El Niño-Southern Oscillation)
  • El Niño:
    - Peaks in winter (Northern Hemisphere)
    - Warm waters off coast of Mexico/South America
    - Cold waters on other side of Pacific
    - Effects: Warmer, drier winters in some regions (e.g., Michigan)
    - Reduces upwelling → less nutrients → ecosystem disruption
  • La Niña:
    - Opposite pattern
    - Cold waters off South America
    - Stronger trade winds
  • Monitoring:
    - Sea surface temperature anomalies
    - Central equatorial Pacific is critical region
  • Teleconnections:
    - ENSO affects weather globally through atmospheric connections
    - Not just local to Pacific

VI.C - Hurricane/Tropical Cyclone Monitoring
  • Formation Requirements:
    - SST > 26.5°C
    - Low wind shear
    - Sufficient Coriolis force (not at equator)
  • Monitoring Tools:
    - ATMS: Temperature and water vapor profiles
    - GOES: Visible and infrared imagery
    - Doppler radar: Precipitation velocity
  • Structure:
    - Eye: Low pressure, clear skies
    - Eyewall: Maximum winds, heavy precipitation
    - Radius of maximum winds: Distance from center to strongest winds
  • Temperature Anomalies:
    - Cold anomalies indicate heavy rain and strong winds
    - Greatest deviation often at specific pressure levels (altitudes)

VI.D - Ocean Altimetry
  • Purpose: Measure sea surface height
  • Applications:
    - Sea level rise detection
    - Ocean circulation patterns
    - Tidal variations
  • Missions: TOPEX/Poseidon, Jason series, Sentinel-6
  • Dual-frequency (Ku-band and C-band):
    - Ku-band: Primary measurement
    - C-band: Corrects for ionospheric delay
  • Return Waveform Analysis:
    - Smooth ocean: Sharp leading edge
    - Rough ocean: Broader, smoother waveform
    - Indicates significant wave height

VI.E - Ice Monitoring
  • Sea Ice:
    - Arctic and Antarctic
    - Declining trends (especially Arctic)
    - Monitored via microwave sensors (penetrate clouds)
  • Land Ice:
    - Ice sheets (Greenland, Antarctica)
    - Glaciers
    - GRACE: Measures mass changes via gravity
    - ICESat-2: Measures elevation changes
  • Climate Indicators:
    - Ice loss contributes to sea level rise
    - Albedo feedback (ice-albedo feedback)

=== SECTION VII: ATMOSPHERIC REMOTE SENSING ===

VII.A - Cloud Detection and Properties
  • Cloud Types:
    - Cirrus: High-altitude, thin, ice crystals
    - Cumulus: Puffy, low to mid-level
    - Stratus: Layered, low-level
  • Detection Methods:
    - Visible: High reflectance
    - Infrared: Cold temperatures (high altitude)
    - Thermal infrared: Distinguishes cloud top temperature
  • Cloud Properties:
    - Optical thickness: How much light blocked
    - Droplet size: Affects brightness
    - MODIS bands: 0.47 μm and 1.24 μm useful for cloud properties
  • Cloud Effects on Climate:
    - Low clouds: Cooling (reflect sunlight)
    - High clouds: Warming (trap infrared)
    - Net effect depends on type and altitude

VII.B - Water Vapor Profiling
  • Measurement:
    - ATMS channels 17-22: Tropospheric water vapor
    - Microwave sensors: Can penetrate clouds
  • Applications:
    - Weather forecasting
    - Climate monitoring
    - Understanding atmospheric dynamics

VII.C - Ozone Monitoring
  • Ozone Layer:
    - Stratospheric ozone: Protects from UV
    - Tropospheric ozone: Pollutant, greenhouse gas
  • Measurement:
    - OMPS: Ozone Mapping and Profiling Suite
    - Limb profiling: Vertical distribution
  • Ozone Depletion:
    - CFCs react with ozone: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂
    - Immediate products: ClO and O₂
    - Montreal Protocol: Phased out CFCs

=== SECTION VIII: APPLICATIONS AND CASE STUDIES ===

VIII.A - Biomass and Carbon Monitoring
  • Standing Biomass:
    - Total mass of living vegetation
    - Important for carbon storage estimates
    - Current measurements may underestimate (don't account for all carbon pools)
  • Measurement Methods:
    - LIDAR: Direct 3D structure measurement
      - Advantages: Accurate height, volume estimation
      - Disadvantages: Expensive, limited coverage
    - NDVI: Indirect vegetation index
      - Advantages: Wide coverage, frequent revisits
      - Disadvantages: Saturation at high biomass, affected by soil background
  • Carbon Sources vs. Sinks:
    - Sources: Release CO₂ (deforestation, fires)
    - Sinks: Absorb CO₂ (forests, oceans)
  • Forest Degradation:
    - Can be detected via time series analysis
    - Both LIDAR and NDVI useful when used together

VIII.B - Wildfire Monitoring
  • Detection:
    - MODIS: Hot spot detection
    - Visible: Smoke plumes
    - Thermal infrared: Active fires
  • Effects:
    - Smoke aerosols: Can brighten clouds, absorb sunlight
    - Carbon emissions
    - Ecosystem impacts
  • Post-fire Analysis:
    - False color composites show burned areas
    - NDVI decreases after fire
    - Recovery monitoring over time

VIII.C - Urban Heat Islands
  • Phenomenon:
    - Cities warmer than surrounding rural areas
    - Visible in thermal imagery
    - Red colors indicate higher temperatures
  • Causes:
    - Reduced vegetation
    - Impervious surfaces (asphalt, concrete)
    - Waste heat from human activities
  • Monitoring:
    - Thermal infrared sensors
    - ECOSTRESS: Measures land surface temperature
    - Day/night temperature differences

VIII.D - Water Quality Monitoring
  • Parameters:
    - Chlorophyll concentration: Indicates productivity
    - Turbidity: Suspended sediments
    - Water temperature
  • Reflectance Spectra:
    - Clear water: Low reflectance, high absorption
    - Turbid water: Higher reflectance
    - Algal blooms: Characteristic spectral signatures
  • NDWI vs. NDVI:
    - NDWI better for water detection
    - Accounts for water's strong NIR absorption
  • Applications:
    - Harmful algal bloom detection
    - Eutrophication monitoring
    - Coastal water quality

=== SECTION IX: MATHEMATICAL RELATIONSHIPS ===

IX.A - Orbital Calculations
  • Orbital Period:
    - T = 2π√(a³/GM)
    - For Earth: T² = (4π²/GM_earth) × a³
    - If period doubles, radius increases by ∛4 (cube root of 4)
  • Orbital Velocity:
    - v = √(GM/r)
    - Lower altitude = higher velocity
  • Geostationary Altitude:
    - ~36,000 km above equator
    - Period = 24 hours

IX.B - Distance and Resolution Calculations
  • Ground Sample Distance:
    - GSD = (pixel pitch × altitude) / focal length
    - For diffraction-limited system: GSD ≈ (λ × altitude) / (aperture diameter)
  • IFOV:
    - IFOV = pixel pitch / focal length (in radians)
    - Convert to degrees: multiply by 180/π
  • Photo Scale:
    - Scale = focal length / (flying height - elevation)
    - Varies with terrain elevation
  • Stereoscopic Parallax:
    - Height = (B × f) / (p + f)
    - B = air base, f = focal length, p = parallax

IX.C - Radiative Transfer
  • Distance Modulus:
    - m - M = 5log₁₀(d) - 5
    - d in parsecs
    - m = apparent magnitude, M = absolute magnitude
  • Solar Constant Calculation:
    - S = L_sun / (4πD²)
    - L_sun = 3.828 × 10²⁶ W (or calculate from Stefan-Boltzmann)
  • Equilibrium Temperature:
    - T = [S(1-A)/(4σ)]^(1/4)
    - For planet with atmosphere, more complex

IX.D - Signal Processing
  • Sampling Rate:
    - For radar: Must sample fast enough to achieve desired resolution
    - Range resolution determines required pulse duration
  • Bandwidth:
    - B = 1/τ (for pulse duration τ)
    - Higher bandwidth = better resolution

=== SECTION I: STELLAR CLASSIFICATION ===

I.A - Spectral Classification Systems
  • Harvard System (Draper System):
    - O, B, A, F, G, K, M (hottest to coolest)
    - Mnemonic: "Oh Be A Fine Girl/Guy, Kiss Me"
    - Based on absorption line strengths
    - O stars: Hottest, ionized helium lines
    - M stars: Coolest, molecular bands (TiO)
  • Morgan-Keenan System:
    - Adds luminosity classes (Roman numerals)
    - Example: G2V (Sun)
    - Combines spectral type with luminosity
  • Secchi Classification:
    - Early system, less precise
    - Based on visual appearance
  • Luminosity Classes:
    - I: Supergiants
    - II: Bright giants
    - III: Giants
    - IV: Subgiants
    - V: Main sequence (dwarfs)
    - VI: Subdwarfs (rarely used)
    - VII: White dwarfs (sometimes used)
  • Sun Classification:
    - G2V: G-type main sequence star
    - Temperature: ~5770 K
    - Luminosity class V

I.B - HR Diagram Fundamentals
  • Axes:
    - Y-axis: Luminosity (or absolute magnitude)
    - X-axis: Temperature (decreasing left to right) or spectral class
    - Alternative axes: Color, magnitude (B-V color index)
  • Main Regions:
    - Main Sequence: Diagonal band from upper left to lower right
    - Red Giant Branch: Upper right
    - Horizontal Branch: Horizontal band in upper right
    - Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB): Above red giant branch
    - White Dwarf Region: Lower left
  • Zero-Age Main Sequence (ZAMS):
    - Red dashed line on HR diagram
    - Represents stars just starting hydrogen fusion
    - Stars evolve away from ZAMS as they age
  • Stellar Positions:
    - Sun: Main sequence, G2V
    - Red dwarfs: Lower main sequence, M-type
    - Brown dwarfs: Below main sequence (substellar objects)
    - Can be plotted on HR diagram but are not true stars

I.C - Stellar Populations
  • Population I Stars:
    - Metal-rich (high metallicity)
    - Younger stars
    - Found in disk of galaxy
    - Example: Sun, most stars in spiral arms
  • Population II Stars:
    - Metal-poor (low metallicity)
    - Older stars
    - Found in halo, globular clusters
    - Formed early in universe's history
  • Population III Stars:
    - Hypothetical first generation
    - Zero metallicity (pure H and He)
    - Not yet observed
  • Wolf-Rayet Stars:
    - Population I stars
    - Massive, evolved stars
    - Strong stellar winds
    - Show emission lines

=== SECTION II: STELLAR EVOLUTION ===

II.A - Star Formation
  • Molecular Clouds:
    - Cold, dense gas (H₂, CO)
    - Example: Orion Molecular Cloud Complex (OMC)
    - Conditions: Low temperature, high density
    - Gravity overcomes thermal pressure
  • Protostars:
    - Contracting cloud of gas
    - Not yet fusing hydrogen
    - Generally brighter than main-sequence counterparts
      - Reason: Larger radius, gravitational contraction releases energy
    - Hayashi Track: Vertical evolution at constant temperature
  • Pre-Main Sequence Evolution:
    - T Tauri Stars:
      - Low-mass protostars
      - Irregular light variations
      - Strong stellar winds
      - Contracting toward main sequence
    - Herbig-Haro Objects: Jets from young stars
  • Initial Mass Function (IMF):
    - Distribution of stellar masses at birth
    - More low-mass stars than high-mass stars
    - Affected by: Cloud temperature, magnetic field strength, metallicity

II.B - Main Sequence Evolution
  • Main Sequence:
    - Stars fusing hydrogen in core
    - Longest phase of stellar life
    - Position depends on mass
    - Low-mass stars: Lower, right (cooler, dimmer)
    - High-mass stars: Upper, left (hotter, brighter)
  • Energy Production:
    - Low-mass stars (< 1.5 M☉): Proton-proton chain dominates
    - High-mass stars (> 1.5 M☉): CNO cycle dominates near end of main sequence
    - CNO cycle: Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen cycle
      - More temperature-sensitive than p-p chain
      - Requires higher temperatures
  • Main Sequence Lifetime:
    - Higher mass → shorter lifetime
    - Luminosity ∝ M^3.5 (approximately)
    - Fuel ∝ M
    - Lifetime ∝ M/L ∝ M/M^3.5 ∝ M^-2.5
    - Sun: ~10 billion years on main sequence

II.C - Post-Main Sequence Evolution (Low-Mass Stars)
  • Red Giant Branch:
    - Hydrogen shell burning
    - Core contracts, envelope expands
    - Moves up and right on HR diagram
    - Low-mass stars: Rapid transition after main sequence
  • Helium Flash:
    - Occurs in low-mass stars (~0.5-2 M☉)
    - Degenerate core ignites helium fusion explosively
    - Why radius decreases: Core becomes non-degenerate, more efficient energy production
    - Occurs before horizontal branch
  • Horizontal Branch:
    - Helium core burning
    - Horizontal track on HR diagram
    - Occurs after helium flash
  • Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB):
    - Both hydrogen and helium shell burning
    - Moves up from horizontal branch
    - Envelope expands further
    - S-process nucleosynthesis occurs
      - Slow neutron capture
      - Produces elements heavier than iron
    - Carbon stars: AGB stars with carbon-rich atmospheres
  • Planetary Nebula Phase:
    - Envelope ejected
    - Exposed hot core
    - Ionizes ejected material
    - Short-lived phase
  • White Dwarf:
    - End state for low-mass stars
    - Electron degeneracy pressure
    - No fusion occurring
    - Cools and fades over time
    - Position: Lower left on HR diagram
    - Chandrasekhar Limit: ~1.4 M☉ maximum mass

II.D - Post-Main Sequence Evolution (High-Mass Stars)
  • Red Supergiant:
    - Massive star equivalent of red giant
    - Very luminous
    - Upper right on HR diagram
  • Blue Loop:
    - Occurs after horizontal branch
    - Moves left (hotter) temporarily
    - Then returns to red supergiant region
  • Advanced Burning Stages:
    - Helium → Carbon
    - Carbon → Oxygen, Neon
    - Successive fusion stages
    - Each stage shorter than previous
  • Iron Problem:
    - Iron fusion is endothermic (absorbs energy)
    - No star can fuse iron in stable way
    - Core collapse inevitable when iron core forms
  • Supernova:
    - Type II: Core collapse of massive star
    - Explosive nucleosynthesis
    - Produces elements heavier than iron
    - Leaves neutron star or black hole

II.E - Stellar End States
  • White Dwarfs:
    - Mass limit: Chandrasekhar limit (~1.4 M☉)
    - Supported by electron degeneracy pressure
    - Cools over billions of years
    - Janus star: White dwarf with hydrogen-dominated and helium-dominated hemispheres
  • Neutron Stars:
    - Mass: ~1.4-3 M☉ (approximately)
    - Supported by neutron degeneracy pressure
    - Density: ~10¹⁷ kg/m³ (extremely dense)
    - Radius: ~10 km
    - TOV Limit: Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit (~2-3 M☉)
      - Maximum mass before black hole formation
  • Black Holes:
    - Mass > TOV limit (or > ~3 M☉)
    - Schwarzschild radius: r_s = 2GM/c²
    - Event horizon: Boundary from which light cannot escape
    - Derived from general relativity
  • Mass Sequence (Lowest to Highest):
    - White Dwarf (< 1.4 M☉)
    - Neutron Star (1.4-3 M☉)
    - Black Hole (> 3 M☉)

=== SECTION III: VARIABLE STARS ===

III.A - Pulsating Variables
  • Cepheid Variables:
    - Period-Luminosity Relationship (Leavitt's Law)
    - Longer period → higher luminosity
    - Used as standard candles
    - Pulsation period: Days to weeks
    - Found in instability strip on HR diagram
  • RR Lyrae Variables:
    - Period < 1 day (typically)
    - Roughly constant absolute magnitude (~0.75)
    - Used as standard candles
    - Found in globular clusters
    - Pulsation mechanism: Kappa mechanism (opacity mechanism)
  • Mira Variables:
    - Long-period variables
    - Period > 100 days (typically 100-1000 days)
    - Red giants on AGB
    - Large amplitude variations
    - Late-stage evolved stars
  • Pulsation Mechanism (Kappa Mechanism):
    - Opacity changes due to ionization
    - Helium ionization zone important
    - More ionized → more opaque → absorbs more radiation
    - Creates pulsation cycle
    - Responsible for instability strip on HR diagram

III.B - Instability Strip
  • Location on HR Diagram:
    - Vertical band crossing main sequence and giant branches
    - Stars in this region pulsate
    - Cepheids, RR Lyrae, Mira variables found here
  • Mechanism:
    - Kappa mechanism (opacity mechanism)
    - Ionization zones create driving force
    - Helium ionization particularly important

III.C - Other Variable Types
  • Classical Novae:
    - White dwarf in binary system
    - Accretes material from companion
    - Hydrogen fusion on surface
    - Recurrent (can happen multiple times)
  • Type Ia Supernovae:
    - White dwarf reaches Chandrasekhar limit
    - Accretion or merger triggers explosion
    - Used as standard candles (very bright, consistent peak luminosity)
    - Produces Ni-56 initially (decays to Co-56, then Fe-56)

=== SECTION IV: DISTANCE MEASUREMENT ===

IV.A - Standard Candles
  • Definition:
    - Objects with known absolute magnitude
    - Can determine distance using distance modulus
  • Types:
    - Cepheid variables: Period-luminosity relation
    - RR Lyrae: Constant absolute magnitude
    - Type Ia supernovae: Consistent peak brightness
  • Importance:
    - Measure distances to distant objects
    - Calibrate cosmic distance ladder
    - Study large-scale structure of universe

IV.B - Distance Modulus
  • Formula:
    - m - M = 5log₁₀(d) - 5
    - m = apparent magnitude
    - M = absolute magnitude
    - d = distance in parsecs
  • Rearranged:
    - d = 10^((m-M+5)/5) parsecs
  • Example:
    - RR Lyrae: m = 12.3, M = 0.75
    - m - M = 11.55
    - d = 10^((11.55+5)/5) = 10^3.31 = 2040 pc
  • Cepheid Example:
    - Period = 10 days, m = 14, M = -4
    - m - M = 18
    - d = 10^((18+5)/5) = 10^4.6 = 40,000 pc

IV.C - Parallax
  • Trigonometric Parallax:
    - Apparent shift of nearby stars
    - Measured over 6 months (Earth's orbit)
    - Distance: d = 1/p (parsecs), where p = parallax in arcseconds
  • Limitations:
    - Only works for nearby stars
    - Ground-based: ~0.01" limit (~100 pc)
    - Space-based (Gaia): Much better precision

=== SECTION V: STELLAR PHYSICS ===

V.A - Stefan-Boltzmann Law
  • Formula:
    - L = 4πR²σT⁴
    - L = luminosity
    - R = radius
    - σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W/(m²K⁴))
    - T = surface temperature
  • Relationships:
    - L ∝ R²T⁴
    - If radius doubles (same T): L increases by 4×
    - If temperature doubles (same R): L increases by 16×
    - If both double: L increases by 64×
  • Examples:
    - Star with T = 6000 K, R = 2 R☉ vs. T = 6000 K, R = 4 R☉
      - L₂/L₁ = (R₂/R₁)² = (4/2)² = 4× more luminous
    - Star with T = 5000 K vs. T = 10,000 K (same radius)
      - L₂/L₁ = (T₂/T₁)⁴ = (10,000/5000)⁴ = 16× more luminous

V.B - Wien's Displacement Law
  • Formula:
    - λ_max = b/T
    - b = Wien's constant ≈ 2898 μm·K
    - λ_max = wavelength of peak emission
    - T = temperature
  • Relationship:
    - Hotter objects emit at shorter wavelengths
    - Peak wavelength inversely proportional to temperature
  • Example:
    - Sun (T = 6100 K): λ_max = 2898/6100 = 0.475 μm (visible)
    - Cooler star: Longer peak wavelength (redder)

V.C - Blackbody Radiation
  • Definition:
    - Idealized object that absorbs all incident radiation
    - Emits continuous spectrum
    - No discrete emission or absorption lines (ideal case)
  • Planck's Law:
    - Describes spectral radiance
    - More accurate than Rayleigh-Jeans Law
    - Rayleigh-Jeans accurate only for long wavelengths
  • Applications:
    - Stars approximate blackbodies
    - Stellar spectra show blackbody continuum with absorption lines

V.D - Spectroscopy
  • Absorption Spectra:
    - Dark lines on continuous background
    - Caused by atoms absorbing specific wavelengths
    - Example: Stellar spectra
    - Can determine composition, temperature, velocity
  • Emission Spectra:
    - Bright lines on dark background
    - Hot, low-density gas
    - Example: H II regions, planetary nebulae
  • Spectral Lines:
    - Hydrogen transitions:
      - Lyman series: UV (n=1)
      - Balmer series: Visible (n=2) - Hα, Hβ, Hγ
      - Paschen series: IR (n=3)
      - Brackett series: Far IR (n=4)
    - Lyman-alpha: Critical for H II region studies (ionization)
    - Balmer-alpha (Hα): 656.3 nm, important for H II regions
  • Doppler Shift:
    - Redshift: Moving away (longer wavelength)
    - Blueshift: Moving toward (shorter wavelength)
    - v = c × Δλ/λ
    - Used to measure radial velocity

=== SECTION VI: DEEP SKY OBJECTS ===

VI.A - H II Regions
  • Definition:
    - Ionized hydrogen regions
    - Surround young, massive stars
    - Emit emission lines
  • Characteristics:
    - Bright red color (Hα emission)
    - Hot, ionized gas
    - Example: Orion Nebula (M42)
  • Formation:
    - UV radiation from hot stars ionizes hydrogen
    - Recombination produces emission lines
  • Observatories:
    - Various telescopes image H II regions
    - Visible and infrared observations

VI.B - Planetary Nebulae
  • Formation:
    - Ejected envelope from AGB star
    - Hot white dwarf at center
    - Ionizes surrounding gas
  • Characteristics:
    - Ring or shell structure
    - Emission spectrum
    - Short-lived (~10,000 years)
  • Molecules:
    - Can contain complex molecules
    - Example: C₆₀ (buckminsterfullerene) detected in some
  • Examples:
    - Ring Nebula (M57)
    - Helix Nebula
    - Not pulsating variable stars (despite appearance)

VI.C - Supernova Remnants
  • Cassiopeia A (Cas A):
    - Bright radio source
    - Type II supernova remnant
    - ~350 years old
  • Crab Nebula (M1):
    - Remnant of 1054 supernova
    - Contains pulsar at center
    - Emits across electromagnetic spectrum
  • Unusual Elements:
    - Some remnants show unexpected elements
    - Provides information about nucleosynthesis
  • Wavelengths:
    - Emit prominently in radio wavelengths
    - Also visible in X-rays, optical

VI.D - Pulsars
  • Definition:
    - Rotating neutron stars
    - Emit beams of radiation
    - Pulse as beam sweeps past Earth
  • Discovery:
    - First detected 1967
    - "Little Green Men" initially
    - Actually natural astrophysical objects
  • Physics Violation:
    - Some pulsars appear to violate energy conservation
    - Actually energy comes from rotational kinetic energy
    - Slows down over time
  • Millisecond Pulsars:
    - Very rapid rotation
    - Likely spun up by accretion in binary systems
    - Neutron star + companion star

VI.E - Galaxies
  • Types:
    - Spiral: Disk with arms
    - Elliptical: Smooth, ellipsoidal
    - Irregular: No regular structure
  • Interactions:
    - Galaxies can merge
    - Tidal interactions visible
    - Example: Antennae Galaxies (NGC 4038/4039)
  • Wavelengths:
    - Observed in multiple wavelengths
    - Different features visible in different bands
  • Telescopes:
    - Hubble Space Telescope
    - James Webb Space Telescope
    - Ground-based observatories

VI.F - Exoplanets and Transits
  • Transit Method:
    - Planet passes in front of star
    - Causes dip in brightness
    - Depth indicates planet size
  • Famous Examples:
    - Some variable stars have confirmed planets
    - Transit depth used to determine planet properties
  • Light Curves:
    - Show periodic dips
    - Period gives orbital period
    - Depth gives planet/star size ratio

=== SECTION VII: BINARY SYSTEMS ===

VII.A - Orbital Mechanics
  • Kepler's Laws Applied:
    - Period from separation and masses
    - T² = (4π²/GM) × a³
    - For binary: M = M₁ + M₂
  • Radial Velocity:
    - Measure velocity from Doppler shift
    - Can determine orbital parameters
    - Graph shows velocity vs. time
  • Orbital Energy:
    - E = -GM₁M₂/(2a)
    - Negative for bound orbits
  • Gravitational Radiation:
    - Binary systems lose energy
    - Emit gravitational waves
    - Causes orbital decay
    - Rate: -32/5 × G⁴c⁻⁵r⁻⁵M₁M₂(M₁+M₂)
    - Leads to merger

VII.B - White Dwarf Binaries
  • Evolution:
    - Two white dwarfs orbit each other
    - Lose energy via gravitational waves
    - Eventually merge
  • Merger Outcome:
    - Can exceed Chandrasekhar limit
    - Triggers Type Ia supernova
    - Explosive nucleosynthesis
  • Observational Signatures:
    - Radial velocity variations
    - Light curve variations
    - Eventually supernova

=== SECTION VIII: OBSERVATIONAL ASTRONOMY ===

VIII.A - Electromagnetic Spectrum
  • Regions:
    - Radio: Longest wavelength, lowest frequency
    - Microwave
    - Infrared: Thermal imaging
    - Visible: 400-700 nm
    - Ultraviolet: Shorter than visible
    - X-ray: High energy
    - Gamma: Highest energy
  • Ordering by Frequency:
    - Increasing: Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible, UV, X-ray, Gamma
    - Decreasing wavelength: Opposite order
  • Applications:
    - Radio: Pulsars, molecular clouds
    - Infrared: Cool objects, dust
    - Visible: Stars, galaxies
    - X-ray: Hot gas, compact objects
    - Gamma: High-energy processes

VIII.B - Observing Techniques
  • Radio Astronomy:
    - Fourier analysis: Decomposes signals into frequencies
    - Used for pulsar detection
    - Signal processing techniques
  • Spectroscopy:
    - Measure spectral lines
    - Determine composition, velocity, temperature
  • Photometry:
    - Measure brightness
    - Light curves for variables
    - Magnitude system

VIII.C - Extinction and Reddening
  • Interstellar Extinction:
    - Light absorbed/scattered by dust
    - Reddens starlight (blue light scattered more)
    - Measured in magnitudes per distance
    - Example: ~1.8 mag/kpc in Milky Way
  • Correction:
    - Must account for extinction
    - Affects distance measurements
    - Color excess: E(B-V) = (B-V)_observed - (B-V)_intrinsic

=== SECTION IX: CALCULATIONS ===

IX.A - Stellar Properties
  • Density:
    - ρ = M/(4/3 πR³)
    - Example: Neutron star, M = 1.4 M☉, R = 10 km
      - M = 1.4 × 1.989 × 10³⁰ = 2.78 × 10³⁰ kg
      - ρ = 2.78 × 10³⁰ / (4/3 π × 10⁷) = 6.65 × 10¹⁷ kg/m³
  • Luminosity from Temperature:
    - Use Stefan-Boltzmann law
    - Need radius and temperature
  • Solar Luminosity:
    - L☉ = 3.828 × 10²⁶ W
    - Can calculate from: L = 4πR²σT⁴
    - R☉ = 696,000 km, T = 5770 K

IX.B - Orbital Calculations
  • Period from Mass and Distance:
    - T² = (4π²/GM) × a³
    - For Earth: T = 1 year, a = 1 AU
    - For planet: M = 2 M☉, a = 5 AU
      - T² = (4π²/G × 2M☉) × (5 AU)³
      - T = √(125/2) = 7.9 years
  • Velocity:
    - v = √(GM/r)
    - For circular orbits

IX.C - Distance Calculations
  • Parallax:
    - d = 1/p (parsecs)
    - p in arcseconds
  • Distance Modulus:
    - d = 10^((m-M+5)/5) pc
    - From apparent and absolute magnitudes

IX.D - Energy and Radiation
  • Solar Constant:
    - S = L_sun / (4πD²)
    - D = Earth-Sun distance = 1.5 × 10⁸ km
    - S ≈ 1366 W/m²
  • Equilibrium Temperature:
    - For blackbody: T = [S(1-A)/(4σ)]^(1/4)
    - A = albedo
    - Accounts for absorbed vs. reflected light

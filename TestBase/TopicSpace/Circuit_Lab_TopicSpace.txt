=== SECTION I: FUNDAMENTAL CIRCUIT LAWS AND PRINCIPLES ===

I.A - Ohm's Law and Basic Relationships
  • Ohm's Law: V = IR (voltage = current × resistance)
  • Power equations:
    - P = VI (power = voltage × current)
    - P = I²R (power = current² × resistance)
    - P = V²/R (power = voltage² / resistance)
    - P = V²R is NOT valid (common error)
  • Energy and work:
    - W = VIt (work = voltage × current × time)
    - W = V²t/R (work = voltage² × time / resistance)
    - E = Pt (energy = power × time)
  • Current definitions:
    - I = Q/t (current = charge / time)
    - I = nAvde (current = charge density × area × drift velocity × charge)
    - I = J · A (current = current density × area)
    - I = ie is NOT valid (common error)
  • Resistance relationships:
    - R = ρL/A (resistance = resistivity × length / cross-sectional area)
    - Resistivity (ρ) is material property, independent of dimensions
    - Same material wires: longer = higher resistance, thicker = lower resistance
    - Temperature dependence: R increases with temperature (lattice vibrations increase electron scattering)

I.B - Kirchhoff's Laws
  • Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL):
    - Sum of currents entering a node = sum of currents leaving
    - ΣI_in = ΣI_out at any junction
    - Charge cannot accumulate at nodes in steady-state DC
    - Useful for parallel circuit analysis
  • Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL):
    - Sum of voltage drops around any closed loop = 0
    - ΣV_loop = 0
    - Conservation of energy in circuits
    - Useful for series circuit analysis
  • Application to complex circuits:
    - Node analysis using KCL
    - Mesh/loop analysis using KVL
    - Systematic circuit solving

I.C - Series and Parallel Circuits
  • Series circuits:
    - Current same through all components
    - Voltage divides across components
    - Equivalent resistance: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
    - Largest resistor dissipates most power in series
    - Voltage divider formed by series resistors
  • Parallel circuits:
    - Voltage same across all branches
    - Current divides among branches
    - Equivalent resistance: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
    - R_parallel always ≤ smallest resistor
    - R_parallel always < R_series for same resistors
    - If one branch opens, total current decreases
  • Mixed series-parallel:
    - Simplify step-by-step
    - Identify series and parallel sections
    - Calculate equivalent resistances progressively

I.D - Equivalent Resistance Calculations
  • Series: R_eq = ΣR_i
  • Parallel: R_eq = 1/Σ(1/R_i)
  • Complex networks:
    - Delta-Wye (Δ-Y) transformations
    - Symmetry exploitation
    - Systematic reduction methods
  • Special cases:
    - Two resistors in parallel: R_eq = (R₁R₂)/(R₁ + R₂)
    - N identical resistors in parallel: R_eq = R/N
    - N identical resistors in series: R_eq = NR

=== SECTION II: CIRCUIT COMPONENTS ===

II.A - Resistors
  • Fixed resistors:
    - Carbon composition, metal film, wire-wound types
    - Power ratings: 1/4W, 1/2W, 1W, etc.
    - Tolerance: ±1%, ±5%, ±10% (gold), ±20% (no band)
  • Resistor color codes:
    - 4-band: Digit1, Digit2, Multiplier, Tolerance
    - 5-band: Digit1, Digit2, Digit3, Multiplier, Tolerance
    - 6-band: Adds temperature coefficient band
    - Color values: Black(0), Brown(1), Red(2), Orange(3), Yellow(4), Green(5), Blue(6), Violet(7), Gray(8), White(9)
    - Gold multiplier = ×0.1, Silver = ×0.01
    - Gold tolerance = ±5%, Silver = ±10%
    - Example: Orange-Blue-Brown-Gold = 360Ω ±5%
    - Example: Violet-Red-Blue-Black-Green-Gray = 736Ω ±0.5%, 1 ppm/K
  • Variable resistors:
    - Potentiometers (pots): 3-terminal variable resistors
    - Rheostats: 2-terminal variable resistors
    - Trimpots: Small adjustable resistors
    - Resistance function: R = ρL/A (varies with wiper position)
    - Thermal failure: Maximum power density at specific wiper positions
  • Resistor networks:
    - Resistor banks/arrays
    - Matrix configurations
    - Standard values: 1kΩ, 3.3kΩ, 10kΩ, 22kΩ, 51kΩ, 100kΩ

II.B - Capacitors
  • Basic principles:
    - Store energy in electric field
    - Block DC, pass AC (once charged)
    - Charge: Q = CV
    - Energy stored: E = ½CV² = ½Q²/C = ½QV
  • Capacitance formula:
    - Parallel plate: C = ε₀εᵣA/d
    - ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m (vacuum permittivity)
    - εᵣ = relative dielectric constant
    - Area (A) and separation (d) determine capacitance
  • Types:
    - Ceramic: Non-polarized, small values
    - Electrolytic: Polarized, high capacitance
    - Film: Non-polarized, stable
    - SMT (Surface Mount): Miniature packages
  • Dielectrics:
    - Inserting dielectric increases capacitance: C_new = κC_old
    - Dielectric constant κ > 1
    - Electric field decreases: E_new = E_old/κ
    - Energy changes depend on isolation vs. fixed voltage
    - Dielectric breakdown: Failure mechanism at high voltage
  • Charging and discharging:
    - RC time constant: τ = RC
    - Charging: V(t) = V₀(1 - e^(-t/τ))
    - Discharging: V(t) = V₀e^(-t/τ)
    - ~5τ for full charge/discharge (99.3%)
    - ~3τ for near steady-state
  • Series capacitors:
    - 1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + ...
    - C_eq < smallest capacitor
    - Charge same on all capacitors
  • Parallel capacitors:
    - C_eq = C₁ + C₂ + ...
    - C_eq > largest capacitor
    - Voltage same across all capacitors
  • PCB capacitance:
    - Coupled capacitance between traces: C = εA/d
    - Fringing fields at edges
    - RC modeling of clock edges
    - Capacitive coupling: i_c = CΔV/Δt

II.C - Inductors
  • Basic principles:
    - Store energy in magnetic field
    - Oppose changes in current
    - Pass DC, block AC (at high frequencies)
    - Hydraulic analogy: Flywheel (inertia)
  • Inductance:
    - L measured in Henries (H)
    - Self-inductance: L = NΦ/I
    - Energy stored: E = ½LI²
  • RL circuits:
    - Time constant: τ = L/R
    - Current rise: I(t) = I₀(1 - e^(-t/τ))
    - ~5τ for steady-state current
  • Solenoids:
    - Magnetic field: B = μ₀nI
    - n = N/L (turns per unit length)
    - Field strength depends on: N, n, I, μ₀
    - Field strength NOT dependent on supply voltage directly

II.D - Diodes
  • Basic operation:
    - Unidirectional current flow
    - Forward bias: Current flows (low resistance)
    - Reverse bias: Current blocked (high resistance)
  • Silicon diodes:
    - Forward voltage drop: ~0.7V average
    - Typical range: 0.6-0.8V
    - Reverse breakdown voltage varies
  • Types:
    - Standard rectifier diodes
    - Zener diodes: Voltage regulation, reverse breakdown
    - Schottky diodes: Lower forward voltage (~0.3V)
    - Photodiodes: Light-sensitive
    - Varactor diodes (varicaps): Voltage-controlled capacitance
  • LED (Light Emitting Diode):
    - Forward voltage varies by color:
      - Red: ~1.8-2.0V
      - Blue: ~3.0-3.5V
      - White: ~3.0-3.5V
    - Typical forward current: 20mA
    - Maximum forward voltage: ~0.4-0.8V higher than typical
    - Efficiency: ~80-90% (much higher than incandescent)
    - Series resistor calculation: R = (V_supply - V_LED)/I_LED
    - Power: P = V_LED × I_LED
  • Rectifiers:
    - Half-wave: Single diode, conducts half cycle
    - Full-wave: Bridge rectifier (4 diodes), conducts full cycle
    - Smoothing: Add capacitor in parallel with load
    - Unidirectional current flow

II.E - Transistors
  • BJT (Bipolar Junction Transistor):
    - NPN: Current flows from collector to emitter when base is positive
    - PNP: Current flows from emitter to collector when base is negative
    - Three terminals: Base, Collector, Emitter
    - Current amplification: I_C = βI_B
  • MOSFET (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor):
    - Voltage-controlled device
    - High input impedance
    - Types: N-channel, P-channel
    - Enhancement vs. depletion mode
    - Fabrication: Controlled silicon oxidation
    - History: Julius Edgar Lilienfeld proposed concept in 1925
    - "Metal" sometimes misnomer (can be polysilicon)
  • Functions:
    - Amplification: Signal amplification
    - Switching: Digital logic, power control
    - NOT: Absorb excess current (incorrect)
    - NOT: Compare voltages (that's op-amp function)
  • Applications:
    - Digital logic gates
    - Power switching
    - Amplifiers
    - Current sources

II.F - Operational Amplifiers (Op-Amps)
  • Ideal op-amp properties:
    - Infinite gain (A → ∞)
    - Infinite input impedance
    - Zero output impedance
    - Virtual short: v+ ≈ v- when A → ∞
  • Basic equations:
    - Output: v₀ = A(v+ - v-)
    - For large gain: v+ = v- (virtual short)
  • Applications:
    - Voltage amplification
    - Signal conditioning
    - Comparators
    - Filters
    - Integrators and differentiators
  • Analysis:
    - Use KVL and KCL
    - Apply virtual short condition
    - Solve for output voltage

II.G - Switches
  • Types:
    - SPST (Single-Pole Single-Throw): Basic on/off
    - SPDT (Single-Pole Double-Throw): One input, two outputs
    - DPDT (Double-Pole Double-Throw): Two independent switches
    - TPTT (Triple-Pole Triple-Throw): Possible but uncommon
    - Toggle switches
    - Push-button switches
    - Reed switches: Magnetic activation
    - Rotary switches: Multiple positions
    - Three-way switches: Control light from two locations
  • Applications:
    - Circuit control
    - Signal routing
    - Safety disconnection

II.H - Batteries and Power Sources
  • Battery types:
    - Lead-acid: Low energy density
    - Nickel-cadmium: Moderate energy density
    - Lithium-ion: Highest energy density
    - Alkaline: Moderate energy density
  • Voltaic cells:
    - Anode: Negative (electron source)
    - Cathode: Positive (electron sink)
    - Electrons flow: Anode → external circuit → Cathode
  • Electrolytic cells:
    - Anode: Positive (electron sink)
    - Cathode: Negative (electron source)
    - Electrons flow: External source → Anode → Cathode
  • History:
    - Alessandro Volta: Invented first battery (Voltaic Pile)
    - Voltage unit named after Volta
    - Voltage = potential difference
  • DC-DC converters:
    - Buck converter: Steps voltage down
    - Boost converter: Steps voltage up
    - Switching regulators

=== SECTION III: AC CIRCUITS ===

III.A - AC Fundamentals
  • AC definition: Alternating Current
  • Household AC (North America):
    - RMS voltage: 120V
    - Peak voltage: 120V × √2 ≈ 170V
    - Peak-to-peak voltage: 2 × 170V = 340V
    - Frequency: 60 Hz
    - Period: T = 1/60 s ≈ 16.7 ms
  • RMS (Root Mean Square):
    - V_RMS = V_peak/√2
    - I_RMS = I_peak/√2
    - Power: P = V_RMS × I_RMS (for resistive loads)
  • Phase relationships:
    - Resistors: Voltage and current in phase (φ = 0°)
    - Capacitors: Current leads voltage by 90°
    - Inductors: Voltage leads current by 90°
    - Voltage lags current in capacitive circuits
    - Current lags voltage in inductive circuits

III.B - Impedance and Complex Numbers
  • Impedance (Z):
    - Complex quantity: Z = R + jX
    - Real component: Resistance (R)
    - Imaginary component: Reactance (X)
    - Magnitude: |Z| = √(R² + X²)
    - Phase angle: φ = arctan(X/R)
  • Reactance:
    - Capacitive: X_C = 1/(ωC) = 1/(2πfC)
    - Inductive: X_L = ωL = 2πfL
  • Complex impedance calculations:
    - Series: Z_eq = Z₁ + Z₂ + ...
    - Parallel: 1/Z_eq = 1/Z₁ + 1/Z₂ + ...
    - Example: Z = 67i² + 21 - 42i → Simplify using i² = -1

III.C - RC and RL Circuits in AC
  • RC circuits:
    - Low-pass filter: Blocks high frequencies
    - High-pass filter: Blocks low frequencies
    - Cutoff frequency: f_c = 1/(2πRC)
  • RL circuits:
    - Low-pass filter behavior
    - Cutoff frequency: f_c = R/(2πL)
  • LC circuits:
    - Resonant frequency: f = 1/(2π√(LC))
    - ω = 1/√(LC) (angular frequency)
    - Oscillations at resonance

III.D - Transformers
  • Basic operation:
    - AC voltage transformation using magnetic flux
    - Primary coil: Input side
    - Secondary coil: Output side
    - Turns ratio: N₁/N₂ determines voltage ratio
  • Voltage transformation:
    - V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁
    - Step-up: N₂ > N₁ → V₂ > V₁
    - Step-down: N₂ < N₁ → V₂ < V₁
    - Example: 1:10 step-up → V₂ = 10V₁
  • Current transformation:
    - I₂/I₁ = N₁/N₂ (inverse of voltage ratio)
    - Power conservation: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂ (ideal transformer)
    - Power in = Power out
  • EMF calculation:
    - Faraday's law: ε = -N dΦ/dt
    - Average EMF: ε_avg = NΔΦ/Δt
    - Both coils experience same flux change
  • Applications:
    - Power transmission: High voltage for efficiency
    - Household: Step-down transformers (120V)
    - Isolation: Safety and signal isolation

III.E - Power Transmission
  • High voltage transmission:
    - P_loss = I²R (power loss in transmission lines)
    - Higher voltage → Lower current → Less power loss
    - Step-up at generation, step-down at consumption
  • AC advantages:
    - Easy voltage transformation (transformers)
    - Efficient long-distance transmission
    - Lower power losses than DC at same voltage
  • Skin effect:
    - AC current density greatest near conductor surface
    - Current density decreases exponentially with depth
    - AC resistance increased by skin effect
    - Mitigation: Litz wire (multiple insulated strands)
    - Tubular conductors have advantage over solid lines

III.F - AC Generation
  • Generators:
    - AC generators: Rotating coils in magnetic field
    - Three-phase AC: Common in power systems
    - Wind turbines: Typically use three-phase AC generators
    - Permanent magnet generators
  • Frequency generation:
    - Period: T = t₁ + t₂ (for variable resistance)
    - Frequency: f = 1/T
    - American grid: 60 Hz
    - Period for 60 Hz: T = 1/60 s

=== SECTION IV: ELECTROMAGNETISM ===

IV.A - Magnetic Fields
  • Magnetic field direction:
    - Always points North to South
    - Field lines form closed loops
    - No magnetic monopoles (Gauss's law for magnetism)
  • Sources of magnetic fields:
    - Moving charges (current)
    - Changing current
    - Steady-state current
    - Permanent magnets
    - NOT: Stationary charges, stationary protons
    - NOT: Moving neutrons (no charge)
  • Right-hand rule:
    - Current-carrying wire: Thumb = current, fingers = field direction
    - Current loop: Fingers = current, thumb = field direction
    - Force on moving charge: Fingers = field, thumb = velocity, palm = force
  • Straight wire:
    - B = μ₀I/(2πr) (1/r dependence)
    - Field forms concentric circles around wire
    - Direction determined by right-hand rule
  • Solenoid:
    - B = μ₀nI (uniform field inside)
    - n = N/L (turns per unit length)
    - Field strength independent of supply voltage directly

IV.B - Magnetic Forces
  • Lorentz force law:
    - F = q(E + v × B)
    - Electric force: F_e = qE (parallel to field)
    - Magnetic force: F_m = q(v × B) (perpendicular to velocity and field)
    - No magnetic force if charge not moving
  • Force on current-carrying wire:
    - F = IL × B
    - Direction: Right-hand rule
  • Force between parallel wires:
    - Same direction current: Attract
    - Opposite direction current: Repel
    - Force per unit length: F/L = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd)
  • Electron motion:
    - Electron in +x direction, force in -y direction
    - Magnetic field direction determined by right-hand rule
    - For negative charge, reverse direction

IV.C - Magnetic Flux
  • Definition:
    - Φ_B = B · A = BAcos(θ)
    - Units: Weber (Wb) = T·m²
    - Maximum when B perpendicular to area
    - Zero when B parallel to area
  • Single coil:
    - Φ = BA (for perpendicular field)
    - Example: B = 0.02T, diameter = 50cm → A = πr²
  • Changing flux:
    - Induced EMF: ε = -dΦ/dt
    - Faraday's law of induction
  • Multiple coils:
    - Total flux: NΦ (N = number of turns)
    - Used in transformers and generators

IV.D - Faraday's Law and Induction
  • Faraday's law:
    - ε = -N dΦ/dt
    - Induced EMF proportional to rate of flux change
    - Negative sign: Lenz's law (opposes change)
  • Lenz's law:
    - Induced current opposes flux change
    - Eddy currents in conductors
    - Example: Magnet falling through conductive tube slows down
  • Applications:
    - Generators: Rotating coils produce AC
    - Transformers: Changing flux induces voltage
    - Induction motors
    - Eddy current braking

IV.E - Maxwell's Equations
  • Contributors:
    - James Clerk Maxwell: Mathematical formalization (1860s)
    - Michael Faraday: Experimental discovery
    - André-Marie Ampère: Current-magnetic field relationship
    - Carl Friedrich Gauss: Electric and magnetic flux laws
    - Heinrich Hertz: Did not contribute to original equations
    - Oliver Heaviside: Recast into vector calculus form
  • Maxwell-Faraday equation:
    - ∇ × E = -∂B/∂t (differential form)
    - Time-varying magnetic field → spatially varying electric field
    - Non-conservative electric field

IV.F - Motors and Generators
  • DC motors:
    - Commutator: Reverses current direction as coil rotates
    - Function: Maintains rotation direction
    - NOT: Converts AC to DC
  • BLDC (Brushless DC) motors:
    - Rotate by induced magnetic fields
    - No brushes (more reliable)
    - Can be more expensive than brushed DC motors
    - Suitable for various speed applications
  • AC generators:
    - Rotating magnet or coil
    - Magnetic flux through loop changes
    - Maximum flux: When field perpendicular to loop plane
    - Minimum flux: When field parallel to loop plane
    - Number of poles affects peak flux
    - Even number of poles required (no magnetic monopoles)

=== SECTION V: ELECTROSTATICS ===

V.A - Coulomb's Law
  • Force between point charges:
    - F = kq₁q₂/r²
    - k = 8.99 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²
    - Inverse square law (1/r² dependence)
    - Attractive for opposite charges, repulsive for like charges
  • Distance dependence:
    - Triple distance → Force becomes 1/9 original
    - Force ∝ 1/r²
  • Example calculations:
    - Two electrons 200 pm apart
    - Multiple point charges (superposition)

V.B - Electric Fields
  • Point charge:
    - E = kq/r²
    - Radial field direction
  • Uniformly charged plate:
    - E = σ/(2ε₀) for infinite plate
    - σ = Q/A (surface charge density)
    - Example: 4.0 mC over 5.0 m² → σ = 0.8 mC/m²
  • Sources:
    - Stationary charges produce electric fields
    - Stationary proton produces field
    - Moving charges produce both E and B fields
    - Changing magnetic field produces electric field

V.C - Electric Potential
  • Point charge:
    - V = kq/r
    - Scalar quantity
    - Zero at infinity
  • Multiple charges:
    - Superposition: V_total = ΣV_i
    - Example: 4 charges forming tetrahedron
    - Calculate potential at each point, sum
  • Potential difference:
    - ΔV = V₂ - V₁
    - Work done: W = qΔV
    - Units: Volts (V) = J/C

V.D - Charge Distribution
  • Conducting spheres:
    - Charge distributes on surface
    - Two spheres touched together: Charge equalizes
    - Final charge: Q_total/2 on each (if identical)
    - Example: 15C + 5C → 10C each
    - Depends on radius if different sizes
  • Charge equilibration:
    - Charges flow until potential equal
    - Conservation of total charge

=== SECTION VI: RC AND RL CIRCUITS ===

VI.A - RC Circuits
  • Time constant:
    - τ = RC
    - Units: seconds (Ω × F)
  • Charging:
    - V(t) = V₀(1 - e^(-t/τ))
    - Q(t) = Q₀(1 - e^(-t/τ))
    - Current: I(t) = (V₀/R)e^(-t/τ)
    - ~5τ for full charge (99.3%)
    - ~3τ for near steady-state
  • Discharging:
    - V(t) = V₀e^(-t/τ)
    - Q(t) = Q₀e^(-t/τ)
    - Current: I(t) = -(V₀/R)e^(-t/τ)
    - ~5τ for full discharge
  • Energy:
    - Stored: E = ½CV²
    - Dissipated in resistor during charging/discharging
    - Energy conservation: Battery energy = Capacitor energy + Resistor energy
  • Complex RC circuits:
    - Multiple capacitors and resistors
    - Series/parallel combinations
    - Time-dependent behavior
    - Steady-state analysis

VI.B - RL Circuits
  • Time constant:
    - τ = L/R
    - Units: seconds (H / Ω)
  • Current rise:
    - I(t) = I₀(1 - e^(-t/τ))
    - ~5τ for steady-state current
    - Voltage across inductor: V_L = L(dI/dt)
  • Current decay:
    - I(t) = I₀e^(-t/τ)
    - Energy stored: E = ½LI²
  • Applications:
    - Inductor opposes current changes
    - Smoothing current variations
    - Filter circuits

VI.C - RLC Circuits
  • Resonant frequency:
    - f = 1/(2π√(LC))
    - ω = 1/√(LC)
    - Example: L = 700μH, C = 8mF → Calculate f
  • Damping:
    - Overdamped, critically damped, underdamped
    - Quality factor Q
  • Applications:
    - Tuned circuits
    - Oscillators
    - Filters

=== SECTION VII: VOLTAGE DIVIDERS AND CIRCUIT ANALYSIS ===

VII.A - Voltage Dividers
  • Basic voltage divider:
    - Two resistors in series
    - V_out = V_in × R₂/(R₁ + R₂)
    - V₁ + V₂ = V_in (KVL)
  • General formula:
    - V_out = V_in × R_bottom/(R_top + R_bottom)
    - Current: I = V_in/(R₁ + R₂)
  • Power dissipation:
    - P_total = V_in²/(R₁ + R₂)
    - P_R1 = V₁²/R₁
    - P_R2 = V₂²/R₂
  • Applications:
    - Sensor interfacing
    - Level shifting
    - Reference voltages
    - ADC input scaling (e.g., 5V to 3.3V)

VII.B - Resistor Selection for Dividers
  • Standard values:
    - 1kΩ, 3.3kΩ, 10kΩ, 22kΩ, 51kΩ, 100kΩ
  • Ratio design:
    - Target ratio (e.g., 1:50, 1:100, 1:10, 1:3)
    - Minimize number of resistors
    - Minimize DC current
    - Series/parallel combinations
  • Examples:
    - 1:50 ratio: Use standard values
    - 1:100 ratio: Use standard values
    - 1:10 ratio: Use standard values
    - 1:3 ratio: Use standard values

VII.C - Variable Voltage Dividers (Potentiometers)
  • Potentiometer operation:
    - Three-terminal device
    - Variable resistance between terminals
    - Wiper position determines resistance
  • Resistance function:
    - R(x) = ρL(x)/A
    - x = 0 at one end, x = L at other end
    - Linear or logarithmic tapers
  • Thermal considerations:
    - Power density: P/A varies with position
    - Maximum thermal failure at specific wiper positions
    - Constant voltage across potentiometer
  • AC generation:
    - Varying resistance → Varying output voltage
    - R_min vs R₁ for amplitude maximization
    - R_max vs R₁ for amplitude maximization
    - Period: T = t₁ + t₂
    - Frequency: f = 1/T

VII.D - Thermistors in Voltage Dividers
  • Positive temperature coefficient (PTC):
    - Resistance increases with temperature
    - R = AT + B (linear approximation)
  • Behavior:
    - As T → ∞: R → ∞, V₂ → V_in
    - As T → 0: R → B, V₂ → V_in × B/(R₁ + B)
  • Expression:
    - V₂ = V_in × R(T)/(R₁ + R(T))
    - Substitute R = AT + B

=== SECTION VIII: MEASUREMENT AND INSTRUMENTS ===

VIII.A - Multimeters
  • Functions:
    - Voltage measurement (voltmeter)
    - Current measurement (ammeter)
    - Resistance measurement (ohmmeter)
  • Usage:
    - Voltage: Parallel connection
    - Current: Series connection
    - Resistance: Power off, component isolated
  • Practical measurements:
    - Voltage drop across components
    - Current through branches
    - Resistance values
    - Circuit analysis verification

VIII.B - Other Instruments
  • Oscilloscope:
    - Waveform visualization
    - Time-domain analysis
    - Voltage vs. time graphs
  • Galvanometer:
    - Current detection
    - Sensitive ammeter
  • Gaussmeter:
    - Magnetic field measurement
  • Function generator:
    - AC signal generation
    - Variable frequency/amplitude

VIII.C - Units and Dimensional Analysis
  • Base units:
    - Coulombs (C): Charge
    - Joules (J): Energy
    - Seconds (s): Time
  • Derived units:
    - Voltage: V = J/C
    - Current: I = C/s
    - Capacitance: C = C²/J = s²·C²/J
    - Power: P = J/s
    - Impedance: Z = V/I = J·s/C²
  • SI units:
    - Volt (V): Named after Alessandro Volta
    - Ampere (A): Named after André-Marie Ampère
    - Ohm (Ω): Named after Georg Ohm
    - Farad (F): Named after Michael Faraday
    - Henry (H): Named after Joseph Henry
    - Tesla (T): Magnetic flux density (named after Nikola Tesla)
    - Weber (Wb): Magnetic flux

=== SECTION IX: SAFETY AND PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS ===

IX.A - Electrical Safety
  • Current vs. voltage:
    - "Current kills, not voltage"
    - ~0.2A can be lethal
    - High voltage warnings: Higher voltage → Higher current risk (Ohm's law)
    - V = IR: High V with low R → High I
  • Shock factors:
    - Voltage
    - Current
    - Frequency (AC vs. DC)
    - Contact duration
    - Path through body
  • Protection devices:
    - Fuses: One-time use, fast response, lower cost
    - Circuit breakers: Reusable, can be slower, higher cost
    - GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter):
      - Compares hot and neutral currents
      - Triggers at ~5mA difference
      - Required: Kitchen, bathroom, garage, outdoor outlets
    - Overcurrent protection: Critical for DC battery systems

IX.B - High Voltage Phenomena
  • Corona effect:
    - Air ionization around high voltage lines
    - Visible glow
    - Power loss mechanism
  • Dielectric breakdown:
    - Capacitor failure at high voltage
    - Dielectric material breaks down
    - Creates conductive path
  • Van de Graaff generator:
    - High voltage, low current
    - Safe to touch (low current)
    - Produces electrical arcs

IX.C - Power Systems
  • Household AC:
    - 120V RMS, 60 Hz (North America)
    - 240V for large appliances
    - Three-wire system: Hot, Neutral, Ground
  • DC systems:
    - Battery-powered devices
    - Overcurrent protection critical
    - Lower voltage typically safer
  • Power transmission:
    - High voltage for efficiency
    - Step-up transformers at generation
    - Step-down transformers at consumption
    - AC preferred for easy transformation

=== SECTION X: MATERIALS AND CONDUCTIVITY ===

X.A - Conductors
  • Best conductors (descending):
    - Silver (highest)
    - Gold
    - Copper
    - Tap water (moderate)
    - Distilled water (very low)
  • Conductivity factors:
    - Material properties
    - Temperature (resistance increases with T)
    - Impurities
    - Crystal structure

X.B - Wire Properties
  • Current carrying capacity:
    - Depends on: Gauge (cross-sectional area), Material
    - NOT primarily: Length, Insulation material
  • Voltage rating:
    - Depends on: Insulation material
    - NOT: Wire material, Gauge, Length
  • Resistance:
    - R = ρL/A
    - Longer wire → Higher resistance
    - Thicker wire → Lower resistance
    - Higher temperature → Higher resistance

X.C - PCB Materials
  • Common materials:
    - FR-4 (Woven glass + epoxy): Most common
    - Phenolic paper: Less common
    - Aluminum: Less common
    - Kapton: Least common (specialty applications)
  • Dielectric properties:
    - Relative permittivity (εᵣ): ~4.5 typical
    - Affects capacitance between traces
    - Affects signal propagation

=== SECTION XII: HISTORY AND THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS ===

XII.A - Key Scientists
  • Alessandro Volta (1745-1827):
    - Invented first battery (Voltaic Pile, 1800)
    - Voltage unit named after him
    - Worked in Italy
  • André-Marie Ampère (1775-1836):
    - Current-magnetic field relationship
    - Ampere's law: 1/r dependence
    - Worked in France
  • Charles-Augustin de Coulomb (1736-1806):
    - Electrostatic force law
    - Coulomb's law: 1/r² dependence
    - Worked in France
  • Georg Ohm (1789-1854):
    - Ohm's law: V = IR
    - Resistance unit named after him
    - Steady-state relationships
  • Michael Faraday (1791-1867):
    - Electromagnetic induction
    - Faraday's law: ε = -dΦ/dt
    - Capacitance unit (Farad) named after him
  • Gustav Kirchhoff (1824-1887):
    - Kirchhoff's laws (KCL, KVL)
    - Steady-state circuit analysis
  • Nikola Tesla (1856-1943):
    - AC power systems
    - Magnetic flux density unit (Tesla) named after him
  • James Clerk Maxwell (1831-1879):
    - Maxwell's equations (1860s)
    - Unified electricity and magnetism
    - Mathematical formalization

XII.B - Theoretical Concepts
  • Relativity and magnetism:
    - Magnetism results from relativistic length contraction
    - Electric and magnetic fields unified
    - Not a fundamental separate force
  • Magnetic monopoles:
    - Do not exist (Gauss's law for magnetism)
    - ∇ · B = 0 (no magnetic charge)
    - Even number of poles required in magnets
  • Wave-particle duality:
    - Light is both particle and wave
    - Photons: Discrete energy packets
    - Interference: Wave behavior

U.S. Air Force basic training takes place at Joint Base San Antonio-Lackland in San Antonio, Texas. Trainees are organized into rolling military units averaging 900 entering and
graduating trainees per week, with each unit further divided into groups of approximately 52 trainees. Trainees are assigned to specific dormitories, which typically house 50–60
persons, sleeping in beds that alternate head directions, creating a foot-to-head orientation in the bay. Trainees arrive weekly and undergo a standardized 7.5-week basic military
training (BMT) curriculum organized by week of training, resulting in training activities and associated exposures that are typically consistent for each class.
To prevent invasive meningococcal and streptococcal disease outbreaks, all trainees receive quadrivalent meningococcal (Menveo) vaccine within 72 hours of arrival and a single
dose of penicillin G benzathine injectable suspension, respectively, within 7 days of arrival (1). Penicillin allergic trainees receive weekly oral azithromycin to prevent streptococcal
disease during BMT.
In February 2025, two cases of Neisseria meningitidis bacterial conjunctivitis were identified in otherwise healthy basic military trainees at Joint Base San Antonio-Lackland in San
Antonio, Texas; an investigation was conducted to identify the source of the outbreak and to make treatment recommendations. Ultimately, 41 cases of N. meningitidis conjunctivit
and 32 cases of Haemophilus species conjunctivitis were identified among 11,797 trainees.
People spread meningococcal bacteria to others by sharing respiratory and throat secretions (saliva or spit). Generally, it takes close or lengthy contact to spread the bacteria.
The 41 confirmed cases of N. meningitidis conjunctivitis occurred in trainees in 37 unique BMT groups. During this period, men constituted 78% of the BMT population but
accounted for 90% of the N. meningitidis cases. Among trainees with confirmed N. meningitidis conjunctivitis, 33 (80%) reported an antecedent upper respiratory infection, 27
people had cough, and 22 people had a sore throat. Overall, 35 (85%) patients had unilateral eye involvement. 
Assume all confirmed cases are actual cases.

The following case study is completely made up, any resemblance to real life figures or events is coincidental.
Dr. Imogen Vale, a senior field epidemiologist at the Department of Environmental Health, was called to investigate an unusual rise in chronic fatigue and skin irritation among urban
gardeners in Brookmere City. The condition, later termed Blight Fever, presented with mild fevers, weakness, and a faintly luminescent rash visible under UV light. Concern
escalated when local physicians noticed a pattern: nearly all affected gardeners worked in one of three community greenhouses that had recently switched to a new soil additive
called EcoBloom X, designed to improve soil nitrogen retention.
To determine whether EcoBloom X was linked to Blight Fever, Dr. Vale and her team identified all active gardeners in the three greenhouses and recorded whether they had
developed Blight Fever within three months of EcoBloom X’s introduction. They also measured each gardener’s level of exposure to the additive, categorizing them as high,
medium, or low exposure based on hours spent handling treated soil. A blood test was later developed to detect a biomarker called BF-α, suspected to be associated with the
illness.
Dr. Vale’s team followed gardeners, observing who developed Blight Fever over time while documenting their baseline exposures and environmental factors such as greenhouse
ventilation and humidity. Participants were contacted biweekly for symptom monitoring and lab testing. While this design provided valuable incidence data, the project required
significant logistical coordination and funding, and many participants dropped out midway, citing time constraints and fatigue.

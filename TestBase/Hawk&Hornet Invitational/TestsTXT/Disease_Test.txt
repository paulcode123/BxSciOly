Pseudoscience Study! In 1998, Andrew Wakefield published a study in The Lancet claiming a link between the MMR vaccine and 
autism. It described a small group of patients (12 children) who shared certain clinical features– gastrointestinal symptoms and 
developmental regression (autism-like symptoms)- after receiving the MMR vaccine. 
 
The study was later found to be fraudulent; Wakefield had undisclosed conflicts of interest and manipulated data. Multiple 
large-scale studies found no link between vaccines and autism. In 2010, The Lancet fully retracted the paper, and Wakefield lost his 
medical license. Despite this, the claim sparked a persistent conspiracy theory that fueled vaccine hesitancy, particularly in the U.S. 
and U.K., and continues to impact public health efforts today. 
 
Multiple lines of evidence demonstrate that vaccination, including the MMR vaccine, is not causally linked to autism. The onset of 
autism symptoms is not related to the timing of vaccination, and the incidence of autism does not increase with the number of 
vaccines received. Follow-up studies show no significant rise in blood mercury levels (something believed to cause 
neurodevelopmental issues) after vaccination. Autism also occurs in unvaccinated children, and not every child who receives the 
MMR vaccine develops autism.  
 
Scenario above is adapted from Effiong (2016) with supplemental data from Rao & Andrade (2011) doi: 10.4103/0019-5545.82529.

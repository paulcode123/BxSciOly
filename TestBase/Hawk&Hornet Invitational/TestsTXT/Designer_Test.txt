Blue-white screening is a molecular biology technique used to identify recombinant bacterial colonies. In E. coli, the lac operon plays 
a central role in this process. When a gene of interest is inserted into the lacZ gene of a plasmid, it disrupts the production of 
Œ≤-galactosidase, an enzyme that normally cleaves X-gal, a substrate. E. coli cells are plated on agar containing X-gal and IPTG (an 
inducer of the lac operon). 
‚óè‚Äã
Blue colonies: These arise from bacteria where the lacZ gene is intact. The Œ≤-galactosidase enzyme is functional, cleaving 
X-gal and producing a blue color. 
‚óè‚Äã
White colonies: These result from bacteria where the lacZ gene is disrupted due to the insertion of the foreign gene. Without 
functional Œ≤-galactosidase, no blue color is produced. 
Thus, white colonies indicate successful cloning (gene insertion), while blue colonies suggest unsuccessful cloning. This makes 
blue-white screening a simple and effective method to identify recombinant E. coli strains.

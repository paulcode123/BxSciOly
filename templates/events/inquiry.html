Welcome to Bronx Science Inquiry

Inquiry is the division that separates the top-tier schools from the rest of the field. Here, we don't study a singular scientific domain; instead, we study the scientific method itself. We learn how to think, analyze, and communicate with flawless, mathematical precision.

Whether you're breaking advanced matrix ciphers, designing an entire scientific experiment from a random box of household materials in under 20 minutes, or writing step-by-step spatial blueprints that allow your partner to construct a complex 3D model sight-unseen, you are building the ultimate analytical cognitive habits. These skills map directly to university research methods, discrete mathematics, and computer science paths.

Subject Lead Expectations

  • Administer timed full-length practice exams and mock experimental design trials.
  • Safely manage, categorize, and transport experimental materials.
  • Train members in discrete mathematical calculations and cryptanalysis patterns.
  • Maintain comprehensive grading rubrics and provide constructive feedback loops.

Codebusters

Team of 3

A high-velocity cryptanalysis race. Apply modular arithmetic, frequency analysis, and pattern recognition to decrypt complex monoalphabetic and polyalphabetic ciphers under a tight timer.

What You'll Learn

  • To crack substitution ciphers using letter frequency profiles.
  • How modular arithmetic underlies classical cryptosystems.
  • How to compute matrix determinants and modular inverses.
  • To divide labor across a 3-person sprint team for peak speed.

Big Questions

How can you utilize the Kasiski examination to mathematically determine the key length of a Vigenère cipher?
How does modular multiplicative inverse recovery ($a^{-1} \pmod m$) restrict your key choices in Affine ciphers?
Monoalphabetic Substitution

Frequency & Pattern Analysis

  • Aristocrats & Patristocrats: Decrypting 1-to-1 letter substitutions (with or without spacing/punctuation) using frequency graphs, double-letter guides, and common word patterns (cribs).
  • Xenocrypts: Cracking Spanish monoalphabetic substitutions using Spanish letter frequencies (e.g., high-frequency 'E', 'A', 'O', 'S') and accent configurations.
  • Baconian & Atbash: Structural decoding of bilateral binary-style Bacon ciphers and simple mirror-alphabet Atbash.
Polyalphabetic & Mathematical

Advanced Modular Ciphers

  • Modular Arithmetic Ciphers: Solving Caesar, Affine ($E(x) = (ax + b) \pmod{26}$), and Hill Ciphers (utilizing $2 \times 2$ and $3 \times 3$ matrix operations, determinants, and cofactors).
  • Polyalphabetic Ciphers: Decrypting Vigenère ciphers using key placement grids and tabular structures.
  • Transposition Ciphers: Navigating Rail Fence, Pollux/Morbit Morse conversions, and RSA Public Key fundamentals (prime selection, totient, modular exponentiation).

Experimental Design

Team of 3

Given a box of random materials and a prompt, design, conduct, statistically analyze, and write up a complete, rigorous scientific experiment in 50 minutes.

What You'll Learn

  • To structure hypotheses around independent & dependent variables.
  • To procedurize step-by-step experimental instructions.
  • To apply basic biostatistics (means, standard deviations, range, trends).
  • To evaluate experimental limitations, biases, and future extensions.

Big Questions

How can you distinguish random experimental variability from a statistically significant treatment trend with minimal trial repetitions?
How do confounding variables introduce systemic bias into your design, and how can they be structurally mitigated?
Formulation & Execution

Designing the Test

  • Hypothesis Logic: Structuring testable "If... then... because..." statements linking a singular independent variable to a measurable dependent variable.
  • Procedural Detail: Drafting highly detailed, repeatable sequential instructions, defining control groups, and incorporating safety/ethical protocols.
  • Data Bookkeeping: Designing multi-trial data tables, tracking correct units, calculating averages, and graphing boundaries.
Statistical Analysis

Data Evaluation

  • Calculations: Calculating sample means, ranges, percentages, and standard deviations to assess data spread and reliability.
  • Graphing Standards: Choosing correct axes, scaling intervals, plotting data points, and adding linear or non-linear lines of best fit.
  • Error & Reflection: Distinguishing random human errors from systemic experimental limits, and proposing concrete future modifications.

Write It Do It

Team of 2 (Writer/Doer)

The ultimate test of clear communication. One partner writes detailed text instructions of a complex, hidden structure; the other partner builds it using only those written words.

What You'll Learn

  • To establish unambiguous absolute and relative spatial coordinates.
  • To construct step-by-step checklists under tight time restrictions.
  • How to interpret written blueprints literally without making assumptions.
  • To develop unified abbreviation codes and terminology standards.

Big Questions

How can you write instructions that prevent your partner from falling into three-dimensional mirror symmetry traps?
What is the mathematical threshold where adding descriptive adjectives starts to slow down your partner's construction speed?
The Writer's Strategy

Blueprinting & Terminology

  • Spatial Reference Frames: Defining a static origin point and using clear, absolute coordinate directions (e.g., "positive z-axis", "pointing towards you") instead of ambiguous relative terms.
  • Standardized Taxonomy: Cataloging complex build components (Lego, K'Nex, craft stems) using standardized terminology (dimensions, color, texture).
  • Step Sequencing: Structuring instructions so foundational components are anchored before complex sub-assemblies are layered on.
The Doer's Strategy

Literal Construction

  • Literal Interpretation: Suppressing the urge to assume what the model "should" look like, and strictly following the literal written words.
  • Verification Checks: Constantly verifying spatial angles, layer orders, and connection points against preceding instruction steps.
  • Time Management: Budgeting building speed to ensure complex final steps aren't rushed due to initial hesitation.

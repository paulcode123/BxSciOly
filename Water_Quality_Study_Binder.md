# PART 1: FRESHWATER ECOLOGY (20-30% of test)

## 1.1 Water Cycle & Hydrology

### Key Concepts (Appears in ALL 5 tests)
- **5 Steps of Water Cycle:** Evaporation, Transpiration, Condensation, Precipitation, Infiltration/Runoff
- **Freshwater Distribution:** 3% of Earth's water is fresh; only ~1% is usable as liquid
- **Watersheds (Drainage Basins):** All land draining to a single body of water

### Urbanization Effects on Watersheds
WITHOUT mitigating practices, urbanization causes:
- ✅ Increased bankfull discharge
- ✅ Increased flood volumes  
- ✅ **DECREASED** time to peak flow (water moves faster through developed areas)
- ✅ Increased flow velocity
- ✅ **DECREASED** max particle size (erosion is more uniform, less bedload sorting)
- ✅ Increased water temperature

### Bankfull Discharge
- Frequency: Once every **1.5 years** on average
- Definition: Peak flow event that defines natural stream shape

### Watershed Management Strategies ✅
- Stormwater detention basins with outlet control
- Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plans (SPPPs)
- Master Plans incorporating land use & soil data
- ❌ National Flood Insurance Program (NOT a management strategy—it's insurance)

## 1.2 Water Chemistry & Nutrient Cycling

### Nitrogen Cycle - HIGHLY TESTED
**Key Terms & Definitions:**

| Process | Definition | Key Point |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Nitrogen Fixation** | N₂ → NH₃ (free nitrogen to ammonia) | Mostly by cyanobacteria & Rhizobium |
| **Nitrification** | NH₃ → NO₃⁻ (biological oxidation to nitrate) | Done by Nitrosomonas & Nitrobacter |
| **Denitrification** | NO₃⁻ → N₂ (reduction under anaerobic conditions) | Reverses nitrogen fixation |
| **Ammonification** | Organic N → NH₃ (organic nitrogen released) | Decomposition process |

### Phosphorus Cycle
- **Most limiting nutrient** in freshwater systems (Liebig's Law)
- **Redfield-Richards Ratio for freshwater:** C:N:P = **106:16:1** (sometimes simplified to 100:10:1)
- **Primary sources:** Terrestrial runoff, erosion of phosphate rocks (NOT atmospheric or hydrothermal)
- **Conversion:** PO₄ → P by **dividing by 5**

### Limiting Factors & Liebig's Law
- **Liebig's Law of the Minimum:** Growth limited by scarcest resource, NOT total resources available
- **Phosphorus** is typically the scarcest element and primary limiting factor in freshwater

### Eutrophication - APPEARS IN ALL 5 TESTS
**Process:**
1. Excess nutrients (N & P) → algal bloom
2. Algae die → decomposition increases
3. Decomposers consume dissolved oxygen
4. **Hypoxia/anoxia** → dead zones
5. Only pollution-tolerant species survive

**Caused by:** Agricultural runoff, sewage, industrial discharges
**Term for human-caused eutrophication:** Artificial/Cultural eutrophication

## 1.3 Benthic Macroinvertebrates

### Why They're Reliable Bioindicators (EPA criteria)
✅ Spend all/most of lives in water  
✅ Easy to collect  
✅ Respond predictably to human disturbance  
✅ Easy to identify in lab  
✅ Live >1 year (integrates water quality over time)  
✅ Limited mobility (represent specific habitat)  
❌ NOT highly tolerant to pollution (sensitive ones are good indicators!)

### Pollution Tolerance Classification

#### **Class 1 - Pollution Sensitive** (EPT organisms - MOST IMPORTANT)
- **Mayfly** (Ephemeroptera) - adult doesn't eat
- **Stonefly** (Plecoptera) - eats primarily aquatic organisms
- **Caddisfly** (Trichoptera) - case-building larvae
- Aquatic Sowbug
- Water Mite
- Riffle Beetle

#### **Class 2 - Moderately Sensitive**
- Damselfly - shoots out hinged lower lip (labium/mask)
- Dragonfly - immature aquatic predator
- Midge larvae
- Scuds (freshwater)
- Crane Fly

#### **Class 3 - Moderately Tolerant**
- Water Strider - uses **surface tension** to walk on water
- Whirligig Beetle
- Back Swimmer - carries air bubble
- Water Boatman - carries air bubble; Hemiptera order
- Gilled Snails

#### **Class 4 - Pollution Tolerant**
- **Tubifex worms** - can survive anoxic conditions
- **Blood Midge** - red hemoglobin for low O₂ environments
- Leeches - annelids; segmented bodies; can be parasitic or feed on small organisms
- Flatworms - can reproduce sexually or asexually
- Mosquito larvae
- Aquatic Nuisance Organisms

#### **Class 5 - Air Breathing**
- Air Breathing Snail
- Water Scorpion

### EPT Index
- **E = Ephemeroptera** (Mayflies)
- **P = Plecoptera** (Stoneflies)  
- **T = Trichoptera** (Caddisflies)
- **High EPT Index** = High proportion of pollution-SENSITIVE organisms = **GOOD water quality**

### Specific Organism Facts (Common Quiz Topics)
| Organism | Key Fact |
|----------|----------|
| **Mayfly adults** | Eat NOTHING (no functional mouth) |
| **Damselfly larva** | Predator; shoots out labium (mask) to capture prey |
| **Dobsonfly** | Easily identified by long mandibles; larva (hellgrammite) |
| **Water Striders** | Use surface tension; Hemiptera order |
| **Water Boatman** | Hemiptera order; carries air bubble to breathe |
| **Leeches** | Annelids; segmented; feed on blood/small organisms |
| **Dragonfly + Damselfly** | Both aquatic predators in larval stages; dragonfly is stronger flier |

### Metamorphosis Types
- **Complete Metamorphosis:** Egg → Larva → Pupa → Adult (mosquito, midges, caddisfly)
  - Adult form completely different from larva
  - Adults may not feed at all
- **Incomplete Metamorphosis:** Egg → Nymph → Adult (stonefly, mayfly, dragonfly)
  - Nymph resembles adult but lacks wings/sexual maturity

### Aquatic Nuisance Species - IMPORTANT

#### **Nuisance Plants** (3 main ones)
1. **Purple Loosestrife** - invasive, outcompetes natives, spreads rapidly
2. **Eurasian Water Milfoil** - dense underwater growth, chokes waterways
3. **Water Hyacinth** - floats on surface, rapid growth

#### **Nuisance Animals**
1. **Zebra Mussel** - First observed Lake Erie 1986
   - Effects: Increased water clarity (more photosynthesis) BUT decreased zooplankton/phytoplankton
   - Clogs pipes, outcompetes native mussels
2. **Spiny Water Flea** - predator on zooplankton
3. **Asian Carp** - filter feeders, outcompete natives for plankton
4. **Asian Tiger Mosquito** - disease vector
5. **Crayfish/Crawdads** - omnivorous, compete with native fauna

# PART 2: WATER MONITORING & CHEMISTRY (30-40% of test)

## 2.1 Water Quality Parameters

### pH - HIGHLY TESTED
| Parameter | Ideal Freshwater Range | Problem Range |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| **pH** | **6.5 - 8.5** | <5 (acidic), >9 (basic) |
| **pH 4.5** | ⚠️ ACIDIC - harmful to aquatic life | Too low |
| **Rainwater pH** | ~5.9 (naturally acidic due to CO₂) | Normal |

**Why rainwater is acidic:**
- CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)
- Equation: CO₂(g) + H₂O → H₂CO₃ ↔ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

**pH Calculation:**
- pH = -log[H⁺]
- Example: [H⁺] = 1×10⁻⁷ M → pH = 7

### Dissolved Oxygen (DO) - CRITICAL FOR SURVIVAL
| Factor | Effect on DO |
|--------|--------------|
| Temperature ↑ | DO ↓ (less soluble in warm water) |
| Salinity ↑ | DO ↓ (salt reduces gas solubility) |
| Altitude ↑ | DO ↑ (lower atmospheric pressure = lower solubility) |
| Daytime vs Night | **Daytime > Nighttime** (photosynthesis adds O₂) |
| Depth ↓ | DO ↑ (deeper = colder = more dissolved) |

**At 70°F:** Water holds ~8.9 mg/L DO (saturation)

**Minimum DO Requirements by Species:**
- Trout: 5 mg/L (sensitive)
- Carp: 2 mg/L (tolerant)

**DO Measurement:** Often measured in **percent saturation** (% of max possible)

### Salinity
- **Freshwater:** 10-50 parts per million (ppm)
- **Parts per million conversion:** 1 ppm = 0.0001% (0.01%)
- Lake Michigan salinity: 10-50 ppm ✅

### Turbidity
- **Definition:** Measure of water cloudiness (NOT pH or salinity)
- **Measurement Methods:**
  - Spectrophotometer (light absorption)
  - Secchi disk (depth of light penetration)
- **Units:** NTU (Nephelometric Turbidity Units) or FNU (Formazin Nephelometric Units)
- **Drinking water limit:** 0.5 NTU (very clear)
- **Causes:** Suspended sediment, algae, organic matter
- **Effects:** ↓ Photosynthesis, ↓ visibility, habitat degradation

### Nitrates & Phosphates
- **Nitrates:** Indicator of pollution (sewage, agricultural runoff)
- **Phosphate conversion:** PO₄ divided by 5 = elemental P
  - Example: 5 ppm PO₄ = 1 ppm P
- **Ideal phosphorus:** <0.5 ppm (prevents eutrophication)
- **Readily bioavailable form:** Orthophosphates (not polyphosphates)

### BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand) - COMPLEX BUT IMPORTANT
**Two Stages:**
1. **Carbonaceous BOD** - oxidation of carbon compounds
2. **Nitrification BOD** - oxidation of ammonia to nitrate

**Typical BOD Tests:**
- **BOD₅** - most common; 5-day test at 20°C
- **Ultimate BOD** - total oxygen demand after full decomposition

**Inverse Relationship with DO:**
- High BOD → microbes consume O₂ → DO decreases
- Dead zones form when DO <2 mg/L

**BOD Calculation Example:**
If 3-day BOD = 400 mg/L with k₂₀ = 0.1/day:
- BOD₅ calculation requires exponential decay formula
- Ultimate BOD uses full decomposition calculation

**What it indicates:** Amount of organic matter (decomposable pollution)

### Fecal Coliform - Health Indicator
- Indicator of human/animal waste contamination
- Diseases linked: Cholera, Salmonella, Dysentery, Typhoid, Hepatitis
- **Safe limit in potable water:** 0 colonies/100mL
- **Testing method:** Culture on selective media, count colonies

### Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) vs Total Suspended Solids (TSS)
| Parameter | Definition | Measurement | Impact |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **TDS** | Dissolved ions/minerals | Conductivity or gravimetric | Salinity indicator |
| **TSS** | Suspended particles | Gravimetric only | Turbidity; conventional pollutant |

### Temperature Effects on Water Chemistry
- ↑ Temperature → ↓ DO solubility
- ↑ Temperature → NO effect on total solids (dry weight)
- ↑ Temperature → NO effect on phosphate concentrations (chemical property)

## 2.2 Water Treatment Processes

### Potable Water Treatment - CRITICAL TEST TOPIC

**Correct Order (ALL 5 TESTS include this):**
1. **Screening** - removes large debris
2. **Coagulation** - add chemicals to neutralize charge of suspended particles
3. **Flocculation** - add chemicals to clump particles together
4. **Sedimentation** - gravity separation of settled solids
5. **Filtration** - sand/charcoal filters remove fine particles
6. **Disinfection** - kill remaining pathogens (chlorine, UV, ozone)
7. **Distribution** - deliver to homes

### Coagulation vs Flocculation
| Process | What | Chemicals | Result |
|---------|------|-----------|--------|
| **Coagulation** | Neutralize charge | Alum, ferric chloride | Particles destabilize |
| **Flocculation** | Combine particles | Lime, alum-based | Forms "floc" (clumps) |

### Common Coagulants ✅
- Aluminum sulfate (alum) ✅
- Ferric chloride ✅
- Ferrous sulfate ✅
- Ferric sulfate ✅
- Sodium aluminate ✅
- Polyaluminum chloride ✅
- ❌ Sodium sulfate (NOT a coagulant)

**Cations enhancing flocculation:** Ca²⁺, Al³⁺, Fe³⁺, Mg²⁺ (NOT Cu²⁺ or Na⁺)

### Activated Sludge Treatment
- **Definition:** Active microorganism sludge used in secondary treatment
- **Return Activated Sludge (RAS):** Fraction returned to aeration tank to maintain microbial populations

### Wastewater Treatment - 3-Stage Process
1. **Primary Treatment**
   - Bar screening - removes large solids
   - Sedimentation - gravity settling
   - Goal: Remove ~30% of BOD

2. **Secondary Treatment** (REQUIRED by law in US)
   - Aeration tank - aerobic bacteria decompose organics
   - Clarifier - settles microbes
   - Goal: Remove ~95% of BOD
   - Activated sludge process most common

3. **Tertiary/Advanced Treatment** (optional)
   - Nutrient removal
   - Advanced oxidation
   - Polishing for discharge standards

**ALWAYS last step:** DISINFECTION (chlorine/UV/ozone)

**Separated component:** Bar screen (mechanical, separate from main treatment)

### Greywater Classification ✅
- Showers/bathtubs ✅
- Sinks ✅
- Washing machines ✅
- ❌ Toilet water (blackwater, not greywater)
- ❌ Drinking water (potable, not wastewater)

### Turbidity Standards
**Drinking water max:** 0.5 NTU
**Measurement standard at 90° scattering:** ISO 7027-1

## 2.3 Specialized Testing Methods

### Winkler Method
- Uses: **Redox reactions** (not titration alone)
- Measures: **Dissolved Oxygen** (not BOD)
- Common in field water quality testing

### Phosphate Testing - Molybdenum Blue Method
1. Add ammonium molybdate to water sample with phosphate
2. Forms molybdenum blue complex (blue color)
3. Spectrophotometer measures absorbance
4. Compare to standard curve to determine phosphate concentration

### BOD Testing Calculations
**Standard equation:**
```
BOD = [(DO_blank_initial - DO_blank_final) - (DO_sample_initial - DO_sample_final)] / (mL_sample/300)
```

### Titration Method Example
**Chloride titration with AgNO₃:**
- Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl (white precipitate)
- Calculate: moles Ag = moles Cl
- Convert to mg/L using molecular weight

# PART 3: AQUATIC ECOLOGY & SYSTEMS (20-30% of test)

## 3.1 Lentic vs Lotic Systems

| Feature | Lentic (Lake/Pond) | Lotic (River/Stream) |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Flow** | Still water | Fast-moving |
| **Zones** | Pelagic, profundal | Source, transition, floodplain |
| **Producers** | Mainly phytoplankton | Most in transition zone (moderate current) |
| **DO** | Varies by depth | Highest in source zone (cold, turbulent) |

### Lentic Zone Definitions
- **Pelagic Zone** - open water with light penetration
- **Profundal Zone** - deep water where light cannot penetrate
- Difference: Lakes have profundal zones; ponds do NOT

### Lotic Zone Producer Distribution
- **Source Zone** - fast, cold, turbulent - FEW producers
- **Transition Zone** - moderate flow - MOST producers (algae, plants can establish)
- **Floodplain Zone** - slow, sediment-rich - many producers

### Lentic System Stratification
- **Thermocline** - layer of rapid temperature change
- **Thermal stratification** - summer: warm epilimnion → cold hypolimnion
- **Lake turnover** (spring/fall): Temperature inversion causes mixing
  - Redistributes oxygen and nutrients
  - Can release anaerobic gases (methane, H₂S)

### Plankton Definitions
| Organism | Characteristic |
|----------|----------------|
| **Phytoplankton** | Autotrophs; main primary producers |
| **Zooplankton** | Heterotrophs; eat phytoplankton |
| **Nekton** | Strong swimmers (fish, large aquatic animals) |
| **Benthos** | Bottom dwellers |

---

## 3.2 Aquatic Food Webs & Energy Transfer

### Trophic Levels
- **Energy transfer:** ~10% per level (90% lost as heat)
- Organisms CAN occupy multiple trophic levels simultaneously
- **Example:** 100 kJ algae → 10 kJ mysis → 1 kJ finescale dace → 100 J lake trout

### Bioaccumulation vs Biomagnification
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Bioaccumulation** | Individual organism accumulates pollutants over lifetime |
| **Biomagnification** | Concentration increases UP the food chain |
| **Result** | Top predators have highest concentrations |

### Bioindicators - Organism Sensitivity
| Organism Type | Indicates |
|---------------|-----------|
| **Mayfly, Caddisfly, Stonefly (EPT)** | Good oxygen levels, LOW pollution |
| **Amphibians (permeable skin)** | Water pollution |
| **Microalgae** | Nutrient levels (eutrophication) |
| **Tubifex, Blood Midge** | Poor oxygen, HIGH pollution |

---

## 3.3 Water Chemistry & Acidification

### Acid Rain & pH Buffering
- **Normal rainwater pH:** 5.9 (due to CO₂)
- **Acid rain pH:** <5.5 (from SO₂, NOₓ)

### Bedrock Buffering Capacity
| Bedrock Type | Buffer Capacity | Vulnerability |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Granite** | ❌ LOW (no carbonates) | VULNERABLE to acidification |
| **Limestone** | ✅ HIGH (CaCO₃ buffer) | RESISTANT |

**Chemistry:** CaCO₃ + H⁺ → HCO₃⁻ + Ca²⁺ (neutralizes acid)

### Riparian Buffers
- **Definition:** Vegetation strips along streams
- **Functions:**
  - Stabilize banks (prevent erosion)
  - Filter runoff (reduce sediment/nutrients)
  - Shade water (keep temperature down)
  - Provide habitat

## 3.4 Ecosystem Properties

### Resilience vs Resistance
- **Inertia/Resistance:** Ability to resist disturbance (stay same)
  - Coral reefs: HIGH inertia (stable systems)
- **Resilience:** Ability to recover after disturbance
  - Coral reefs: LOW resilience (slow recovery)

### Succession Types
- **Primary succession:** Bare rock → pioneer species → climax community
- **Secondary succession:** After disturbance to existing ecosystem

### Pioneer Species
- Early colonizers on bare ground
- Often acidify soil to increase nutrient availability (weathering)
- Short-lived, high reproductive output

### Wetlands
- **Defining characteristic:** Soil saturated with water (part of year)
- **Benefits to humans:** Flood control, water filtration, habitat, carbon storage

### Lake Formation Processes ✅
- Volcanic activity ✅
- Glaciation ✅
- Crustal displacement ✅
- ❌ Diagenesis (rock transformation - does NOT form lakes)

# PART 4: ADVANCED TOPICS (Higher Points, Less Common)

## 4.1 Population Ecology

### Predator-Prey Models
- **Red Queen Hypothesis:** Predators and prey co-evolving
- **Lotka-Volterra Model:** Population cycles where prey lags behind predator

### Paradox of the Plankton
Why can many species coexist in homogeneous environments?
- Kill-the-winner hypothesis (viral lysis)
- Metabolic differentiation
- Fluctuating environmental conditions
- Differential predation

### Survivorship Curves
- **Type I:** High initial survival, rapid decline in old age (humans)
- **Type II:** Constant mortality rate (birds, many insects)
- **Type III:** High early mortality, then stable (fish, trees)

### Shannon-Wiener Index
Measure of species diversity (calculation with formula provided)

---

## 4.2 Evolutionary Ecology

### Selective Pressure Types
- **Directional selection:** One phenotype advantaged
- **Stabilizing selection:** Middle phenotype advantaged (disadvanages extremes)
- **Disruptive selection:** Both extremes advantaged, middle disadvantaged

### Example: Oyster shells
- Sandy areas: Light shells favored (camouflage)
- Dark areas: Dark shells favored
- Both colors selected for = **DISRUPTIVE selection**
- Neither particularly light nor dark = **STABILIZING selection pressure**

---

## 4.3 Climate Change & Aquatic Systems

### Ocean Acidification
**Mechanism:**
1. ↑ Atmospheric CO₂
2. CO₂(g) dissolves → H₂CO₃
3. ↓ pH (ocean becomes acidic)
4. ↓ Saturation state of aragonite/calcite
5. Calcifying organisms struggle with shell formation

**Affected organisms:**
- Corals (aragonite users - most affected)
- Mollusks
- Pteropods
- Echinoderms

### Coral Reef Extinction Process
1. ↑ CO₂ → ocean acidification
2. ↓ pH and carbonate saturation
3. Coral bleaching (symbiotic algae expelled)
4. Reduced calcification
5. Erosion > growth
6. Ecosystem collapse

### Hydropower Effects on Freshwater
1. Thermal stratification in reservoir
2. Fluctuating water flow (↓ DO in surface, ↑ turbidity)
3. Habitat fragmentation (fish migration blocked)
4. Methane & CO₂ from anaerobic decomposition in reservoir
5. Biofuels require land → deforestation → runoff ↑ → eutrophication
